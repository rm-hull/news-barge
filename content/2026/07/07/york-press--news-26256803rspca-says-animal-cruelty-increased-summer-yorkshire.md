## Studies show summer rise in animal abuse reports in North Yorkshire

A staffy left abandoned in Hull *(Image: RSPCA)*

REPORTS of animal cruelty in the summer months are on the rise, according to a leading animal charity.

The RSPCA says it has seen a spike in reports during the summer months, coinciding with its busiest period of the year, with reports suggesting that abuse in North Yorkshire rose by 6 per cent last summer.

According to figures published today (July 7), cruelty reports during the summer in North Yorkshire increased from 86 to 91 cases and in East Yorkshire, there was 72 cases - slightly down from 73 the year before.

Between June and August last year, the RSPCA received 6,322 cruelty calls nationally - one call every 10 minutes when the line is open - which up from 3,852 during the same period in 2021 and a 64 per cent increase over five years.

One of those cases involved the discovery of a dead dog dumped in a suitcase along a rural lane in Guisborough - close to Middlesborough and Whitby in the north of the county.

The young female Rottweiler-German Shepherd cross was found in undergrowth on Wilton Lane by a member of the public who was out litter picking.

Due to the suspicious circumstances, an RSPCA officer was sent to the scene to recover the dog who was described as extremely thin.

The level of decomposition prevented investigators from determining a cause of death.

In East Yorkshire, one of the reports included an emaciated puppy found dead in Hull; she was severely underweight, had an untreated mass, and was bleeding from her right ear.

  A suitcase containing the remains of a dog in North Yorkshire

A member of the public discovered the white female Staffy, believed to be around nine months old, at Brackley Park near the Hornsea rail line and contacted the charity.

Despite public appeals, the individual responsible for abandoning her has not been identified.

In response to cases like these, RSPCA’s Cruelty Hurts Love Rescues campaign has been launched; it highlights the charity’s work rescuing and rehabilitating animals and calls for public support through donations.

Jo Hirst, RSPCA superintendent, said: "It’s incredibly alarming that we are seeing year after year of rising reports of cruelty.

"The summer months are our busiest times of year and our frontline rescue teams, specialist vets, behaviourists, rehabilitation and rehoming teams will be working tirelessly to help as many animals as possible.

"Today we have launched our Cruelty Hurts, Love Rescues campaign to highlight this shocking trend and the work we are doing to save so many animals from horrific abuse and suffering."
