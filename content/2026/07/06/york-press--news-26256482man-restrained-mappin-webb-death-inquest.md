A man died after being restrained for around seven minutes at a York jewellery store, an inquest has heard.

Sam Diatta threw punches at the door supervisor of Mappin and Webb, then in Coney Street, before he was restrained by the supervisor on July 26, 2022, CCTV footage shown in court revealed.

One man who was in the jewellery store with his family helped the supervisor restrain Mr Diatta, 39, with two passers-by also stopping to help hold Mr Diatta on the ground.

A police officer arrived at around 4.45pm and rolled Mr Diatta onto his back then started administering CPR when it became apparent that he was not breathing, the inquest heard.

More police and paramedics arrived and Mr Diatta was later taken to York Hospital. He was pronounced dead in hospital shortly after 6.40pm.

The hearing was told Mr Diatta had been diagnosed with bipolar disorder and was on medication for heart disease.

His cause of death was given as hypertensive heart disease and the effects of being restrained.

The Mappin and Webb door supervisor, Gary Prothero, told the court that Mr Diatta entered the jewellery store and asked to take photographs of three watches for his social media pages, telling staff he had 18,000 followers online.

Staff told Mr Diatta, of Suffolk House in Lowther Terrace off Holgate Road in York, he would have to arrange this with head office, the inquest heard.

Mr Prothero said Mr Diatta was “slightly aggressive” and had asked for the watches in a “very loud” voice.

The door supervisor alleged that Mr Diatta, who was half Senegalese, accused him of being racist after staff declined to give him the watches to photograph, an allegation Mr Prothero denied.

Mr Prothero said he opened the shop’s front door and asked Mr Diatta to “please leave”.

Mr Prothero told the hearing that Mr Diatta then attempted to grab the Covid mask the door supervisor was wearing, describing it as a “volatile” situation.

Mr Prothero said he attempted to grab Mr Diatta’s arm and guide him out of the shop.

CCTV footage showed Mr Diatta struggle backwards while Mr Prothero held his arm before Mr Diatta threw punches at the door supervisor.

Mr Prothero said he was left with bruises and cuts to his face.

He described Mr Diatta’s behaviour as “bizarre”, adding that “to this day I still don’t know” why Mr Diatta acted the way he did.

Mr Prothero said police had been called and he wanted to restrain Mr Diatta until officers arrived to protect the store’s staff and customers from any potential risk.

He described Mr Diatta as “incredibly strong” and told the court he could not recall Mr Diatta saying he could not breathe while being restrained by the four men.

## 'Chaotic situation' as man restrained in store, says door supervisor

CCTV footage showed Mr Diatta lying face down with three of the men restraining him by putting pressure on his back, with the fourth man holding Mr Diatta’s legs.

Mr Prothero said: “It was a chaotic situation and it was easier to get him [to the floor] on his front.”

Medical professionals told the court a post-mortem examination revealed that Mr Diatta had an enlarged heart with an irregular beat, which was exacerbated by being in stressful situations.

Pathologist Peter Nigel Cooper told the court that lying face down for someone with the medical conditions Mr Diatta had “isn’t very good, especially if you’ve got a big tummy … What is really bad is having weight on your chest.”

The hearing was told that Mr Diatta being restrained along with his medical issues was a “bad combination”.

Asked by the Diatta family’s lawyer, Parissa Najah, if Mr Diatta being restrained on his front would have contributed to his death, Dr Cooper replied: “The longer he’s in that position, the worse it is.”

After Mr Diatta’s death, North Yorkshire Police said four men were arrested in connection with the death and were interviewed and released while under investigation.

But in July 2023 the force said no one would be charged with any offence following Mr Diatta’s death.

Mr Diatta’s family described him as a “wonderful member of our family who will be greatly missed by us, his friends and family around the world”.

The family members, in a statement released by police after Mr Diatta’s death, said Mr Diatta developed mental health issues in his late teenage years “which stayed with him throughout his life”.

Their statement added that Mr Diatta was a “a gentle giant, who was kind and generous and loved by all who met him”.

The inquest, at Northallerton Coroners’ Court, continues.
