## 'Fashion, scooters, nicknames – I was one of York's Mods in the swinging 60s'

York's Outcrowd Mods in the 1960s and veteran York Mod John Fletcher, right

  *JOHN FLETCHER takes us inside York's Outcrowd Mod scene of the Swinging 60s*

I TURNED 16 in June 1967 and that meant freedom! In those wonderful carefree days you just bought a scooter (or a motorbike…ugh!) got a provisional licence, stuck on an L plate, bought a parka and off you went.

The open road, the wind in your hair (helmets not compulsory for another six years) and you were the coolest Mod in the city!

Except I wasn’t. A friend of mine at school was slightly older than me and he turned 16 first. He had a BSA Sunbeam scooter, blue, and it was his pride and joy.

Being a bit of a follower rather than a leader I was desperate to have the same thing, so when I saw an identical red one in Russell’s Bike Shop in Lawrence Street I just had to have it.

I was working by then, so it was duly bought and paid for and my dream start to being the coolest guy on the road had been realised.

Until that is, I came across York’s Outcrowd.

Today, I will take you on a journey back in time - giving the inside story of York's Outcrowd Mod scene of the Swinging 60s.

  Some of York's Outcrowd Mods from the 1960s. Press photo


Dave Smith was a year younger than me, but we were musicians and in a band together. He had a parka, and a Lambretta, and was part of a group of Mods who based themselves around the central square outside York’s Art Gallery. He suggested I come with him on my scooter to meet everyone and join the group.

So, one Saturday afternoon, I turned up on my lovely red Sunbeam – and everyone fell about laughing. “ Egg on wheels …” was one of the printable comments and I quickly realised that every other person there had Lambrettas or Vespas, either stripped down to basics or covered with mirrors, or customised with specialist paint jobs.

My lovely pride and joy couldn’t have been more uncool if it tried, and humiliated, I vowed to get a Lambretta as soon as possible.

I also needed a parka – easily solved by a trip to the Army & Navy stores at the top of Fossgate. I purchased a tatty, second-hand ex-Scandinavian army issue one which had a complete polecat around its hood. The dried head of the animal was still attached, and when you shook it something suspicious rattled inside, possibly its eyeballs.

Now halfway to becoming an accepted Mod I needed a cool scooter so I returned to Russell’s. Stung by the comments and determined to show everyone I was a true Mod, I traded in my Sunbeam for one of the first Lambretta LI 150 Specials to arrive in York.

This gave me instant cool and total acceptance into York’s Outcrowd. By the start of 1968 I had my parka, my Lambretta and my nickname.

Err yes…everyone had a nickname, it was part of the ethos of the group. No one used their own names, or very rarely, so one was created for me. There was a saying that whatever time you arrived in Exhibition Square, even in the middle of the night, a member of the Outcrowd would be there, and as I used to stay late myself, napping on the bike, I became Dormouse, or Fleggs. I joined the ranks of Miff, Dodge, Python, Dylan, Fuzz, Woolly, Twirly, Catty, Big Steve and Little Steve, and gradually the group swelled as more young Mods attached themselves.

Like any group, York’s Outcrowd boasted its fair share of cool ladies, too young to be WAGS but certainly girlfriends. Fabulous fashions, leather coats, mini skirts and boots, posing on their boyfriend’s scooters they attracted envious glances from other passing females who secretly wished they could be as trendy and part of the cool group.

Folklore has it that the group was founded by Ron Wright, Alan Smith, Roger Turner, Marilyn Hayden and Carole Kirkwood. Sitting on their scooters in Exhibition Square they were passed by a group of other Mods going to a party who taunted: “We’re the in crowd". “Well, we’re the Outcrowd…” was shouted back and the name stuck.

  1968: Exhibition Square,York - this was a meeting point for York's Outcrowd Mods in the late 60s


Dave Smith (Miff) has many Outcrowd memories. “The earliest one which had an impact was when we went to a house in Pocklington to someone’s party and stayed for a week or so! Seemed like another world and so far away from York! I remember being woken up there one night by someone scrabbling at the window, somehow they’d got on to the roof and couldn’t get back down, I think Woolly was involved but can’t be sure!

"Also think he and Twirly were responsible for the fried-egg floating challenge in the garden pond! Another memory was a ride out to the east coast as usual but it was decided we would call in at Primrose Valley.

"On arrival we were greeted by a jobsworth who let us know in no uncertain terms we were not welcome and there was nothing there for the likes of us!

"Finally there was the ceremonial dressing of William Etty’s statue outside the art gallery in Exhibition Square and somehow it was decided to make the statue a member of the Outcrowd, so he was duly dressed in a parka and scarf.

"Passers-by not too impressed by a few parka-clad youths scrambling up the statue and dressing it up! There are plenty more memories but it is difficult to remember who did what as we started out with just a few of us meeting in Exhibition Square just for something to do and it grew to quite a big group.”

Graham Hayden (aka Dylan) also remembers the Primrose Valley incident.

  York Mod John Fletcher


“We got kicked off Primrose Valley and Danes Dyke and ended up on Flamborough Head or Filey Brig. We woke up to a sea fret and we were stuck there until midday. Nobody dared move it was so thick.

"After an article in the Yorkshire Evening Press bemoaning the fact we weren't welcome anywhere we went, a kind vicar offered us the use of his church hall behind the BeesWing on Hull Road.

"For one reason or another that didn't last so it was back to Exhibition Square. I remember it being exciting times in the Square, somebody would come in with news of a party somewhere, so invite or not we would all pile out there.

"Looking back I can understand how the parents must have felt of whoever the party was for, coming home to find 20 or so scooters parked on their front lawn!"

And at the end of the day, York Outcrowd were iconic. The camaraderie and friendship has lasted till the present day, there have been two reunions, and ex-members still meet to share stories and anecdotes.

At a time when Mods and rockers had become entrenched in the public consciousness and the national papers were awash with this new youth trend, a good-natured group of scooter enthusiasts were putting York on the map.

They were the first – riding the wave of Mod culture as it actually happened in the late sixties, shocking the establishment with their hairstyles, their scooters and their music just as teddy boys had done in the previous decade.

Being a Mod when it was rebellious and exciting to be one – we were part of York’s social history which should never be forgotten.

The Outcrowd were role models of later scooter groups that emulated them, such as York Inset Scooter Group.

  Mods in York today: The 2026 York Inset Scooter Club Mods and Rockers ride out. Image: Kevin Glenton


They were part of the 1960s scooter scene alongside the York & District Lambretta Club, with both groups largely disbanding by 1973. The York Scooter Club was subsequently formed in 1974.

Being a Mod in 1960s' York, having pride in their scooter ownership, and displaying fierce loyalty to one another in any sort of adversity, made them unique.

I for one was proud to be member and to ride with them.
