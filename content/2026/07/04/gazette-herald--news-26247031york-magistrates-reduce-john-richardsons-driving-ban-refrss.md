A CAR criminal who broke motoring laws 16 times has had a driving ban of more than seven years slashed so he can pursue a career at sea.

John James Richardson, 37, told York magistrates his employers required ship’s masters to have driving licences and that they wouldn’t be able to employ him after September 30 if he couldn’t drive.

He was initially banned in 2020 for four years and 11 weeks for five offences of failure to blood or saliva samples when suspected of drink or drug driving, four each of driving whilst disqualified and without insurance, two of possessing cannabis, one of possessing diazepam without a prescription, one of making a false statement to obtain insurance and one of breaking coronavirus lockdown regulations by driving.

In April 2023, York magistrates banned him for four years, five months and 30 days after he pleaded guilty to driving whilst disqualified, driving without insurance, failure to provide a blood sample, assaulting a police officer by spitting on him and possessing cannabis.

The effect of the two bans was that he was banned continuously from August 2020 until February 2028.

The 2020 bench jailed him for 22 weeks and the 2023 bench jailed him for 18 weeks.

“I think it took going to prison for me to realise my life was spiralling out of control,” Richardson told York magistrates as he applied to have his 2023 ban reduced.

He said he was ashamed of his offences.

“I do accept responsibility for my actions,” he said. “I understand the seriousness of it. I was going through a difficult period of my life. I don’t know how to explain the lack of respect I had for the law.

“That was a period when I was struggling mentally. I have changed,” he said.

Richardson, of Promenade, Bridlington, said since leaving prison, he had got work at sea on short term contracts.

His employers were prepared to offer him a contract from September 1, but the ship involved had no overnight accommodation and the Scottish harbour it used did not have dockside accommodation.

Therefore the company needed the ship’s master to be able to drive so he could take the crew to their accommodation at a distance from the port. He handed in a letter from his employers.

Magistrates agreed to lift the driving ban from September 30. They told him he could apply to DVLA to have his driving licence back from that date but ultimately it was up to DVLA to decide whether it would allow him to drive again. They couldn’t order DVLA to give him his licence back.

Richardson had twice tried to get the 2020 ban reduced before the 2023 ban was imposed but on each occasion had failed to attend court.

In 2023, he had told York magistrates that as he was a freeman, he couldn’t be disqualified from driving.
