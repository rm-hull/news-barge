A MAN is set to walk nearly 100 miles through the Scottish Highlands with his guide dog who “gave him his life back”

  John Reeder, 45 from York, is taking on the West Highland Way from Milngavie to Fort William this September to raise funds for Guide Dogs.****

John lost his sight gradually over years due to Type 1 diabetes, which led to diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma, and macular oedema.

He said: "My sight loss built up gradually over time. I always had one 'good eye', so I found ways to cope – until suddenly I couldn’t anymore.

"It got to the point where everything became distorted. The way I’d describe it is it’s like looking through bubble wrap with ink splashed on it. Objects can seem like they’re multiplying and it’s really disorientating.”

A former hospitality worker and later a support worker for adults with learning disabilities, John was forced to stop working as his sight and health declined.

  John credits Max for 'giving him his life back' 

*(Image: Guide Dogs)*

During the pandemic, he shielded due to diabetes and kidney disease, leading to increased isolation.

He said: "I hadn’t really learned how to adapt to my sight loss yet. I was trying to figure things out on my own – even simple things like making a cup of tea with my deteriorating sight."

John found using a white cane limiting, so he reached out to Guide Dogs and was matched with Max four and a half years ago.

  Max the Guide Dog 

*(Image: Guide Dogs)*

He said: "When I got Max, it was like finding myself again. He brought me back out of my shell and gave me the confidence to start living again.

"At first we were learning each other’s ways, but then something clicked – it felt like we became one team."

With Max, he has regained independence and confidence, even meeting his girlfriend at a festival which he only attended thanks to his renewed sense of freedom.

  John and Max out for a walk 

*(Image: Guide Dogs)*

He said: "Even though Max wasn’t with me at the gig, he gave me the confidence to be there in the first place."

The 96-mile challenge has been in the works for more than a year and was partly inspired by Scott Cunningham MBE, who completed the route with his own guide dogs.

Mr Reeder said: "As my health conditions progress, I want to do something while I still can. I’ve always loved walking and the great outdoors. This felt like the right challenge to take on.

  Guide dog owner John with guide dog Max in Guide Dogs t shirt 

*(Image: Guide Dogs)*

"One of the main challenges for me on this route isn’t necessarily my sight loss, but my peripheral neuropathy, which will make this extremely tough.

"Other than Max, the main tool I rely on to get from A to B is my feet — and with the loss of feeling I’ve got now, that’s what’s going to make it really difficult."

He is also encouraging others to join him along the route.

  A portrait of the pair ahead of their walk 

*(Image: Guide Dogs)*

John said: "The more the merrier. I’ll have some support along the way, but anyone else who would like to join us for part of the trek would be very welcome.

“Max proves that disability doesn’t mean a lack of ambition. This is about showing what’s possible.”

So far, he has raised more than £1,000 and hopes to double that amount so that he can name a guide dog puppy.
