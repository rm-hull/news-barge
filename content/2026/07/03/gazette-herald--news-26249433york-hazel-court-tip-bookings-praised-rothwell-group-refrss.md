THE boss of a firm based near a York tip has welcomed the introduction of a booking system there.

Tom Gotts, managing director of the Rothwell Group, said requiring visitors to Hazel Court to book their visits made much more sense and was supported by nearby businesses.

Cllr Jenny Kent, City of York Council’s Labour environment spokesperson, said the changes were about making sure the tip worked efficiently for residents, businesses and the wider road network.

Ian Hoult, the council’s head of environmental services, said they were continuing to refine the system based on feedback.

It follows the launch of bookings for slots to visit Hazel Court, in James Street, on Monday, February 23 ahead of the system going live the following week.

Bookings were brought in in part to tackle congestion around the site which was affecting nearby businesses.

Rothwell Group Managing Director Mr Gotts said the bookings had drastically improved the experience around Hazel Court.

He said: ” It has saved my business hours of time sat in queues, and the booking system makes much more sense for all businesses operating around the area.

“We’d like to see it made permanent.”

The booking system, which sees residents allowed four 10-minute slots a week and businesses two-a-day for 15 minutes each, is being trialled for a year.

The council’s Towthorpe tip, in Moor Lane, Strensell, remains open to visit without requiring a booking.

A council survey found more than eight in 10 visitors reported spending less time waiting to visit the site than they had previously.

The proportion of respondents satisfied with the booking system was 86 per cent.

But data from tip operator Yorwaste showed the number of visitors to Hazel Court fell year-on-year from 21,332 to 9,909.

Speaking three months after the launch of the trial, Cllr Kent said the results so far showed the trial was working well and was helping to manage demand and traffic.

Cllr Paula Widdowson, environment spokesperson for the Liberal Democrat opposition, said visitor number figures showed the new system was not working and risked fuelling fly-tipping.
