Search the Public Notice PortalAn elderly couple have been threatened with a bill of almost £1,000 unless they remove a tiny garden outside their home.

Hilary and Christopher Garner have been told by a contractor that they have broken the rules by sprucing up the ‘managed ground’ next to their driveway in Hallow, Worcestershire.

After moving in towards the end of last year, the keen gardeners thought they were doing the neighbourhood a favour by adding attractive plants and flowers to the roughly 3x3-metre patch.

But they have since been sent letters by Ground Solutions UK Ltd, who maintain the developer's land, warning them that 'cultivation' is banned on shared open spaces in the estate.

The retirees have been told that £954 will be added to their service charge if they do not ‘rectify’ the patch.

## 'Witch-hunt'

Mr Garner, 85, said: “It’s a new estate and our house was the last to be built.

"When we moved in, the ground next to our drive was not in a good state at all, so I planted it up and it looks pretty lovely.

“I thought everybody would be quite pleased, the neighbours certainly are, but that’s not the case with the company who have been contracted to do the grass cutting.”

Mrs Garner, 83, is adamant they have not blocked access to infrastructure such as pipes and cables.

"When we moved in we knew we'd be squashed up against a hedge but we were promised cultivation and planting which didn't happen despite the fact we had asked for it," she said.

“So we did it ourselves on a tiny, minute piece of land, and everyone has said how nice it looks.

“It doesn’t interfere with anybody at all, so we’re absolutely amazed to receive letters telling us that if we don’t take it all down they will and we’ll be charged £1,000.

“We have asked where exactly the land is that they are talking about and it changes from letter to letter.

"It feels like a witch-hunt, we can’t understand it.

"At the moment we are standing our ground, it will be interesting to see if they have the nerve to come and do it.”

## 'Guerrilla gardening'

The couple’s son, Nathan Garner, is also bemused and angry about the letters, which gave a deadline of June 22, leaving his parents waiting to see what action the contractors will take.

He said: “Where’s the common sense?

“My parents understand that the land isn’t theirs and access for any maintenance, drains, etc. is fine.

"I see it as guerrilla gardening which should be celebrated.

“It encourages wildlife and improves a bare grassy corner.

“The world has gone mad!”

## Why have they been told to remove the garden?

Mike Pett, managing director of Ground Solutions, confirmed that the company has written to the couple on five occasions.

He told the Worcester News that the intent was “to resolve this matter amicably and without the need for further action.”

Mr Pett said: “This approach has been taken following concerns and complaints raised by residents of the development.

“The land in question remains in the ownership of the developer and is due to be transferred to the Residents’ Management Company in due course.

"The use of this land is governed by the covenants contained within the transfer documentation completed upon the purchase of each property.

“These covenants provide that all managed open spaces within the development are intended for recreational purposes only, and the cultivation or planting of such areas without prior authorisation is not permitted.”

Mr Pett said that the planted area is subject to three-metre wide Western Power Distribution ‘easement’.

He explained: “This easement grants the utility provider rights to access, inspect, maintain, repair, replace and upgrade the electrical infrastructure located within the corridor.

"As a result, the planting of trees within this easement area is not appropriate and may interfere with the exercise of those rights and over time, may cause damage to the services.”

Mr Pett added: “Our reason for issuing repeated correspondence has been to provide Mr and Mrs Garner with every reasonable opportunity to resolve the matter voluntarily.

"Our preference is to avoid the need to instruct contractors to remove the trees and plants at additional cost.

"The matter can be resolved simply by relocating the planting from the managed land onto their own property.

"Nevertheless, we remain committed to finding a practical and amicable solution and would welcome the opportunity to discuss the matter further with Mr and Mrs Garner.”
