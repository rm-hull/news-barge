A growing campaign is calling on the Government to halve VAT on pubs, restaurants and cafes amid warnings that rising costs are forcing more hospitality businesses to close their doors.

The petition, which has attracted almost 15,000 signatures, argues that cutting VAT from 20% to 10% would help save jobs, protect high streets and prevent more venues disappearing from towns and cities across the UK.

Campaigners say many hospitality businesses are struggling to cope with soaring food prices, energy bills, wages and rent while paying the full rate of VAT.


The petition states: "Reduce VAT on hospitality to 10% to prevent closures and protect tax revenue."

It adds: "Hospitality businesses operate on very tight margins and struggle to absorb 20% VAT alongside rising food, energy, wage and rent costs."

Campaigners believe a permanent VAT cut would help businesses stay open while continuing to pay taxes and employ staff.

They argue: "This would protect local jobs, keep pubs and restaurants open, and help high streets, tourism and communities survive."


## Government says VAT cut would cost £11bn

However, ministers have rejected the proposal, warning that such a move would leave a huge hole in the public finances.

In its official response, the Government said cutting VAT on hospitality to 10% would cost around £11 billion every year.

HM Treasury said: "Reducing VAT for hospitality to 10 per cent would come at a significant fiscal cost."

Officials added that £11 billion is roughly equivalent to the UK's annual Child Benefit bill, the Royal Navy's yearly budget or increasing the standard rate of VAT by one percentage point.

Instead, ministers pointed to the new Great British Summer Savings scheme, which temporarily cuts VAT from 20% to 5% on children's meals in participating restaurants and on selected family attractions until September 1.

The Government said: "Pubs, cafes and restaurants are at the heart of our communities."

But it added that tax changes are considered only at Budget time and must be balanced against the wider state of the public finances.

The petition remains open until September 12 and would need 100,000 signatures to be considered for a debate in Parliament.
