A forgotten trophy, a smallpox scandal, and a fresh look at rugby league history are among the revelations in a new book.

These are just some of the forgotten stories uncovered by sports journalist and author Ian Lockwood, marking York Knights’ rise to the Super League.

The book offers the most detailed account yet of York’s rugby league origins and early years, beginning with the story of seven men who gathered in a York house to launch what would become a club with 158 years of history.

Mr Lockwood, a former sports editor of The Press who covered York Rugby League Club from 1988 to 1992, said: "When York were promoted to the Super League I thought I would look up their early history and really there were very few details.

"I’ve always been interested in sport in that era, and so I started research.

"Almost all of the book is new material."

Among the most intriguing tales is the story of a missing trophy.

In 1901, York won the Division Two Yorkshire Championship and earned promotion to the top division.

The club was presented with a cup, which was handed to the club president and York MP George Faber for safekeeping, but it was never seen again.

Shortly after their victory, league authorities reorganised the competition into a single division, making York the final holders of the now-defunct trophy.

The book also sheds light on York’s move from the Yorkshire Gentlemen’s Cricket Ground to Clarence Street, which became their home for the next 103 years.

Contrary to long-held beliefs that the move was financially motivated, Mr Lockwood’s research found that the club paid more than four times as much in rent at the new ground.

Other revelations include a major controversy in 1904, when York defied medical advice and played a match during a smallpox epidemic.

The decision led to serious warnings, including the threat that players could be forcibly quarantined.

Army officers were stationed at Clarence Street to prevent soldiers from Fulford Barracks from attending, and schools warned pupils they would not be readmitted without a medical certificate if they went to the game.

The book also uncovers a darker chapter in the club’s history.

Tot Moore, one of the first inductees into the York RL Hall of Fame, saw his career end after being brought before York magistrates for domestic abuse and drunkenness.

Mr Lockwood has previously written biographies of England and Yorkshire cricketer Ted Peate, as well as French world champion boxer and First World War fighter pilot Georges Carpentier.
