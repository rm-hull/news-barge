A YORK MP has called for a major rethink of how Yorkshire Water is owned and run after the company's annual report has been posted.

York Outer MP Luke Charters has called for 'fundamental change' to the region's water operator.

He says that mutualisation - a model giving customers and communities a direct stake in the company - would put public interest above profit.

His comments follow Yorkshire Water’s latest annual report, which revealed a 72 per cent increase in pollution incidents (rising from 151 to 260), £81 million paid to foreign shareholders in dividends, and a company debt now exceeding £7.5 billion.

  Mr Charters has been a long-time campaigner on Yorkshire Water - taking on **a campaign which saw him join local residents in calling on Yorkshire Water to take action and reintroduce a pump to stop flooding at Hartrigg Oaks in New Earswick**, an issue residents say has been going on for years. 

On this year's annual report, Luke said: "If Yorkshire Water handed this report in as homework, it would be sent back covered in red ink.

"Pollution is up. Bills are up. Debt is up. Yet somehow the rewards at the top keep flowing."

Ofwat fined the company £88.8 million for missing performance targets, and the Environment Agency is expected to give it its lowest possible one-star rating this year.

The report also confirmed that former chief executive Nicola Shaw continues to receive £660,000 through offshore arrangements.

Luke said: "That £660,000 payment is 660,000 reasons why people have lost faith in Yorkshire Water. Every pound spent rewarding failure is another pound that could have been invested in fixing leaks, upgrading ageing pipes and cleaning up our rivers."

The MP said he has heard from hundreds of constituents over the past year who are fed up with Yorkshire Water’s performance, mentioning issues such as sewage in rivers, burst pipes, and rising bills.

He said: "I've said before that we should seriously consider mutualisation, and Yorkshire Water has become the strongest argument for it.

"Instead of making decisions to maximise profits for shareholders, a mutual company would exist to provide the best possible service for the people who pay the bills."

Mr Charters argued that under mutualisation, regional bill payers and governments could gradually take ownership, ensuring the company serves the communities it operates in.

He said: "People don't expect miracles. They expect clean rivers, reliable pipes and a company that spends their money on fixing problems instead of explaining why they haven't. Yorkshire Water has run out of excuses.

"It's time to stop rewarding failure, start investing properly in its infrastructure and rebuild the trust of the communities it serves."

As reported by The Press last month, Luke hosted a public town hall meeting to discuss the future of Yorkshire Water with residents, campaigners, and academics and he has previously called for tougher action against failing water companies.
