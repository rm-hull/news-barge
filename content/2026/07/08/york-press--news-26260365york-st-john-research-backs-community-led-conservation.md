Nature engagement and environmental action go hand in hand, according to new research from York St John University.

A five-year evaluation of the REConnect programme highlights the power of community involvement and emotional connections with nature in driving positive environmental behaviour.

The REConnect initiative, focused on the River Esk and Coastal Streams Catchment, aims to reconnect communities with the natural world while supporting long-term conservation and biodiversity recovery.

At the heart of the programme is the River Esk, a 28-mile waterway running from the North York Moors to the North Sea at Whitby.

The river is ecologically renowned for harbouring England's last significant population of critically endangered freshwater pearl mussels and is the sole major river in Yorkshire to host Atlantic salmon.

It is also home to otters and water voles.

The REConnect programme is delivered by a partnership including the North York Moors National Park Authority, Yorkshire Wildlife Trust, Groundwork, and the Yorkshire Marine Nature Partnership.

The project is funded by The National Lottery Community Fund and is designed to test how place-based approaches can address biodiversity loss and build community resilience.

Dr Jenny Hall, associate professor in cultural geographies at York St John University, said: "Our research highlights that positive and lasting results grows from meaningful experiences in nature, strengthened by community insights and a sense of shared responsibility.

"By combining hands-on learning with local knowledge and citizen science, communities can play a powerful role in driving sustainable behaviour and conservation action.

"The next phase of our work is to understand what nature in the Esk Valley means to people living there and how we can protect it together."

The report’s early findings show that emotional and sensory experiences—such as wildlife watching, riverside exploration, and creative activities in nature—not only deepen understanding but also foster empathy and a sense of duty towards the environment.

The research emphasises the value of everyday observations by residents, suggesting that informal networks could be transformed into powerful citizen science groups with the right support and training.

Childhood experiences in nature were also identified as key in shaping lifelong environmental values, underlining the importance of involving children in outdoor activities and education.

Recommendations to strengthen the programme include expanding education schemes that highlight natural river processes and connectivity from source to sea, using sensory and experiential learning methods.

Other proposals include launching intergenerational learning activities, formalising citizen science networks, and partnering with schools and community organisations to broaden participation.

A new survey has been launched by York St John University to gather insights from people living in the River Esk and Coastal Streams Catchment.

The results will help partners better understand which activities have the greatest impact and shape future engagement efforts.
