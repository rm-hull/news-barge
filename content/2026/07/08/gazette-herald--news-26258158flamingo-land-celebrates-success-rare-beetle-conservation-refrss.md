A rare beetle known as the “Jewel of York” is at the heart of a new conservation effort.

The iridescent green insect is being protected and reintroduced thanks to conservation work at Flamingo Land Resort in North Yorkshire.

The beetle, often called the Jewel of the Riverbank, now has a new home within a dedicated area at the park, designed to support its long-term survival, as part of wider efforts led by the Yorkshire Derwent Catchment Partnership (YDCP).

Kieran Holliday, head of conservation, education and research at Flamingo Land, said: "This area represents a small but vital piece of the puzzle.

*(Image: Kieran Holliday)*

"We’re trying to restore the missing building blocks for a species that is clinging to survival.

"With the right plants in the right places, we can begin to offer new lifelines for the tansy beetle.

"It has taken five years to get to this point, with planting taking place in 2024 and the beetles being released in May this year, and we have already seen signs of success with tansy beetle larvae seen, proving they have settled in nicely and are breeding."

The project is part of a broader conservation effort supported by the Species Recovery Trust and the Tansy Beetle Action Group.

  Tansy beetle larvae 

*(Image: Kieran Holliday)*

In early May, 50 tansy beetles were released into the carefully managed "tansy garden" within the park’s zoo area.

The beetle, once common along British riverbanks, now survives mainly along a short stretch of the River Ouse in York.

Its decline is due to habitat loss, flooding and competition from invasive species.

The Flamingo Land site is intended to be a safe, well-connected habitat where the beetles can feed, breed, and eventually disperse into new areas.

  The project is part of a broader conservation effort supported by the Species Recovery Trust 

*(Image: Kieran Holliday)*

Mr Holliday said: "This enclosure not only serves as a stepping stone, helping the tansy beetle move beyond its current stronghold, but also as an area for education, where guests can further their knowledge of this intriguing species and the amazing wildlife found in Britain."

The habitat is planted with tansy (Tanacetum vulgare), the beetle’s sole food source and breeding plant.

Adults feed on the leaves, while females lay eggs on the stems.

This is the latest in Flamingo Land’s series of conservation projects, which also include its Yorkshire Crayfish Hatchery, Yorkshire Beaver Facility, and extensive on-site wildlife monitoring.

The park recorded the first sighting of a harvest mouse in North Yorkshire in more than five years.
