## Teachers picket York school amid trust changes saying '150 years of experience' going

NASUWT members on the picket line outside Manor CE Academy school in Millfield Lane today (Wednesday, July 8) *(Image: Supplied)*

MEMBERS of a teaching union picketed outside a school in York today (Wednesday, July 8) claiming 27 pastoral staff will be losing their roles and “150 years of experience will be going out of the window” in changes planned at the local academy trust.

The NASUWT said the industrial action outside Manor CE School in Millfield Lane, Nether Poppleton, was in response to a decision by Heartwood Learning Trust to introduce “non-teaching learning manager” roles from September.

The trust said pastoral support will be strengthened, and no redundancies have been made as part of the changes, and it consulted with colleagues and trade unions.

  


  NASUWT members on the picket line outside Manor CE Academy school in Millfield Lane today (Wednesday, July 8) 

*(Image: Supplied)*

Tim Toepitz, a teacher and NASUWT national executive member for West and North Yorkshire, said 50 per cent of members balloted - over 75 teachers in formal ballots and 120 in indicative ballots - voted for the action and the decision was almost unanimous in three schools.

Pastoral roles are student-support positions focused on wellbeing, behaviour, attendance and welfare across its academies.

At Heartwood Learning Trust schools, these are said to include head of year, head of house - which the union said has been led by teachers since 1924 - and progress leader roles, the union said, and almost all of the pastoral roles also include classroom responsibilities.

Union reps say they play a vital part in supporting the academic function and Mr Toepitz claimed around one-third of trust staff in executive teaching or senior positions could include pastoral roles within their career progression to date and these moves effectively “close down the very route” which was open to them.

He said: “The trust has known about this planned action since the start of the year.

“The only agreement is that it guarantees more hours.

“The union has no problem with a pastoral system which involves both teachers and support staff – we encourage it as it is the best model.

“This is a really good opportunity to embed that into the schools but they decided to let 150 years of teaching experience go out of the window.

“This isn’t good for the kids and the staff.

“Behaviour and academic outcomes are two strands of the same DNA”

A spokesperson for Heartwood Learning Trust said: “The proposed restructure was shared with trade unions in January 2026.

“We were notified of the NASUWT's ballot for industrial action in May and have continued to engage with union representatives throughout.

“A formal consultation was undertaken with all employees whose roles were directly affected by the proposals.

“We also kept all colleagues across our schools informed throughout the process, so they were aware of the proposed changes and the reasons behind them.

"Our executive team reflects a broad range of professional backgrounds, including in curriculum, SEND and pastoral leadership.

“We recognise the valuable contribution that teachers have made, and will continue to make, to the pastoral life of our schools.”

Tim Toepitz said: “Pastoral roles teach us what’s going on outside the core development areas, and some of the 27 colleagues have been in their role for decades.

“There’s been no OFSTED report or questions over pastoral staff ability and the consultation letter thanked them for the contribution and then effectively said, ‘We’re taking your job’.”

Mr Toepitz claims pastoral staff have been taken off their day-to-day duties to complete additional data entry tasks until the end of the academic year to support the handover – a claim the Trust denies, saying “management systems are updated on an ongoing basis to ensure records remain accurate and compliant.”

The Trust spokesperson said: “Throughout the consultation process, we actively engaged with colleagues and trade unions and carefully considered all feedback received.

“Changes were made to the original proposals as a result of feedback, including the creation of additional teaching leadership opportunities.

“Individual discussions have taken place with affected staff and career pathways have been identified to support their future professional development.

“No redundancies have been made as part of these changes and colleagues affected by the restructure remain employed by the trust and continue to receive their current salary.

“Importantly, this new structure is not a cost-saving exercise.

“We have made a significant financial investment in the new structure, which will provide many additional hours of pastoral support each academic year across our secondary schools.

“This additional capacity will improve the support available to young people and help schools respond more effectively to the increasing pastoral needs of students.

“Many other schools and trusts already structure their pastoral support in a similar way and this change has been developed with one clear objective, to strengthen the pastoral support available to students and families.

“As they are not in teaching roles, learning managers will be fully available throughout the school day to support students, respond to concerns, work with families and external agencies, and provide early intervention assistance.

“The new model simply reflects our belief that dedicated pastoral professionals can provide expertise, availability and support for students, while allowing teachers to focus on providing excellent teaching and learning.”
