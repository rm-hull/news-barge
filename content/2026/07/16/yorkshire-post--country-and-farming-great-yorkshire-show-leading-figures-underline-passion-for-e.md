# Great Yorkshire Show: Leading figures underline passion for event

## The spectrum of rural life on display at the Great Yorkshire Show was brought into sharp relief as leading figures underlined their passion for the event. Stuart Minting and Emma Ryan report.

Amid the dust and straw of the stables beside the Great Yorkshire Show’s equestrian rings legendary Upper Cumberworth equestrian John Whitaker cut an unassuming figure as he prepared for one of his four gruelling days competing at the event.

Stopping to embrace friends, the 70-year-old has competed at the show well in excess of 40 times. The 70-year-old said: “Sometimes I have missed it if I’m on a championship team and it’s unavoidable, but if I can I’m here.

“The horse I’m riding, Unick Du Francport, was a bit unlucky not to win Cock of the North last year.

![Archbishop of York Stephen Cottrell is pictured with Barney O'Kane with his Charolais cow called Isla Goldstar April](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOmJkMjIzZjFjLTE4YzctNDQ4OS1iNjlhLWNiZWNjZmViYzFlODo1MGE3NDdjMC0wMWNjLTRhMzUtOTZjNy04NjUxNDk4YzU2OWE=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



"I had the quickest time in the jump-off at one down. He hasn’t actually won it, but he does jump well here. I’m happy to be here and I’ll try and win.”

When asked how he maintained his fitness, the 1984 Los Angeles Olympics silver medallist said he rode for four or five hours a day.

He said: “I don’t do much more physical training. My body has adapted to sitting on a horse.

"Walking around you might think I look a bit like an old man, but when I get on a horse I’m ready to go!

“People say don’t stop riding. If I can do it I’ll do it! I say I can’t stop now because I’ve just bought some new boots and they last me four or five years!”

A short distance away, a packed Agri-Innovation Village Teepee, visitors were hearing insights from one of the country’s leading agricultural industry experts, secretary of the Central Association for Agricultural Valuers Jeremy Moody.

He said the loss of direct payments to farmers was a “passing of the baton” that represented a new era in how agricultural enterprises are run.

Mr Moody said: “Now the central decision isn’t looking to government for a steer, it’s looking at what best suits the business, what best meets the family’s objectives.”

He said the sector remained unaware of the direction that the incoming government administration would take the sector, but said Andy Burnham would have “between now and the autumn budget for him to show where he is going if he is simply not going to be a Starmer II”.

Mr Moody said: “There is a blank canvas called Burnham. The fear is that he is at the bottom of his learning curve for these things.

"If he is to be effective, the three years left in him, with very strained public finances, he has got to do the big things early, get in and strike.”

How trends can affect farming businesses was the focus of discussions at a Future Farmers of Yorkshire meeting at the Ariat GYS Stage.

Rufus Pilgrim, managing director of York-based potato farm, R S Cockerill, said even the rising price of traditional dishes was having a knock-on effect. He said: “Even good old fish and chips have gotten expensive and that is nothing to do with potatoes but it is the cost of fish making it a rare and expensive treat.”

However, he said some trends, a lot driven by social media, can work in favour of the farmer and even encourage them to grow different types of crop or breed different livestock.

Baked potatoes, for example, are having a resurgence through social media.

Mr Pilgrim said: “Our farming sector has shown how resilient and adaptive it can be. With the right support it can turn short term gain into long term opportunities.”

People no longer want large joints of meat to spend hours cooking, she said, adding that busy families prefer mince, diced cuts.

Among crowds admiring what onlookers considered to be a particularly fine array of Charolais heifers being judged in the Upper Cattle Rings was the Archbishop of York Stephen Cottrell.

“I’m here because it’s one of the great events in the Yorkshire calendar,” he said.

“As Archbishop of York how could I not be here? It’s a lovely day and I’m privileged to have presented a rosette to the champion Violet, but really I’m here because farming is so central to the life of our nation, obviously to our county.

“Food security is a really important issue. Our nation produces less of its own food than it did ten or 20 years ago.

"That’s not good for the environment, the rural economy here or the food security of our nation. There are so many reasons why supporting local farmers and eating local produce makes sense.”

Mr Cottrell said he had made a beeline to see woodcrafters in action because I wanted to see the people making shepherd’s staffs.

He said: “As a bishop I carry a shepherd’s staff, it’s one of the basic symbols of the church and also shows how the farming and church community are bound together.”
