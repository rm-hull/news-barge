## Council pledges support in fight to save paid coastguards

 *(Image: Whitby Coastguard)*

The leader of North Yorkshire Council will lobby the Government over a plan to cut the pay of coastguard rescue officers.

Coastguard rescue officers should not be replaced by charities and volunteers, Cllr Carl Les, the leader of North Yorkshire Council, has said.

It comes as more than half of the coastguards in Yorkshire and Humberside said they would be forced to reduce their hours or stop volunteering altogether over plans which would see coastguard officers stripped of their hourly pay.

The scheme would make coastguards volunteers rather than paid workers from later in the year and has been opposed by dozens of councils, unions, MPs, and members of the public.

Cllr Derek Bastiman, the chair of a group of 58 coastal local authorities and a member of North Yorkshire Council, backed the idea of an inquiry by the House of Commons Transport Select Committee into proposals.

In a letter to Keir Mather, the Maritime Minister and MP for Selby, Cllr Bastiman noted: “Coastal local authorities recognise the invaluable contribution made by the approximately 3,500 coastguard rescue officers who provide frontline emergency response services in communities around the United Kingdom.

“CROs are the fourth emergency service and should be treated as such – government must not just rely on charities to maintain a statutory rescue service.”

Cllr Les said he would join Cllr Bastiman in writing to Mr Mather.

He noted: “Coastguard rescue officers do an important job supplemented by volunteers and charities, but should not be replaced by volunteers and charities, especially when the savings proposed are quite small in the scheme of things, and when compared to the value of what they do.”

  Coun Carl Les


Speaking at a full meeting of NYC on Wednesday, July 15, Cllr Bastiman said: “They are extremely important people who get paid a pittance of £11 an hour for the first three hours and nothing after that.

“They’ve been involved with flooding and search and rescue, and it does affect everyone in this chamber. They also attend incidents no one else would want to.”

The scheme to change the status of coastguards follows a Court of Appeal ruling, which confirmed that people who had been serving as coastguard rescue officers had been doing so as workers, rather than volunteers.

Subsequently, the Maritime and Coastguard Agency (MCA) decided to stop the payments from September, in order to start legally classing CROs as volunteers.

Cllr Bastiman, who is chairman of the LGA’s Coastal Special Interest Group, has urged Mr Mather to “undertake meaningful consultation with serving coastguard rescue officers, elected representatives and coastal stakeholders and to publish the assessment of the potential operational impacts on recruitment, retention and emergency response capability before implementation – after implementation it will simply be too late”.
