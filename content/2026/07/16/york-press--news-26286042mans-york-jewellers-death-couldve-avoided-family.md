A man’s death after being restrained by four men at a York jewellery store “could have been avoided”, his family has said.

Sam Diatta, who had bipolar disorder and heart disease, went into cardiac arrest while being restrained at the Mappin and Webb jewellery store, then in Coney Street, for around seven minutes on July 26, 2022, an inquest heard.

Recording a narrative conclusion on Thursday (July 16), senior coroner Jon Heath said Mr Diatta, 39, of Holgate, “died as a consequence of naturally occurring heart disease combined with the effect of being restrained”.

CCTV footage played at the inquest showed Mr Diatta throwing punches at the shop’s door supervisor then being restrained by the supervisor and three members of the public.

Mr Heath said he could not conclude that the way Mr Diatta was restrained by the four men met the threshold for manslaughter.

But he said he did recognise the medical cause of death which was given as hypertensive heart disease and the effects of being restrained.

The inquest heard that Mr Diatta had been discharged from Foss Park Hospital four days before his death and had been on a community treatment order, allowing him to receive support while living in the community.

Speaking to reporters outside Northallerton Coroners’ Court, the Diatta family’s barrister, Parissa Najah, said: “The family remain convinced that Sam was not well enough to be living in the community on the days prior to his death due to the concerns about his mental health and how it had deteriorated.

“Their view is reinforced by the fact that just four days after his discharge from hospital, Sam lost his life in circumstances that could have been avoided.”

  Parissa Najah speaks to the media outside Northallerton Coroners' Court on Thursday (July 16)

She said Mr Diatta had been suffering an “acute mental health episode” on the day of his death.

“He was restrained face down on the floor for more than seven minutes during which time he shouted, ‘I can’t breathe.’ Tragically, those words were not heard,” the barrister said.

“Being restrained in that position for so long by several men was established by the coroner as being part of the medical cause of Sam’s death.”

Mr Heath declined to make a prevent of future death report, made when a coroner has ongoing concerns about a matter revealed during the inquest, but told the court he may do so at a later stage.

The coroner did, however, ask the Tees, Esk and Wear Valleys NHS Foundation Trust (TEWV), which runs Foss Park Hospital and provides mental health services in York, for clarify about the trust's process for interacting with pharmacies.

It came after the inquest heard Mr Diatta had been unable to collect medication from his local pharmacy in Blossom Street as staff had not been told he was discharged from hospital.

During the inquest, Shona McIlrae, an NHS consultant psychiatrist who treated Mr Diatta since 2014, was asked if Mr Diatta should have been kept in hospital longer before he was discharged.

Dr McIlrae responded by saying Mr Diatta had been given a “robust assessment” before he was deemed fit to be released.

Dr McIlrae also said the Mental Health Act, which Mr Diatta had been recalled to hospital under, only allows patients to be held in hospital for 72 hours before they are eligible to be discharged.

After Mr Diatta’s death, North Yorkshire Police said four men were arrested in connection with the death and were interviewed and released while under investigation.

But in July 2023 the force said no one would be charged with any offence following Mr Diatta’s death.
