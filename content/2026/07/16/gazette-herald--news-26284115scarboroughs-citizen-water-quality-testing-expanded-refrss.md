Community-led testing of water quality could be rolled out across North Yorkshire, council bosses have said.

Scarborough’s South Bay, which is a popular bathing spot for tourists and residents alike, is classed as ‘poor’ by the Environment Agency and it advises against swimming at the resort’s beach.

A new citizen science project on the North Yorkshire coast is hoping to demonstrate that despite a poor annual classification, the water quality is not consistently bad, and an expansion of the pilot project could be on the cards.

Independent councillor Rich Maw, who set out and proposed the plans for the citizen science bathing water pilot, which is now underway, has urged North Yorkshire Council to take note of the scheme.

Speaking at a full meeting of the council on Wednesday, July 15, he said: “The pilot is now underway with trained volunteers using rapid testing to provide indicative daily information alongside official EA monitoring.

“Subject to the success of the monitoring, would the executive be willing to explore, with partners, a medium-term rollout of community-led testing to provide more regular information for residents and visitors, particularly at South Bay, where the official annual classification is ‘poor’?”

He added: “Indicative testing may show that water quality is suitable on many days during the bathing season.”

Cllr Richard Foster, executive member for the environment, said that there may be scope to expand and support community-led testing projects.

He noted: “Current water quality testing relies on periodic sampling and testing during the bathing season.

“While essential, these processes are time-consuming and only provide a historic picture, rather than a view of what is happening when people enter the water.

“As a result, the public often learns about potential health risks after the fact.

“Therefore, we would be pleased to explore, with partners, community testing, which is a valuable and powerful tool for strengthening environmental monitoring and boosting public engagement.”

Cllr Foster added: “Although no replacement for official monitoring, we recognise that local groups possess a unique connection and geographical knowledge that make them ideal for leading local monitoring projects.”
