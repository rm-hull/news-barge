Family members have said they “choose forgiveness” for the men who restrained their son and brother at a York jewellery store resulting in his death.

Sam Diatta, 39, of Holgate, who had bipolar disorder and heart disease, died after being restrained by four men at the Mappin and Webb jewellery store, then in Coney Street, on July 26, 2022.

Recording a narrative conclusion on Thursday (July 16), senior coroner Jon Heath said Mr Diatta “died as a consequence of naturally occurring heart disease combined with the effect of being restrained”.

After Mr Diatta’s death, North Yorkshire Police said four men were arrested in connection with the death and were interviewed and released while under investigation.

But in July 2023 the force said no one would be charged with any offence following Mr Diatta’s death.

Speaking to the media outside Northallerton Coroners’ Court, Mr Diatta’s family said he was suffering an “acute mental health episode” when he threw punches at the jewellery store’s door supervisor which resulted in him being restrained.

The inquest heard that Mr Diatta had been released from Foss Park Hospital four days before his death on a community treatment order.

Mr Diatta’s family said he was “not well enough to be living in the community” and believed his death “could have been avoided” if he was kept at the psychiatric hospital.

  Sam Diatta 

*(Image: North Yorkshire Police)*

During the inquest, Shona McIlrae, an NHS consultant psychiatrist who had treated Mr Diatta since 2014, said Mr Diatta had been given a “robust assessment” before he was deemed fit to be released from Foss Park Hospital.

Mr Diatta’s father, mother and sister said they hoped the four men who restrained Mr Diatta could move on with their lives.

## 'We choose forgiveness'

The family members said they had spoken to some of the men involved in the restraint during the inquest and shook hands.

Mr Diatta’s sister, Teba, said: “We choose forgiveness. We don’t want to hold it against the people involved – we want them to get on with their lives and not be held back by this."

“Sam wasn’t a bad person,” she said. “He was having a really bad day due to his mental health.

“He was a lovely, gentle soul who was very forgiving.

“He was kind, loving, he was generous – we really miss him.

“We just don’t want him to be remembered for an act of violence on a day that he was so poorly.”

Teba said her brother was “really generous” and “looked out for people”.

She said he was a “gentle giant” and the case demonstrated the “reality of having a severe illness – it can change your mood and your actions”.

“When he was 20, he jumped into the River Ouse and saved somebody who tried to kill themself," Teba added. "That was typically him.”

Mr Diatta’s mother, Judith, described her son as “kind” and “generous”.

She said Mr Diatta grew up in Leeman Road and attended St Barnabas CE Primary School then Manor CE Academy where he “loved all sports”.

He was a member of St Barnabas Church in Leeman Road and Judith said: “As Christians, we know Sam had a real faith in Jesus – and we do too – and we know that we will meet him again. And that’s a great comfort.”

Judith said she hoped lessons could be learned from her son’s death, telling reporters: “To be restrained in the prone position for so long must have been terrible for him – I hope that’s taken seriously for future cases.”
