## Major York employer issues warning over shares offer

The York offices of insurance giant Aviva. *(Image: Nadia Jefferson-Brown)*

A MAJOR York employer has urged its shareholders not to sell its shares cheaply to a US investment firm.

Insurance giant Aviva, which employs around 2,000 people in York, has issued a warning about a company called Litani.

The US firm is targeting Aviva shareholders through a “mini-tender” offer, seeking to buy Aviva shares at £5.30 each - significantly below Aviva’s trading price today (Wed) of around £6.61.

Litani has contacted around 100,000 Aviva investors about the offer, which does not require approval from the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) under UK regulations.

The FTSE 100 insurer has written to shareholders warning people not to sell to Litani.

  Aviva has also posted on its website: “We want to make you aware that a company called Litani LLC (“**Litani**”) may write, or may already have written, to you offering to buy some or all of your Aviva shares.

“Litani has told Aviva that it proposes to buy shares at 17.5 per cent below the market price, which means you would receive significantly less than the value of your shares.

  “Aviva has taken legal steps to try to prevent Litani from contacting shareholders, as we believe this offer is not in your best interests. Litani is not connected with Aviva, and **we do not recommend accepting any such offer.**

“Offers like this, sometimes called 'mini-tender offers', typically involve buying shares at a lower price and then selling them on at the full market price, allowing the buyer to make a profit at shareholders’ expense."

In the US, the Securities and Exchange Commission has issued warnings about mini-tender offers, which can mislead retail investors into selling shares below market value.

  Aviva, which is headed by CEO Amanda Blanc, has warned shareholders not to sell to US investment firm Litani. 

*(Image: PA)*

Litani made the approach after winning access to Aviva’s shareholder register in a recent legal challenge.

The firm is targeting up to one million shares, with its offer set to expire on January 29 next year.

It claims to be offering shareholders a "convenient way to sell your shares," with no commission or dealing charges.

While not illegal, the practice has raised concerns.

Litani is known for targeting shareholders of demutualised companies and reselling shares at a profit.

A demutualised company is one that has changed from being owned by its members or policyholders into a shareholder-owned business.

A High Court ruling last year found the offers were "economically disadvantageous" but stopped short of deeming them "commercially exploitative, oppressive or immoral".

Aviva, headed by CEO Amanda Blanc, maintains a large base of private investors dating back to its demutualisation.

In 2022, the company also gave £1,000 of shares as a ‘thank you’ to each of its 22,000 employees.
