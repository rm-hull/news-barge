## York city centre suffers 8 per cent slump in sales - with footfall down too

Busy Stonegate, but it seems not as busy as it was last year. *(Image: David Harrison)*

York city centre businesses suffered an 8.2 per cent slump in sales in May, compared to the same month last year, with a similar drop in footfall of 8.8 per cent.

The grim figures are revealed in the latest Monthly Insight report from York BID (Business Improvement District), which uses a variety of credit card spend and other data.

Sales and customer activity “weakened sharply in May”, said the report, with an 8.2 per cent drop in sales and 6.9 per cent drop in customer volumes compared with last year. The report said both figures were "below national benchmarks and marking the steepest decline of 2026 so far.”

The report also noted footfall “continues to trend down in 2026”, showing a decline of minus-8.8 per cent in May and down 8.2 per cent for the Year to Date, “closely mirroring sales and customer trends.”

Overall though, York BID’s figures report city centre sales in May of £35.1 million from 410,000 customers and footfall of 2.9 million. The average retail spend per customer was £85.52 based on 1.8 billion recorded transaction.

The May sales figure (down 8.2 per cent over the year), the insight report continued, was “the sharpest decline recorded so far this year” and was almost twice the UK annual decrease of 4.5 per cent.

The report also noted in the year to date, York performed worse than the national average sales figures, which was also down by 4.1 per cent, with York down 4.7 per cent.

Sector performance shifted significantly, with fashion sales down 30.4 per cent, general retail down 10.2 per cent, and food and drink down 7.5 per cent, seeing the largest drops. Year-to-date figures show fashion and general retail declining at roughly double the national rate, the report continued.

York residents remain the biggest spenders (39 per cent), but their city-centre spending has been falling since early 2024, contrasting with stronger performance witnessed at out-of-town shopping centres, where sales dropped by just 1.5 per cent.

The report said: “The divergence in performance suggests that local consumers may increasingly favour the convenience and accessibility offered by these destinations, presenting an ongoing challenge for the city centre retail offer.”

Visitors from outside Yorkshire and the North East generated 30 per cent of sales, with Yorkshire residents collectively responsible for 70 per cent of total spending.

The report also noted a sharp decline in the number of Chinese and Australian visitors, with an increase in French and Canadian tourists. The USA remains the top source of overseas visitors, responsible for a quarter, similar to last year.

Shop vacancies rose slightly to 5.7 per cent, still far below the national benchmark of 13.5 per cent, “signalling continued resilience in York’s retail occupancy despite wider market pressures.”

This compares to figures from Savills in its Shopping Centre and High Street Report, which estimates a national vacancy rate of 13.5 per cent as of the end of September 2025, the insight report added.

York Bid Executive Director Andrew Lowson said he would be discussing the figures at the Annual General Meeting of York Bid on Thursday night and he would save his comments for then.

The AGM, at the Radisson York Hotel will also feature retail expert Ojay McDonald, Chief Executive of the Association of Town & City Management (ATCM). At the meeting, Ojay will explore the evolving role of high streets in our communities, how national policy is shaping their future, and the opportunities and challenges ahead.
