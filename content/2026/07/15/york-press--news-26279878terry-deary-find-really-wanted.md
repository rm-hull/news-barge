## Writer Terry Deary to appear at Malton Roman Festival

Author Terry Deary

He's one of the country's best known writers but putting pen to paper was not Terry Deary's first career choice.

The author of the acclaimed Horrible Histories series, which introduced new generations of children to learning about the past in an entertaining, gory style, actually wanted to be a country singer.

"And then a professional actor," he tells me, as we chat ahead of his appearance at this Sunday's Malton Roman Festival.

"I always wanted to be a country music singer and still do but that didn't happen so I became an actor with a Welsh theatre company and we had to perform original stories for the communities we visited."

"We had the 'facts' but they needed to be shaped and given a dramatic structure and dialogue. It turned out I had a knack for writing so I accidentally became the company playwright.

"I wanted one of my popular children's plays to live on, so I adapted it as a children's book. It was accepted and writing became my life.".

Born in Sunderland in 1946, Terry put his so-called “fascination with the grotesque” down to the fact that, at the age of seven, he was taken to see a cow being slaughtered and skinned - his dad, for clarity, was a butcher.

Rather than leaving the young Terry in shock, he said that, instead, this experience “gave me a sense of realism”, teaching the valuable lesson that “life, death, is all part of the same cycle”.

At the age of 80, Terry's fascination with people is in no way waning.

"As an actor we are told the whole purpose of drama is to ask one question - "Why do people behave in the way they do?"

"I am fascinated in people - how they behave and what makes them tick, after all once a human being is dead they are pretty boring."

His latest book Revolting: A Riotous History of Rebellions and Revolutions offers a humorous exploration of rebellions and uprisings throughout history, which he will be discussing during his second visit to Malton's Roman Festival, courtesy of Malton Museum.

As Terry explains, although many may not actively rebel, there are those who defy conformity all around us.

"From the peasants to the slaves, the martyrs to the mutineers, Revolting celebrates the resilience and determination of those who dared to challenge the status quo through the ages."

Terry highlights well-known protests such as the Suffragettes and revolts as a result of the Irish Famine to more obscure ones, such as the Uprising of Ivaylo, a peasant revolt in medieval Bulgaria as a result of widespread discontent.

Closer to home, Deary explores the leader of the Pilgrimage of Grace, Robert Aske, who was hanged from the walls of York Castle and left to endure a slow agonising death, along with martyr Margaret Clitherow, who was incarcerated at York Castle and crushed to death on Ouse Bridge.

Born in 1556, Margaret was an English Catholic recusant known as The Pearl of York and converted to Roman Catholicism in 1574.

Her home became one of the most important hiding places for fugitive priests in the north of England, however on March 10 1586, Clitherow House was searched and the location of the priest hole found.

Although pregnant with her fourth child, Margaret was executed on Lady Day, 1586.

She was pressed to death for refusing to enter a plea to the charge of harbouring Catholic priests.

Margaret was canonised in 1970 by Pope Paul VI and in 2008, a commemorative plaque was installed at the Micklegate end of York's Ouse Bridge to mark the site of her martyrdom.

"Now Margaret is an incredible example to us all," Terry explains.

"Such was her influence, that 20,000 people are said to have passed by her corpse.

"The theory is that a 16-year-old Guy Fawkes was amongst those people and was influenced to convert to Catholicism. Now, that theory isn't supported by any particular historical evidence, but as a writer, and not an historian, I can say that it is pretty likely."

As well as Revolting,Terry's A History of the World in Ten Wars, has also recently been published.

"This is probably a more controversial one," he says.

"War is a terrible thing but we gain huge entertainment from it. The First World War poets wrote about awful things but they were praised for it, while half Shakespeare's plays are about war and killings."

On a 'lighter' note, Terry has also turned his attention to adult fiction, with novels ‘Actually, I’m a Murderer’, followed by 'Actually, I'm a Corpse'.

His third of the series, 'Actually, I'm a Gravedigger', is due out later this year.

Described as 'hilarious and whip-smart crime novels, perfect for lovers of Anthony Horowitz, (Terry's a big fan) and Richard Osman.

Set in the early 1970s in North Yorkshire and North East, the books were inspired by a late-night train ride from York to Sunderland.

"I was on the last train home and there was just me and four strangers, I didn't really think about it at the time, but years later when I started thinking about writing adult fiction, I thought 'what if one of them was a murderer' and it carried on from there."

Despite selling over £39million books in 45 languages, Terry has no wish for any sort of recognition and certainly nothing which mentions the British Empire, which he is said to 'loathe with a passion'.

"All these famous people taking these award, I don't get it.

"If my work makes people happy then that makes me happy - I certainly don't do it for the recognition."

"Take the volunteers who are organising Malton Roman Festival, these are the heroes who go around unnoticed doing good and give their time up for others - and they are such nice people.

"Malton Museum is also a great place with so much potential. I'm really looking forward to coming to the town on Sunday."

As our interview draws to a close, it's clear to hear how Terry has entertained generations of children and is now doing the same with the older generation.

And although he may not have made it into country music or acting, I think we can safely say his third choice of career has certainly been a bit of a success.
