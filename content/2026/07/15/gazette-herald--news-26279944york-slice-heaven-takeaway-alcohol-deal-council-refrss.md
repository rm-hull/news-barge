A York takeaway’s bid to sell alcohol with meals has been approved after it agreed to stop at an earlier time than planned amid public health fears.

Slice of Heaven, in Clifton’s Burton Stone Lane, reached a deal with York Council licensing officials who have approved its application to sell alcohol.

But the takeaway will only be able to sell drinks with meals until 11pm, stopping two hours earlier than the 1am it originally applied for.

A council spokesperson said they welcomed the agreement and changes which addressed concerns raised during the licensing process.

It comes as the application was set to go before a licensing hearing on Tuesday, July 14 following an objection from public health officials.

Slice of Heaven’s original application stated drinks would only be sold via deliveries with meals after 11pm and until 1am daily.

Applicant Taylan Ozer said their zero-tolerance approach to crime, disorder, violence and antisocial behaviour included a strict entry policy and only delivering alcohol to people aged 18 and over.

Mr Ozer’s application stated: “A very strict age lD policy will be applied at all times, both at the premises and on deliveries, although it is fair to say that the vast majority of customers are adults.

“Staff training will focus very carefully on ensuring that alcohol is not served to children.”

But council public health officials said late night sales of alcohol from the takeaway would pose risks to the local population.

They referred to data showing there were 245 hospital admissions caused primarily by drinking in the north of Clifton between 2016/7 and 2020/1.

Figures also showed admission rates were 40 per cent higher than the national average.

Officials said in their objection: “Burton Stone Lane features a diverse demographic, including young families and vulnerable adults.

“Public health data consistently demonstrates that expanding the hours during which alcohol can be procured triggers an increase in violent crime, public disorder and use of emergency services.

“Whilst premises such as pubs and clubs have on-site supervision and security, takeaway establishments and delivery services do not have the same protocols or controls.”

The council’s spokesperson said the reaching of a deal with the takeaway had removed the need for a full licensing hearing.

The spokesperson said: “We can confirm that following mediation an agreement was reached to amend the application so that the sale and supply of alcohol will end at 11pm, rather than 1am as originally requested.

“The council welcomes the agreement reached, which addresses the concerns raised during the licensing process and enables the application to proceed on the basis of the amended operating schedule.”
