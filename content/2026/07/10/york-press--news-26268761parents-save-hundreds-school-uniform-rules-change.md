From September 2026, schools across England will face new legal limits on the number of compulsory branded uniform items they can require pupils to wear.

The changes are designed to reduce the soaring cost of sending children back to school, with ministers saying affordability should never stop families choosing the school they want or taking part in school life.

Under the new rules, most schools will only be allowed to require three branded uniform or PE kit items, while secondary schools can require four if one of them is a compulsory school tie.

That means many families could soon be swapping expensive logo-embroidered jumpers, PE kits and specialist clothing for cheaper supermarket alternatives.

## The new school uniform rules

Schools have been told they must review their uniform policies before families begin buying clothes for the new school year.

From September 2026 they must:

- Limit compulsory branded uniform and PE kit to three items (or four in secondary schools where one is a tie)
- Allow generic alternatives wherever possible
- Publish updated uniform policies on their websites
- Tell parents where second-hand uniforms are available
- Review supply contracts with retailers

The Department for Education said schools should keep branded clothing to an absolute minimum and avoid requiring expensive bespoke items where a generic alternative would do.


## Parents could buy cheaper alternatives

The guidance makes clear that schools do not need branded clothing simply to control colour, style or appearance.

Instead of insisting on school-specific jumpers or trousers available from one retailer, schools can require plain items in a particular colour or style that parents can buy from supermarkets or high street shops.

Officials also encourage schools to use sew-on or iron-on badges instead of requiring expensive embroidered garments.

## Schools told to think about struggling families

Schools have also been instructed to consider whether the overall cost of their uniform could discourage low-income families from applying.

By September 2027 they must assess both the price of individual items and the total cost of a complete uniform, ensuring no item is unnecessarily expensive.

The government says particular attention should be given to traditionally costly items such as blazers and jumpers.

## Second-hand uniforms to become easier to access

The guidance also tells schools they should make second-hand uniforms widely available to all parents.

Information about second-hand sales or swap shops must be published on school websites and made available to families before children start school.

Schools are encouraged to ensure parents can access second-hand uniforms discreetly where needed.

## Single-supplier deals discouraged

Schools are also being warned against relying on a single uniform supplier unless they regularly put contracts out to competitive tender.

The government says cost and value for money should take priority when choosing suppliers, with any savings negotiated expected to be passed directly on to parents.

Schools should also avoid making frequent changes to uniforms, as these can leave families replacing perfectly good clothing unnecessarily.

## Why the rules are changing

Ministers say school uniforms can promote identity and reduce peer pressure, but only if families can afford them.

The new guidance says no parent should have to worry about the cost of school uniform when choosing a school, while no child should miss lessons, PE or extracurricular activities because uniform requirements are too expensive.

The changes form part of the government's wider plans to reduce the cost of the school day and tackle child poverty.
