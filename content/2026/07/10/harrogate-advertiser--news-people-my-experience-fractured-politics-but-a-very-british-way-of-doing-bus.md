# My Experience: Fractured politics but a very British way of doing business in the House of Commons

## Two years after winning the election as Harrogate and Knaresborough’s MP, the Harrogate Advertiser joined Tom Gordon at Westminster to take the temperature of Britain's fractured politics and see for himself how the town's interests are represented.

No Keir, no Kemi, no Farage or Burnham, for that matter. I suppose it is getting close to the summer recess at Parliament.

There have been many great moments at Westminster through the eras, though, perhaps, not as many in recent years.

But the proud heritage of Britain’s cradle of democracy isn't built on great leaders or bad ones, for that matter.

A series of giant dolls houses in ornately carved wood and gold, the hundreds of high and mighty rooms that make up the Houses of Parliament are so steeped in history, it wouldn’t come as a surprise to bump into Disraeli or Pitt the Younger.

No one is allowed to navigate the narrow maze of corridors and stairways that lead to the public gallery until the Speaker and a small procession including the Serjeant at Arms with his giant ceremonial Mace has passed in ordered silence in a a quiet show of ancient pomp and circumstance.

It’s not that the teak brown wood panneling has disappeared entirely but there’s an air of intimacy and informality and modernity, comparatively.

A small forest of microphones is dotted around the chamber to make the MPs audible and digital display screens hang in the air with the order of questions.

If there was any doubt the Commons is not just about a show, it lies in the chamber’s iconic benches for the rank and file of opposing MPs facing each other.

At this distance it’s possible to spot something not visible on the TV news – the green leather upholstery is clearly scuffed and a bit scruffy and torn in places.

There are rules here, mostly conventions rather than anything actually written down.

But, as for airs and graces, of them there are few.

With time running out before the start of PMQs at noon, there is still a distinct lack of big political beasts in the Chamber, though Skipton and Ripon MP Julian Smith, a former Tory Secretary of State for Northern Ireland, has turned up to listen in as the current Labour Northern Ireland minister, Hilary Benn bats away a series of tricky deliveries with a command of his brief and unfailing patience.

When Deputy Prime Minister David Lammy, who is standing in for Sir Keir Starmer, who is otherwise engaged at a NATO summit, arrives in the company of Chancellor of the Exchequer Rachel Reeves, there are no big announcements, no ushering of these household names to a special seat of their own.

Instead, these political heavyweights hover next to the Speaker in his umpire’s chair chatting quietly, waiting for a quiet moment in the Chamber when they can slip in quietly and squeeze into a seat on the bench as fellow MPs budge up.

Already, before a word has been spoken in PMQs, there is a sense that the House of Commons is where the action happens.

This is part of the reason why the Harrogate Advertiser has been invited here by Harrogate and Knaresborough MP Tom Gordon.

It’s Wednesday, the day of Prime Minister's Questions.

A few weeks earlier Harrogate’s MP had spoken directly to Sir Keir Starmer across the floor on the same occasion, making an emotional admission about his own health condition.

The Lib Dem MP is hoping to make a contribution this time, too, though he hasn’t secured a spot on the Order Paper in advance.

So far, proceedings have oozed good manners and, mostly, good sense, whatever your own political beliefs.

The traditional pantomime of the event only kicks in when Lammy faces James Cleverly, the Shadow Secretary of State for Housing, Communities and Local Government who is standing in for his party leader Kemi Badenoch.

All the “The Honourable Member Fors” in the world can’t hide disguise the fact that what follows is the usual yah boo sucks, you’re wrong, I’m right adversarial type of politics centred on the early release of criminals that gets the place a bad name.

Neither side lands a knockout blow and good sense only returns when backbenchers from all sides get their brief turn in the limelight with the chance to ask a question.

I can see Harrogate and Knaresborough MP Tom Gordon bobbing up and down in a corner of the Chamber, hoping the Speaker will spot him and call his name.

But he’s not alone and the Speaker can only do so much.

Eventually it becomes obvious that the ever-busy Harrogate’s MP focus will have to turn instead to a series of meetings to come this afternoon of a serious nature.

As time winds down to the end of the half hour or so of this weekly tradition, it also becomes apparent that there are characteristics of behaviour in the Commons that transcend the wide range of political divides on the benches below.

A general lack of tolerance of dull, ill-informed or simplistic statements.

A readiness to find funny anything that is funny, no matter who says it.

A tendency for the mood of MPs in the House of Commons to switch the moment something interests them.

The atmosphere changes from boredom to rapt attention the instant something important happens.

In that sense, the Chamber’s judgement is impeccable.

When David Lammy suddenly changes the subject to announce a conditional pardon for Ruth Ellis, the last woman to be hanged in the UK, the Commons falls silent.

The second that is over it's back to normal.

Hearteningly, if the House of Commons is a ‘lived in’ democracy, it’s a truly British one.
