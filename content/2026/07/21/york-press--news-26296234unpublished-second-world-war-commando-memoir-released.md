## New memoir reveals life of an Army Commando during the Second World War

Martin Ferrey Cameronian uniform *(Image: Supplied)*

A previously unpublished World War II memoir has hit the shelves.

Helion has released "Soldier of Misfortune: Memoirs of an Army Commando, 1940–1945," offering a unique perspective on the war from the archives of the Cameronians (Scottish Rifles).

The memoir, penned by Martin Ferrey, provides a vivid account of military life, marked by absurdity, terror, and dark humour.

Ferrey, a civilian turned volunteer soldier in 1940, found himself navigating the strange world of the wartime British Army.

His journey took him from a bewildered recruit at Chelsea Barracks through Scots Guards training and Sandhurst, eventually serving in No. 9 Commando in the Italian and Adriatic campaigns.

Ferrey's account is noted for its human touch, marked by self-deprecation, intelligence, and rare honesty.

His narrative captures the transformation of civilians into soldiers, and the psychological toll of war.

The memoir darkens as it progresses, detailing Commando training in the Scottish Highlands, amphibious operations, Albania, Anzio, and the savage fighting around Mount Ornito.

His descriptions of exhaustion, fear, and battlefield chaos starkly contrast with the heroic simplicity often associated with the Commandos.

Ferrey's encounters with figures like Lord Mountbatten and Evelyn Waugh, though fascinating, are secondary to his personal observations of ordinary servicemen.

The memoir is illustrated with photographs from family collections, regimental archives, and wartime sources, providing a broader context to Ferrey's experiences.
