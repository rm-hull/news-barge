Pauline Hanson says Trump had 'a great way' of dealing with migrants

Pauline Hanson says she thinks Donald Trump’s approach to migration was “a great way” of dealing with what she sees as people “using all these ploys to stay in the country”.

Hanson was speaking at a Perth event this morning. Over the course of an hour or so, she reiterated many of her claims about migration and other matters, such as her relationship with Gina Rinehart, which Guardian readers will be familiar with from our previous reporting.

On migration, Hanson said if she came to power she’d “work with the departments” who might be able to identify people overstaying their visas.

Then she said:

I tell you what I do like. Trump did this – Trump said OK, all those in the country illegally, I’ll actually give you a one-way ticket out of the country. And he said, then you can apply to come back in some time later. But if you don’t go, he said, and we find you, you’ll never get back into the country again … I think that’s a great way of doing it.

Australian athletes urged to pack extra undies for Commonwealth Games

Australians athletes are being urged to fiercely compete like warriors at the Commonwealth Games, rather than go commando.

AUS HQ delivers services aplenty for the Australian Team, with laundry being one.

Rising robbery risk to Australian cash transfers, inquiry hears

Cash transporters have warned their crews are at rising risk of being robbed as unions say some million-dollar shipments are being driven around by lone workers in small, unmarked, un-armoured cars.

A Senate inquiry into reforms to cash distribution has heard from the chiefs of Armaguard, the cash moving company. Matt Caulfield, its chief executive, told the inquiry the company took more than 8,000 jobs each day, moving millions of dollars between them, and some of its jobs were being done by single, unarmed people in un-armoured vehicles. He said:

Just in this last three months, so we’ve had two armed robberies … We have what’s called a tracking system called Myosh that looks at near misses and red flags. So they’re at the highest they’ve been in my time at Armaguard.

Gavin Webb, from the Transport Workers’ Union, told the inquiry two workers had been robbed and assaulted at a south-east Queensland shopping centre in May, and cash-in-transit workers had historically been killed during cash drops in public places. He said:

We have seen single-crew operators carting millions of dollars of cash in vehicles such as Hyundai i30s … in major cities around Australia, in those unmarked vehicles.

Webb said the limited staff protection was due to banks choosing cheaper cash movers with poor safety standards. Proper protection is costly: Bill Kelty, the director of Armaguard, said its secure vehicles typically cost $300,000 each.

Armaguard and the union are both warning that the reforms being considered by the inquiry are not accounting for the cost of operating safely. Cash distribution has become more expensive and major operators are disagreeing with retailers and banks over how the costs should be covered.

Armaguard’s executive chair, Peter Linfox, said the company’s owners, the Linfox family and Spanish former competitor Prosegur, had paid $125m propping up Armaguard’s cash distribution losses. Its owners did not expect to be paid any profits for years, Linfox said.

A US judge has signed off on artificial intelligence company Anthropic’s landmark $US1.5bn settlement of a class action lawsuit brought by a group of authors who accused it of misusing their books to train its AI chatbot Claude.

US district judge Araceli Martinez-Olguin granted final approval of the settlement, the largest known settlement of a US copyright case, rejecting arguments that it was too small.

The case is one of dozens brought by copyright owners including authors and news outlets against tech companies over the training of their large language models, and the first major US case to settle.

A number of prominent Australian writers were involved. A searchable list of books used to train the AI includes six Michelle de Kretser books, Anna Funder’s All That I Am, Hannah Kent’s Burial Rites, and two Gerald Murnanes – to name a few.

Anthropic deputy general counsel Aparna Sridhar said in a statement:

We reached this settlement in 2025, after the court’s landmark ruling that training AI on books is fair use under copyright law – which remains the law today.

We are pleased that more than 91 per cent of authors and publishers covered by the settlement have claimed their share of the payment, and we’re looking forward to bringing this matter to a close.

Son of Holocaust survivors describes shock at Sydney Opera House protest

The royal commission on antisemitism has heard from Dr George Foster OAM, a member of the South Sydney synagogue and president of the Australian Association of Jewish Holocaust Survivors.

Foster, who was born in Budapest to two Hungarian Holocaust survivors shortly after the end of the second world war, arriving in Australia in 1947, has described feeling shocked by a protest on the steps of the Sydney Opera House on 9 October 2023:

We heard expletives and you know vilification of Jews to a point which shocked me, and I know much, if not all, of the Jewish community. I never thought that I would see such a thing in this country.

Foster has described an incident in which the South Sydney synagogue was among several firebombed after the first Iraq war. He said it was easier for the South Sydney synagogue to recover from the event during the 1990s.

At that time, again we received many messages of support and assistance, and there wasn’t the overt vilification of the Jewish people then, and I think because of that it was easier for us to come to terms with and move forward after that incident.

Foster has described several subsequents incidents at the South Sydney synagogue, including a petrol bombing in 1993, swastikas being painted and bricks being thrown through windows in 1995, and an arson attack in 2003. In 2025, after the synagogue was allegedly vandalised with swastikas, the synagogue erected a security fence, which was completed four days after the Bondi attack.

Interfaith relations after Bondi attack have broken down, royal commission hears

The chief rabbi of Sydney’s Central Synagogue, Levi Wolff, has told the royal commission on antisemitism and social cohesion that interfaith relations renewed following the Bondi attack have broken down.

He said that previously good relations had “chilled” following October 2023. He said while Jewish community leaders had had a constructive meeting with leaders from the Islamic community following the December attack, relations had returned to their previous state following the visit by the president, Isaac Herzog, in February:

Relationships have broken down since. We’ve always tried to reengage that, and we hope that we can in the future.

The royal commissioner, Virginia Bell, has asked Wolff, who was born in the US but came to Sydney in 2001, if conversations with his family in New York show “that even if there’s been a spike in antisemitism there, there hasn’t been the same degree of distress for members of the community?”

Wolff said:

Yes, there seems to be something which stands out about what’s happening here almost more than anywhere else. It could also be to the fact that it is of such a shock. You know, Australia was just, it’s always looked at as this paradise so far away.

Wolff noted that Australia had become home to the highest number per capita of Holocaust survivors outside Israel.

He ended his submission by calling for “three pillars” to respond to antisemitism: “security, legislation and education”. He said education about antisemitism should go beyond the Holocaust, saying it “should encompass how Jews live, not only how Jews died”.

Queensland government to spend similar on CFMEU inquiry as CCC

The Queensland government will spend more on the investigation of a single union as the annual budget for the state’s Crime and Corruption Commission.

The attorney general, Deb Frecklington, and the treasurer, David Janetzki, announced that the state would budget $95m for the state’s CFMEU inquiry, an additional $75m than first budgeted.

The Crime and Corruption Commission is responsible for investigating complaints against all state government bodies including the police; administers the state’s witness protection program; recovers the proceeds of crime; and combats major crime. It has an $82.1m budget this year.

The commission investigates fewer than 1% of complaints referred to it, according to its reporting to parliament. It charged a single person with a corruption offence last year.

Asked whether the lack of investigation was related to budgetary constraints, Frecklington said:

I don’t know whether that’s true. I think I’d like to see your evidence for that one.

Frecklington compared the CFMEU inquiry – Queensland’s version of a royal commission – to the Fitzgerald inquiry, which caused the early resignation and unsuccessful prosecution of the state’s longest-serving premier, four ministers and a police commissioner to be jailed for corruption, led to reforms to the state’s voting system, the creation of the an independent anti-corruption commission and is the subject of several books and films.

The inquiry was initially scheduled to run until October but was extended by another 18 months in May. It will now run about six months longer than Fitzgerald’s inquiry.

KPMG appoints lifer as new Australia CEO amid leaks scandal

Luca Ittimani

KPMG has made a lifelong staff member and top executive its new Australian chief executive, rejecting external candidates, as it grapples with its audit leaks scandal.

John Sams will take over after Andrew Yates resigned as CEO and took accountability for the firm’s failure to address staff breaches of client confidentiality.

Sams started his career as a graduate at KPMG UK in 2003, moving to Australia in 2006. He has been a partner at the firm for over a decade, was elevated to chief financial officer in October 2025 and replaced KPMG’s chief operating officer, Eileen Hoggett, after she resigned in June for her role in the leaks.

KPMG Australia said candidates from KPMG Australia, the firm’s international branches and outside the firm were considered for the position, announcing Sams’ appointment on Tuesday.

The firm earlier this month replaced its other headline resignation, its chair, with an internal candidate: independent director Michael Ebeid. Ebeid sat on the firm’s CEO selection panel and cheered Sams’ appointment on Tuesday. He said:

John has a clear mandate from the Board: to strengthen leadership and culture, improve confidence with our people, clients, regulators, government and the Parliament, and focus KPMG on the areas where it can make the greatest contribution.

Sams said he was ready to take “tough decisions” to put KPMG “on the right path”. He said:

The firm fell short of the standards rightly expected of us, and the accountability for these failures will continue to be implemented … Despite everything we have faced so far, I am optimistic about the future. Otherwise I would never have taken on this role. I will lead the changes that will make us better, whatever that takes.

Rabbi tells royal commission of ‘almost daily’ threats and abuse after Bondi attack

Penry Buckley

The chief rabbi of the Central Synagogue in Sydney, Levi Wolff, has told the royal commission he receives “almost daily” threats and abuse after his public profile was raised after the Bondi terror attack.

Wolff, whose synagogue is about a kilometre from Bondi beach, has told the inquiry that Australian Jews “feel that they’re being asked to justify their place in a country to which they have contributed loyally and peacefully and constructively for generations.”

He has described an incident after an attack in which a member of the community had anonymously posted him a mezuzah, a box with a scroll with Hebrew verses, normally affixed to the entrance of Jewish homes, “with a note with it that said: ‘I am afraid to have this’”:

‘I’m afraid to have this on my door. Please pass it on to somebody who is brave enough and who needs it.’ That note has stayed with me … No one should have to have courage to simply place a symbol of their faith on the doorpost of their very own home.

Wolff said that after the Bondi attack, “my public profile has increased significantly and, with that, the hatred that has been directed towards me has also increased significantly”:

Almost daily, I receive messages of abuse and threats of violence, and initially those messages actually shocked me. But what shocks me now is something else. I have become accustomed to it. I have become accustomed to threats of violence. That should disturb every Australian.

Nine newspapers to cut 30 roles in further redundancies

Amanda Meade

Staff numbers at the Sydney Morning Herald and the Age will be cut by 30 in another round of redundancies announced by Nine’s publishing arm today.

The managing director of publishing, Tory Maguire, called staff to a town hall meeting at 10.30am on Tuesday to unveil what she said was structural change in a digital-first era.

Maguire said in an email seen by Guardian Australia:

The best way to protect our quality journalism is to ensure our organisation adapts to a changing media landscape, that we’re growing commercially and investing in new areas which connect with new audiences.

In particular we need to keep shifting towards the digital-first future.

Marco Rubio expected to join Penny Wong at Asean conference

The US secretary of state, Marco Rubio, was scheduled to land in Manila on Tuesday morning for the Asean conference, joining the Australian foreign affairs minister, Penny Wong, and their international counterparts, as the rekindled war with Iran is placing traditional American allies under economic strain.

To give some international context to the talks, the import-dependent Philippines declared a national energy emergency in March and has been forced to cast an ever-wider net for fuel, including purchases of oil from Russia, whose foreign affairs minister, SergeiLavrov, will arrive on Tuesday as well.

Before departing from Washington on Monday, Rubio said he was open to the possibility of sideline meetings with both Lavrov and China’s top diplomat, Wang Yi, who Beijing only officially confirmed would attend on Monday.

A meeting with Wang would come days after the US president, Donald Trump, accused Beijing of meddling in US elections.

The week is also expected to see renewed efforts towards regional energy security, including the ongoing push for ratification of the bloc’s Framework Agreement on Petroleum Security fuel-sharing scheme.

In May, members signed off on a push for an Asean power grid, aimed at connecting national electricity networks to enable cross-border power trading.

Rubio will meet the Philippines president, Ferdinand Marcos Jr, as part of a strengthened US partnership with the Philippines, with the disputed South China Sea certain to be on the agenda. Chinese and Philippine ships regularly clash in the crucial waterway, which Beijing claims nearly in its entirety, despite an international ruling that its assertion has no legal basis.

The Philippines has previously said it hoped to conclude a code of conduct between Asean and China before ending its year as the bloc’s rotating chair but analysts have suggested any deal Beijing would be willing to sign would be toothless.

Synagogue unsuccessful in application for security grant prior to arson attack

Penry Buckley

A Melbourne synagogue was unsuccessful in applications for government grants for security before a December 2024 arson attack that has seen its doors closed ever since.

The royal commission’s fifth block of hearings, which are being held in Melbourne this week, is examining security arrangements for the Jewish community.

Yesterday, counsel assisting, Richard Lancaster SC, said evidence would show “a substantial and ongoing threat” to the security of the Jewish community, leading to a “shrinking of Jewish communal life”.

Today’s first witness is Benjamin Klein, a board member of the Adass Israel synagogue, the subject of an arson attack in December 2024. Asked about applications for grant funding for security in 2023 and 2024 before the attack, he said:

There were letters sent out to both government, local members, to different parts of the Jewish of the community seeking grants. It was local council, there was state government, there were federal grants, which we saw were available.

We’d sent out letters and were unsuccessful. We also sent out a letter to Mark Dreyfus at the time, who was the attorney general, and we weren’t successful. There were technicalities in terms of incorporated entities, unincorporated entities. There was a lot of red tape to sort of get through and we weren’t successful.

The commission has heard that the synagogue’s board has since had to employ someone to oversee funding applications for security grants. After the attack, the federal government has pledged up to $30m to rebuild the synagogue.

Qantas apologises to passengers stuck on tarmac for more than four hours

Qantas has apologised to about 250 passengers who were stuck on the tarmac in Sydney for four-and-a-half hours on Monday afternoon while the airline dealt with multiple separate aircraft issues.

Flight QF43 to Denpasar had been scheduled to leave at 4.20pm yesterday.

A passenger on the flight called ABC Radio Sydney last night, after four hours of waiting on the tarmac, saying:

They’ve come back with multiple messages and they’ve constantly been changing throughout, the time frame, to the point where a lot of people are not really sure now what’s going on and what is the truth and are we actually going to get off the ground? But there’s been some engineers in and out for hours trying to work out what’s going on with the plane, and now we’re just being told they’re about to shut the computers down and reboot them.

A Qantas spokesperson told Guardian Australia that the flight was delayed initially due to a refuelling issue but then engineers identified a separate issue that required assessment and resulted in further delays.

The spokesperson said:

We understand this was a frustrating experience for our customers and sincerely apologise for this inconvenience. We’re reviewing this incident and acknowledge that our communication could have been better in this instance.

The flight departed for Denpasar at 8.50pm.
