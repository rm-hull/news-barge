Australia news live: Wong pushes Chinese counterpart on submarine missile launch in face-to-face Manila meeting

Australia’s foreign affairs minister discussed the importance of peace in the Pacific at her meeting with Wang Yi in the wings of Asean. Follow today’s news live

The foreign affairs minister, Penny Wong, met her Chinese counterpart, Wang Yi, on the sidelines of the Asean foreign ministers’ meeting in Manila last night, a statement from her department said.

In their 12th meeting, the ministers discussed Asean priorities and the importance of peace and stability in the Indo-Pacific.

Wong raised Australia’s concerns about China’s test of a nuclear-capable submarine-launched missile earlier this month – and noted the strong concerns expressed by many Pacific island countries.

Wong also raised a range of consular issues including the case of Dr Yang Jun, respect for Australia’s laws, sovereignty and freedom of expression, the importance of safe and professional maritime conduct, and human rights.

Barnaby can do almost anything. Nothing would surprise me – if he went back to the National party, especially now that David Littleproud is not the leader – I don’t have any inside information, but nothing would surprise me if this continues with some of these less mainstream comments being made.

Shorten and Pyne cast doubt on One Nation's staying power

Bill Shorten and Christopher Pyne have had their disagreements in the past, but they both agreed last night that voters will not turn out for One Nation at the next election at the levels suggested by recent polls.

Appearing on ABC’s 7.30, the Labor and Liberal veterans were asked about the impact of more scrutiny on the party, Pauline Hanson’s controversial trip to the UK, and possible tensions with Barnaby Joyce.

Shorten, a former Labor leader, said:

I don’t think [voters] are happy with the system, but I don’t think she should assume her 30% of people who say they want to support One Nation are the same as her 6% to 7% rusted-on hardline true believer vote of the past.

Some of the people who are looking to her now are sick of the system, and they’re hurting, but I think they would be perturbed by dusting off the bones of a White Australia policy.
