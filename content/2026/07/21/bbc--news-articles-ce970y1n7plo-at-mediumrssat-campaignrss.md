**Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar is set to stand down to become a minister in Andy Burnham's government.**

It would mean Sarwar leaving the Scottish Parliament and his leader role, then taking up a seat in the House of Lords.

Sarwar publicly called for Sir Keir Starmer to resign in February, provoking a political near-death experience for the then prime minister.

It is thought Sarwar, who has led the Scottish Labour Party since 2021, will become a business minister.

He was at the event in London last Friday when Burnham became the new Labour Party leader.

Scottish Labour won just 17 seats out of 129 in the Scottish Parliament elections in May.

Andy Burnham still has a raft of ministerial appointments to make

At the time Sarwar promised to stay on as the leader of the party in Scotland but his appointment as a minister at Westminster is expected to create a leadership vacancy.

There wouldn't, though, be a by-election for his Holyrood seat as he was elected via the regional list.

As of last year, new rules prevent politicians from sitting at both Westminster and Holyrood at the same time.

In May, as reported by The Herald, external, when asked if he would ever take up a seat in the House of Lords, Sarwar said: "No. I'm focused on being first minister of this great country."

He added: "My only commitment, my only focus, is serving my constituents in Glasgow and serving the great people of this country, if I get the honour of being first minister."

After Sir Keir's resignation in June, when asked if he planned to serve as party leader for the full five-year Holyrood term he said: "That is my intention.

"I'm hungry for the fight ahead and I want to play my full part in making sure this country never ends up in the hands of Nigel Farage and Reform."

When Burnham became prime minister on Monday, Sarwar hailed him for his "bold Labour vision".

Former Greater Manchester mayor Burnham appointed his cabinet on Monday night, but a raft of ministerial appointments are still to come.

BBC Scotland Westminster correspondent Paris Gourtsoyannis said Scottish Labour parliamentarians appeared to have been blindsided by the news.

An MSP called Sarwar's decision "bizarre", while a senior Scottish Labour MP said it was "absolutely mad. I cannot begin to fathom the logic of it."

As a Scottish Labour peer, Sarwar would be entitled to attend meetings of the Scottish PLP at Westminster - a group which earlier this year collectively refused to back their leader's call for Keir Starmer to resign as prime minister.

The SNP's deputy leader Keith Brown said: "This is a terrible look for Andy Burnham who waxed lyrical about a new politics and devolving power - he's just pulled the most undemocratic trick in the Westminster book.

"Anas Sarwar led Scottish Labour to their worst ever election defeat, but as is always the case with the Labour Party, there's always a cushty number in the House of Lords.

"What a signal to send after their democratic defeat - this is the Labour Party literally lording it over the people of Scotland."

## 'Voters betrayed'

Reform UK's Scotland deputy leader Thomas Kerr said: "Only weeks ago, Anas Sarwar stood before the people of Glasgow asking for their trust and insisting that his future was here in Scotland. Today, those same voters will rightly feel betrayed.

"Throughout the election campaign, he dismissed suggestions that he had ambitions elsewhere and claimed his focus was on serving Scotland.

"Now, at the first opportunity, he has walked away from the very people who placed their faith in him."

Jim Murphy, who was Scottish Labour leader from 2014 to 2015, told BBC News: "If it's confirmed I think it's good news for him [Sarwar] personally but it probably, on balance, strengthens the government.

"It will open up a vacancy, of course, for leader of the Scottish Labour Party."

He said Andy Burnham had a maximum three years until the next election and had appointed people he felt could deliver quickly.

"He's come to a judgement that Anas, as part of a wider team, can do that," he said.

"That will be difficult for him [Sarwar]. He will have to get up to speed very quickly but congratulations to him."

Murphy added that there was turbulence whenever there was a leader change within a party.

"I don't enjoy it," he said.

"A good group of really talented people were removed from government yesterday but there are always more talented people than jobs available, so Andy will have to work pretty hard delivering what he promised - which is uniting the Labour tribe to enable them to take on Reform and others."
