Concerns have been raised about the emergency response to a 19-year-old York student who drowned in a river in the city centre.

Evan D’Andilly drowned after entering the River Ouse from Ouse Bridge four days before his 20th birthday on March 5, 2025, an inquest heard.

  Ouse Bridge in York 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

The hearing was told Evan, a University of York business management student from Scunthorpe, had been on a night out with friends in the city centre when he entered the water at around 3.03am.

Evan tried to stay afloat in the freezing river for 20 minutes as emergency services worked to save him.

  Evan D’Andilly 

*(Image: North Yorkshire Police)*

In CCTV footage played in court, Evan could be heard calling for help as police officers stood on the riverside shouting advice.

Police arrived within two to three minutes, and an officer threw a life ring into the water to reach Evan, who was in the middle of the 41-metre-wide river, without success.

Evan’s mother, Lorraine D’Andilly, told a previous inquest hearing the available riverside rescue equipment – 18-metre reach poles and 25-metre throw lines – were unsuitable and had no chance of reaching him, meaning "Evan lost a substantial chance of survival".

  Evan D’Andilly 

*(Image: Family)*

York fire crews were notified at 3.09am, with Acomb and York appliances arriving by 3.14am and 3.18am.

But the inquest heard that the fire service’s rescue boat was in Selby and did not arrive until 3.37am – 14 minutes after the last sighting of Evan.

Volunteer-run York Rescue Boat, moored in King’s Staith next to where Evan was in the water, was the nearest vessel capable of a water rescue and could reach locations quickly, but Ms D’Andilly said the charity boat was not immediately called.

The inquest heard police called for the York Rescue Boat at about 3.10am and it arrived at 3.22am – one minute before Evan was last seen above the water.

In police body cam footage played in court, PC Grace Sylvester, who shouted advice to Evan as he tried to stay above the water, said: “It’s a city river. Why is it taking this long [for a rescue boat to arrive]?”

She was next to the York Rescue Boat as Evan was in the water and said: “We just wished we had access to that.”

Asked by Ms D’Andilly if the rescue equipment was not fit for purpose, PC Sylvester said: “On occasions it’s worked. On occasions, it hasn’t worked.”

Sergeant Daniel Stubbs, who was the acting police inspector on the night of Evan’s death, told the court both York Rescue Boat and the fire service’s rescue boat had been requested to attend the scene.

But he said he was unaware that the fire service’s rescue boat was not in York.

The inquest heard that North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue relies on the York Rescue Boat for water rescues in York, as the fire service’s nearest boats are in Selby and Malton.

Ms D’Andilly questioned why York Rescue Boat was not called immediately by the fire service when it was told about the incident, rather than when Evan had been in the water for 10 minutes.

She said her son had “fought for another 10 minutes” to stay above the water which extended the time he could be rescued.

“He did everything he could to survive,” Ms D’Andilly said, adding: “I will never understand how a safety protocol can be so flawed.”

Ms D’Andilly described her son as "kind" with a "gentle nature” and said his death was “incredibly senseless” and a “tragedy”.

“I still struggle to believe that Evan is gone from this world – that I will never see him again,” she said. “It’s hard to believe that my little water baby died from downing.”

Ms D’Andilly said her son was “one in a million”, adding: “He is loved so much and we will miss him every second of every day.”

Evan’s father, Gordon D’Andilly, described his son as someone “full of life” who “got on with everyone really well”.

The inquest heard Evan struggled with his mental health and had previously attempted suicide, but Mr D’Andilly said he saw his son the week before his death and he was in a “good place”.

“Ev was everything,” Mr D’Andilly said. “He was such a good boy. He was a thoughtful boy and so funny.”

A North Yorkshire Police spokesperson said: “The death of Evan D’Andilly is tragic and our thoughts remain with his family and friends. North Yorkshire Police will fully support the inquest, and we will await the conclusion before commenting further.”

The inquest, at Northallerton Coroners’ Court, continues.
