You can’t say that prosecco isn’t popular. Some 667 million bottles of the sparkling (or in some cases semi-sparkling) wine from Italy were sold globally in 2025 at a cost of more than £3bn. And while the largest export market is the US, the UK remains the second most important with 36 per cent of the global consumption.

You can see why so many people like it. It’s light, fruity, refreshing, has slightly less alcohol than champagne and, above all, is relatively inexpensive. Some will also say that prosecco is actually good for you. The natural trace elements it contains – zinc, magnesium and potassium – can boost your mood, while polyphenols, the plant chemicals from red and white grapes found in prosecco, increase the availability of nitric oxide which controls blood pressure.

Others may complain that it’s too sweet. This may be because the labelling of the wine can be confusing. If it is called “extra dry” that means there will be some sweetness there. If you want a prosecco that is less sweet and drier, then look for those labelled “brut”. There are also two sets of initials to note. DOCG or Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita signifies superior quality, the following strict rules during production and government approval. DOC (Denominazione di Origine Controllata) is the next level and guarantees that the wine comes from a specific geographical area. Both classifications should guarantee excellent quality.

So, with a huge range of prosecco available from supermarket shelves to specialist wine stores, there are bottles to suit every taste. Here we look at the best examples of this party favourite.

- **Best** prosecco overall

Valdobbiadene is the name to remember. Believed to derive from the Latin “vallis ad bene" or “valley of goodness”, it’s the area in Italy’s Veneto region where the finest proseccos are to be found. Made exclusively from the hand-picked glera grape, this example has earned the select DOCG status, the highest classification for Italian wines, signifying superior quality, the observance of strict production rules and government-mandated approval.

![Della Vite Valdobbiadene Superiore Prosecco DOCG best proseccos IndyBest]([https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/17/16/38/Della-Vite-Valdobbiadene-Superiore-Prosecco-DOCG-best-proseccos-IndyBest.png?quality=75&width=608&auto=webp](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/17/16/38/Della-Vite-Valdobbiadene-Superiore-Prosecco-DOCG-best-proseccos-IndyBest.png?quality=75&width=608&auto=webp) 608w)

Extra dry, which in the slightly confusing world of wine-speak actually means that there’s a little sweetness there; it’s also crisp, fruity and elegant with a delicate mousse and that easy-drinking, happy valley appeal.

Another Valdobbiadene prosecco with a DOCG rating made from glera grapes from a vineyards nestled between the steep local hills. Rumour has it, that the vineyards were so high up and inaccessible that by the time the grapes were eventually picked they had become over-ripe, which was a good thing as it resulted in wines, like this one, with huge, concentrated apricot and peach flavours tempered by notes of blossom.

- **Best** free-run prosecco

Free run? Well, it’s nothing to with pounding pavements in your shorts and trainers but everything to do with creating what some call “the purest expression of wine”. What it means, simply, is not pressing the grapes but letting gravity do the job instead. The natural free-run juice then goes on to result in an elegant and fresh sparkling wine that escapes the bitter compounds that could have come its way. Lots of ripe fruit flavours here with zesty apple and ripe peach doing the heavy lifting.
