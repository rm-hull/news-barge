### TechRadar Verdict

Splatoon Raiders is a wonderful solo reinvention of the popular multiplayer series filled with smartly designed missions and endlessly engaging progression systems. Underpinned by the franchise’s fantastic shooting mechanics, it’s a fresh and exciting adventure that should be in every Nintendo Switch 2 owner’s library.

#### Pros

- +Satisfying progression systems 
- +Shooting feels fantastic 
- +Smartly designed levels 
- +Loads of weapon types 
- +Meaningful upgrades with plenty of build variety 
- +Visually appealing, with wonderful character designs 

#### Cons

- -Can feel a tad grindy 

Why you can trust TechRadar

If you’ve always loved the world of the *Splatoon* series but have been — understandably — put off by the prospect of its sweaty competitive multiplayer modes, then this latest Nintendo Switch 2 exclusive third-person shooter is for you.

**Platform reviewed:** Nintendo Switch 2**Available on:** Nintendo Switch 2**Release date:** July 23, 2026

*Splatoon Raiders* offers an incredibly compelling, single-player take on the series’ trademark ink-spewing action. It’s all underpinned by the same excellent shooting mechanics that make online matches of *Splatoon 3***such a blast, now smartly recontextualized in a wholly player versus environment (PvE) format and enhanced with a plethora of satisfying progression systems that allow you to experiment with countless impactful character builds.

The sheer volume of content here is formidable: there are a vast number of highly replayable missions that can be tackled in multiple difficulty levels, more than a hundred weapons to wield, and even lend others a hand in online co-op for more unlockable rewards. This is comfortably one of the best Nintendo Switch 2 games and I know I’ll be coming back to it, even now I’ve seen the credits roll.

## Fishy business

 ![A Splatoon Raiders screenshot taken on Nintendo Switch 2 in handheld mode.](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/FGFu6Lmo6MrkpFDoe7hrjQ.jpg) 


While the main *Splatoon* games are known for their clean urban aesthetics, *Splatoon Raiders* sets itself apart with a much grimier, more salvaged look. Set on the distant Spirhalite Islands, it follows an unnamed mechanic working for the Deep Cut trio — the iconic TV show hosts from *Splatoon 3* who apparently moonlight as treasure hunters. With the group stranded on a raft after their latest treasure hunting expedition goes awry, they’re forced to improvise gear and track down the resources necessary to escape.

Fashioned from wooden crates and metal grates, the raft functions as your hub world in between missions and can be outfitted with a range of useful features like a customizable firing range and a range of upgrade stations, in addition to more whimsical options like an arcade cabinet that offers some cute pixel-art minigames. You always feel like there’s a meaningful upgrade that you’re working towards, and watching your craft develop from a ramshackle structure into a fully-fledged mobile base as you progress through the story is immensely satisfying.

That’s not to say that you need much encouragement to complete missions, however, as they’re a load of fun. There are three main types: quickfire dens that have you beating a few waves of foes in a short time limit, longer raids where you drop into large semi-open environments to secure nearby treasure chests, and dungeons that challenge you to get to a goal with a set loadout.

 ![A Splatoon Raiders screenshot taken on Nintendo Switch 2 in handheld mode.](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/m27Nc7ePY2o2mKfzKJmcfQ.jpg) 


**I love the character creator inSplatoon Raiders. It lets you choose between an Inkling or Octoling protagonist, with customizable facial features and a range of cute hairstyles that are perfect for expressing yourself in co-op. The generous selection of unlockable outfits is lovely too.**

It seems pretty basic at first, but there are an incredible number of unique twists that keep each new mission feeling fresh. A den, for example, might throw hundreds of the same area-denial enemies at you — coating the small arena with noxious slime that chips away at your health and forces you to stay on your feet — while a raid could surprise you with tricky platforming sections and a strict time limit.

## In the tank

 ![A Splatoon Raiders screenshot taken on Nintendo Switch 2 in handheld mode.](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/CfKnQAQHFmRx5i9DAoMxGR.jpg) 


Your character can carry one of three ink tanks (which basically determine your chosen ‘class’), designed for raw power, speed, or more careful ranged tactics. They all play completely differently, with their own roster of powerful gadgets to unlock and an overall power level that gradually ticks up with use.

I mainly relied on the Power Tank, which, true to its name, is all about big, bombastic attacks that deal massive damage. My favorite gadgets include the inky hatchet with its huge slashing strike that is invaluable for taking out tankier foes and a charging rocket launcher that wonderfully shreds through groups of smaller ones.

It might be tempting to rely on one favorite, but there are plenty of good reasons to try the others. Fully upgrading one tank requires you to spend time with the others, and some dungeons even have specific rewards depending on which you use to complete them. I initially thought that this might prove a frustrating barrier to progression, but soon came to appreciate being made to step out of my comfort zone because all three tanks are equally enjoyable to use with a huge variety of gadgets to discover.

 ![A Splatoon Raiders screenshot taken on Nintendo Switch 2 in handheld mode.](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/AuFQS6ghLG5q35Yyq3dxGR.jpg) 


Every gadget is highly customizable as well, with a plethora of collectible parts that do everything from minor stat upgrades to fundamentally change how they work. You can only add a certain number of parts at a time, so you’ll need to think carefully in order to craft a new build to successfully overcome a particularly tricky challenge.

There’s just as much depth to the wide selection of weapons. The 11 types on offer will all be familiar if you’ve played *Splatoon 3*, with a good mix of long-range shooters, nimble pistols, sloshing buckets, and so on, all with their own mechanics to master. Tackling more difficult missions rewards more powerful weapons at a strong pace, which, alongside the steady introduction of new rarities with their own unique qualities, further stops everything from becoming stale.

The same is true of the many enemy types. It’s the same malevolent Salmonids from the Salmon Run side modes of *Splatoon 2***and* 3*, but with a healthy number of new varieties to surprise even the most seasoned fish hunters.

It all comes together for an experience that, even when some of the more difficult challenges force you to grind up your power level, remains fresh and exciting like a delicious piece of sashimi throughout.

## Should I play Splatoon Raiders?

## Play it if...

**You’ve always wanted a single-player Splatoon***Splatoon Raiders* finally delivers the single-player *Splatoon* experience that many have been waiting for, giving you a new way to discover the franchise without the pressure of PvP.

**You want something to play for the long haul**

With its wealth of meaty progression systems, *Splatoon Raiders* gives you loads of goals to work towards. Pick this up if you want a game that you can dip into over and over again.

**You’re after a meaty co-op experience**

Although primarily a single-player experience, *Splatoon Raiders* supports online co-op that lets you tackle the majority of its missions with up to three friends.

## Don't play it if...

**You’re not interested in Splatoon***Splatoon Raiders* is a superb game, but it’s not going to win you over if you already disliked the series’ third-person, ink-based combat.

## Accessibility features

Like most modern Nintendo releases, *Splatoon Raiders* is lacking when it comes to accessibility features. It offers the ability to invert both thumbsticks, or use motion controls to aim.

The color of ink is usually random, but you can lock it to either blue or purple in order to be easier to see. In addition to offering harder versions of most missions, the game features three overall difficulty modes which decreases or increases the power of enemies.

## How I reviewed Splatoon Raiders

 ![A Splatoon Raiders screenshot taken on Nintendo Switch 2 in handheld mode.](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/B8h7YhuEVHNBcd6ynpZoHR.jpg) 


I played nearly 20 hours of *Splatoon Raiders* on Nintendo Switch 2 using the Joy-Con 2 controllers in handheld mode. For audio, I used the system’s built-in speaker setup.

I experienced the whole of the game’s story, hitting the credits having tackled all of the major missions on the standard difficulty mode, in addition to some online co-op play.
