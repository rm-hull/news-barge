More than 1,700 HIV treatment clinics and service sites have closed around the world and at least 16,000 health workers have lost their jobs after sweeping aid cuts by Donald Trump disrupted the world’s largest AIDS/HIV prevention programme, despite a US pledge to protect lifesaving care.

The cuts have caused “severe and devastating” disruption to dozens of countries around the world, according to new research – with experts warning that the cuts and wider changes to US policy risk reversing decades of progress in the fight to end the global AIDS epidemic by 2030.

Prevention programmes have also been devastated as spending has fallen by more than half, and even organisations that retained US funding were forced to stop providing services to key populations, such as sex workers.

The findings lay out, for the first time, the impact of the disruptions to the President’s Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR) around the world. Researchers warned that the damage could be long-lasting.

“What we saw is that no part of PEPFAR was untouched by these cuts, and that includes areas that the administration intended to protect,” said Elise Lankiewicz, a policy associate at amfAR, the Foundation for AIDS Research, which carried out the survey. The research is due to be presented next week at the 26th International Aids Conference in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.

Although the Trump administration had pledged to protect HIV treatment services and the prevention of mother-to-child transmission from its foreign aid cuts, 63 per cent of organisations reported disruptions to those services.

Spending on prevention also fell by over half in a year, while funding for condoms and lubricants was almost completely cut. Overall, 43 per cent of organisations permanently stopped at least one prevention activity, risking decades of progress against HIV.

"It’s like bailing out a sinking ship without actually patching the hole,” said Ms Lankiewicz. “You can’t cut out certain awards without seeing a ripple effect throughout PEPFAR. What’s left is a model that’s heavily focused on treatment, which I think is absolutely necessary, but insufficient for controlling the HIV response.”

US President Donald Trump slashed foreign assistance after his return to office last year, dismantling the US Agency for International Development (USAID) and terminating thousands of overseas aid awards.

PEPFAR, launched by President George W. Bush in 2003, was credited with saving more than 26 million lives through HIV treatment and prevention programmes in more than 50 countries, but many of the organisations delivering those services have now lost funding or seen payments delayed due to the cuts.

Health systems were particularly impacted by the cuts and the HIV response is “an interconnected web of partners and providers” so it’s difficult to “cut one piece without seeing impacts elsewhere”, Ms Lankiewicz said.

Researchers warned that they found almost no organisations were able to replace lost US funding, while domestic governments often lacked the resources to take over services, with key populations often criminalised.

“These are the populations most at risk of HIV, which in many places are sex workers, gay men, trans people and people who use drugs,” said Lankiewicz.

Nearly three-quarters of organisations serving those most at risk ended at least one service for those groups.

"We really can’t achieve epidemic control just ignoring these populations," she said. “From a data-driven epidemic response perspective, I think that’s going to have a huge impact and is at great risk of being lost from many countries.”

The Trump administration has issued executive orders aimed at ending federal support for what it called “gender ideology”, alongside broader efforts to eliminate diversity, equity and inclusion initiatives. Aid organisations say those policies have led to the withdrawal or restriction of funding for some programmes serving transgender people and other LGBTQ+ communities.

The report also found locally-based organisations were significantly more likely than international organisations to lose funding and close service sites, despite longstanding US efforts to shift HIV programmes towards local ownership.

The effects have been particularly evident across much of eastern and southern Africa, where governments focused on maintaining treatment after the cuts but scaled back community HIV testing, prevention programmes and peer support workers that help bring people into care.

These services are critical to controlling the epidemic because they help identify new infections and link people to care.

*The Independent*’s Bel Trew reported recently on the world of “kito” attacks in Nigeria: where __members of the LGBTQ+ community are catfished online__, kidnapped, tortured and extorted by gangs in a rising surge in violence and hatred.

Amid unprecedented aid cuts, survivors also contend with the collapse of the very clinics and shelters that once helped them.

“This is the time to make decisions,” said Ms Lankiewicz. “What we target with the funding that’s left is going to shape what the next couple of years look like.”
