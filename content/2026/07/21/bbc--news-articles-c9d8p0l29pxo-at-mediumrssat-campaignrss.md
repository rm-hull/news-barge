Construction of the Digital Campus in Manchester will not be completed before 2032

**Civil servantsworking for government departmentsbased in London have been offered relocation packages to encourage them to move to the North of England, the BBC understands.**

It comes as the first details are emerging about the new No 10 North which has already started work, according to sources close to new Prime Minister Andy Burnham.

The BBC has been told that the Northern branch of Downing Street will be overseen by cabinet minister and ally of Burnham, Louise Haigh.

Haigh, who is the first secretary of state, is thought to have been asked to lead a new Office for the Prime Minister and the Cabinet (OPMC), which will give her control of key decision- making bodies across government.

The OPMC will include No 10, No 10 North and teams within the Cabinet Office.

Announcing the changes,, external which will also include an AI Taskforce, Leader of the House of Lords, Baroness Angela Smith said No 10 North would be a "dominant driver of the UK's economy".

It is understood that the initial location of No 10 Northwill be Heron House in central Manchester, in the same building which houses offices for the intelligence service GCHQ. Specialist police officers have been seen guarding the building.

It is likely that the longer term base for No 10 North will be at a new government "Digital Campus" which is under construction in the Ancoats area of Manchester. It is not due for completion until 2032.

However, a good indication of what it could look like comes from a new report, released on Tuesday.

The blueprint has been authored, external by the Re:State think tank which Burnham was involved with until he stepped down as mayor of Greater Manchester.

It sets out a possible recruitment plan that would bring in new hires from across Greater Manchester and the wider North of England.

In total, the report suggests around 300 staff should be employed within 18 months.

It proposes transferring teams from Whitehall departments, including the Treasury, Cabinet Office, and the Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government – all of which are now being led by ministers who represent constituencies inthe North of England.

Whitehall officials willing to move to Manchester have been told they will be offered relocation packages, the BBC understands.

It's also likely there will be a significant movement of staff from Burnham's former mayoral team at the Greater Manchester Combined Authority (GMCA).

The BBC has been told that at least three senior figures are understood to have agreed to work at No 10 North. Some GMCA staff expressed surprise at only finding out their colleagues would be moving after seeing them on TV greeting the new prime minister in Downing Street.

No 10 North will lead on a key plank of Burnham's agenda; handing power from Whitehall and Westminster to regions across the country.

It means strategic decisions about devolution and local economic growth will shift out of the Treasury, now led by Chancellor John Healey, and Angela Rayner's Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government - though both departments will have some "remaining functions" in these areas.
