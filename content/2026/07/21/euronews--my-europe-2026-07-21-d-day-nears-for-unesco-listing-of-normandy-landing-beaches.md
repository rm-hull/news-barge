## The bid to have the D-Day landing beaches listed as UNESCO World Heritage has entered a decisive phase, with a decision due between 24 and 27 July. Beyond its symbolic value, the status could boost tourism while helping to protect this endangered heritage.

Nearly twenty years after the project was launched, Normandy is on the verge of seeing its bid for UNESCO World Heritage status come to fruition. The United Nations body is due to announce its decision between 24 and 27 July, at the 48th session of the World Heritage Committee in Busan, South Korea.

If the application is approved, the D-Day landing beaches will join the 54 French sites already inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List, alongside Mont-Saint-Michel, the Palace of Versailles and Chartres Cathedral. The designation is reserved for sites considered to have ‘outstanding universal value’ and requires states to ensure their protection.

The listing would cover the five historic Allied landing beaches of 6 June 1944, Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno and Sword, as well as several emblematic sites such as Pointe du Hoc, the German battery at Longues-sur-Mer, the artificial harbour at Arromanches, and numerous military cemeteries and memorials.

On 6 June 1944, more than 150,000 Allied soldiers, mainly Americans, Britons and Canadians, landed on the Normandy coast in the largest amphibious operation in history. Over 4,000 of them lost their lives on that single day, which proved decisive in the liberation of Europe from Nazi occupation.

For Hervé Morin, president of the Normandy regional council, who has championed the bid since 2008, the listing would first and foremost represent ‘immense recognition’ for the soldiers who came to fight to bring Nazism to an end.

Beyond its historical significance, UNESCO World Heritage status could also have a major economic impact. The D-Day sites already attract almost 1.8 million visitors a year, more than a third of them from abroad, notably from the United Kingdom, the United States and Germany.

According to the regional tourism board, this visitor traffic generated 700 million euros in economic benefits in 2022 and supports more than 8,400 direct and indirect jobs. Hervé Morin believes that World Heritage listing could increase tourist numbers by around 15%.

**Safeguarding a fragile heritage**

Such recognition would also help protect a heritage that is becoming increasingly vulnerable. Several D-Day remains, notably bunkers from the Atlantic Wall, wrecks and some Allied installations, are now under threat from coastal erosion and rising sea levels.

At Longues-sur-Mer, for example, the former observation post of the German battery has been closed to the public since 2025 because of the risk of the cliff collapsing.

Situated between Omaha Beach and Gold Beach, it formed part of the Atlantic Wall built by Nazi Germany. Its mission was to shell Allied ships as they approached the beaches. Despite relentless Allied bombardment, it remained operational until the early hours of 7 June, when its garrison surrendered to the US Army.

Museum directors across the region nevertheless point out that UNESCO listing does not guarantee additional funding. In their view,preserving the memory will also require the digitisation of sites and virtual reconstructions, so that the history can be preserved once some of the physical remains have disappeared for good.

If the listing is confirmed in the coming days, the D-Day beaches will become not only a global symbol of remembrance of the Second World War, but also a site whose preservation is recognised as a matter of concern for humanity as a whole.
