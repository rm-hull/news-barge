Search the Public Notice PortalTHE building of a new home on land next to a recently-refurbished East Yorkshire village pub can go ahead after a successful appeal against a county council decision.

On June 11, the Government's planning inspectorate allowed an appeal by Amelia Heard against an East Riding of Yorkshire Council decision in January to refuse her November 2025 application for planning permission on land in Main Street, Etton.

The premises next to the land re-opened as The Pub at Etton in April 2025 and was formerly called The Light Dragoon - the vacant land was once the pub car park.

  


Construction works have begun on a detached house on the other side of the new pub, the inspector's report said.

The area has been the subject of a number of planning applications to the county council in recent years, including one for two houses on the same plot, which was dismissed, planning documents said.

The council said the plans to build a four-bedroom detached house on a 0.09 Hectare site with space for three cars would impact the Etton conservation area and also cause harm to the pub - which is a non-designated heritage asset (NDHA) - according to the inspector.

An NDHA is a building, monument, site, place, area or landscape judged to have local or regional heritage significance, but which does not meet the criteria for national designation (such as a listing, scheduling or registration).

According to council planning documents, Etton Parish Council voted unanimously against the proposed development saying it would have "too great an impact on the wider conservation area" and would compete with the character and appearance of the pub.

The council's objections also stated that "the proposed dwelling is immediately adjacent to the public house, whose normal operation including music performances on the external terrace has previously generated noise complaints from residents living further away than the application site.

"Locating a new residential property in such proximity would therefore expose future occupants to unacceptable noise levels and harm their residential amenity."

Other objectors stated that the land "provides a break in built form, allows views through to the countryside and reinforces the linear, historic form of the village" and "when considered alongside the separate application to the west of the pub, the cumulative effect is the near-complete loss of the open setting around the pub, diminishing the village's traditional character."

The inspector said that the "open and recently altered appearance on the appeal site at present does not positively contribute to the significance of the Etton conservation area and would be a "modest cottage" in relation to the pub, and that "relationship with the countryside from within the conservation area would be maintained."

The inspector added that the "development of the appeal site with a single dwelling, sited on a similar building line to it, but smaller in scale and height and of a traditional design would ensure the key significance of the NDHA as a prominent and focal building with a spacious setback is preserved.
