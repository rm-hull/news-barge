## Renowned catering firm entered liquidation owing almost £4.7 million

 *(Image: Newsquest)*

A renowned catering and events firm entered liquidation owing almost £4.7 million.

Brasted's Limited, of Norfolk, and its sister company Brasted's Twenty20 Limited went into liquidation in May, with owners Nick Mills, Chris Busby and Sian De Waal citing challenges facing the hospitality industry.

Brasted's Ltd, which was incorporated in 1986 and ran for 40 years, had an overall deficiency to creditors of more than £1.9 million, according to its statement of affairs.

Meanwhile, its property company Brasted's Twenty20 Ltd, which it used to borrow the money to purchase Caistor Hall hotel for £3.2 million in 2021, had an overall deficiency of about £2.8 million.

A total of almost £1.38 million of the companies’ combined liabilities was debt internal to the group, owed from Brasted's Twenty20 Ltd to Brasted's Ltd as an inter-company loan.

*(Image: Brasted's)*

  Sian de Waal, Brasted's director 

*(Image: Supplied)*

The debts of Brasted's Ltd included £1.2 million owed to Benjamin James Du Brow, the landlord and owner of Manor Farm Barns in Framingham Pigot, where Brasted’s previously operated a hotel and fine-dining restaurant.

The landlord and charity trustee is the chairman of the Du Brow Charity and a former director of the Gresham’s Foundation, which supports Gresham’s private school in Holt. He resigned in December last year.


Brasted’s opened its Framingham Pigot restaurant in 2004, and it quickly became a renowned spot - even hosting a private lunch party for Queen Elizabeth in 2010.

The two-AA rosette restaurant closed in 2024, with Brasted’s turning its attention to its Caistor Hall 21-bedroom hotel and wedding venue in Caistor St Edmund, south of Norwich.

However, Brasted's sold the Georgian property to corporate training provider Quadmark Group for £1.365 million in April.

  Brasted's sold Caistor Hall in April 

*(Image: Denise Bradley)*

Managing director Mr Mills, who joined the firm in 1991, said the decision to sell Caistor Hall was due to “extremely difficult issues” facing the hospitality sector since the pandemic, compounded by planning restrictions at the site.

Brasted’s had previously hoped to obtain planning permission to build an additional 36 bedrooms at the property, along with a new spa and events space, but was unable to do so as the site was located near the former Roman town, Venta Icenorum.

Seventeen staff were made redundant following the sale, with 30 now working for another outside catering company owned by the three directors, Complete & Utter Limited, which continues to run the Brasted's brand.

Brasted's Ltd’s debts included more than £9,000 in staff wages and holiday pay, which should be paid in full using the company’s £30,000 in available assets, which it expects to receive from the sale of assets to Complete & Utter Limited (£18,000) and Quadmark Limited (£12,000).

It also owed HMRC £135,000, which is not covered by its assets, leaving a shortfall of more than £114,000.

  Brasted's directors Chris Busby (left) and Nick Mills 

*(Image: Denise Bradley)*

Its unsecured creditors included 19 employees, owing a total of £98,000, and almost £24,000 to trade creditors.

South Norfolk Council was listed as a creditor owing £11,000, according to the company’s statement of affairs.

“Because of the downturn in the economy with hospitality, it's just been nightmare after nightmare," Mr Mills told this newspaper in May, when the two companies appointed liquidators.

“The Ukraine war started it off after Covid with the increased utility prices. Then there were other things, including 'Trussonomics'.

"It was just tough in the industry trying to make an independent work.”

The "Great Bon Viveur" John Brasted launched the firm when he opened his first restaurant in Norwich’s St Andrews Hall in 1984.

Brasted's Twenty20 Limited, which traded as Caistor Hall and also appointed liquidators on May 11, owed creditors almost £2.8 million, its statement of affairs shows.

  Caistor Hall, off Stoke Road in Caister St Edmund 

*(Image: Denise Bradley)*

Its largest creditor, investor Agha Developer and Investor Limited, was owed £1.28 million.

The company has just £18,000 of realisable assets, which will go towards the £135,000 owed to HMRC, leaving nothing for other creditors.
