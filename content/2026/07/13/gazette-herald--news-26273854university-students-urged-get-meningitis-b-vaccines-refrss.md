Hundreds of thousands of teenagers heading to university this autumn are now eligible for a long-awaited health boost.

17- and 18-year-olds in Year 13 across England can now book pharmacy appointments for the meningitis B (MenB) vaccine.

It follows the launch of a national programme aimed at protecting young people from the potentially deadly infection.

The offer also extends to anyone under 25 who will be starting university or another form of residential further education this autumn.


## Students heading to university urged to get meningitis vaccine

Eligible students will receive two doses of the vaccine, spaced at least 28 days apart, with appointments available from July 20.

The Government announced the programme last month after a series of meningitis outbreaks, including a fatal incident in Kent that claimed the lives of two teenagers.

Dr Shamez Ladhani, consultant epidemiologist at the UK Health Security Agency, said: "The risk of MenB rises sharply for young people starting university, where mixing closely with so many new people increases exposure to the bacteria.

"Getting both doses of the vaccine over the summer will provide robust protection against most meningococcal strains, offering vital peace of mind for students and families as they take this next big step."

MenB can cause a fast-moving and life-threatening infection.

  Year 13 pupils aged 17 and 18 will be eligible for the one-off jab programme.

The bacteria are spread through close contact such as kissing, sharing drinks, or living together in student accommodation.

NHS England says first-year university students are around seven times more likely to contract MenB compared to peers who are not attending university.

Caroline Temmink, NHS director of vaccination, said: "Heading off to university is an exciting time, but it’s also when the risk of MenB disease is higher as you spend lots of time with new people in shared living spaces, making it easier for the bacteria that cause this potentially deadly disease to spread.

"So, before you pack your bags, take a few minutes to get your meningitis jab – it’s one of the simplest things you can do to protect yourself before starting university."

Health experts, patient advocates, and the Government have all backed the urgent rollout of MenB vaccines ahead of the autumn term.

Brian Davies, head of health insights and policy at the Meningitis Research Foundation, said: "Too many of the people we support are left asking ‘what if?’ after MenB has changed their lives forever.

  The risk of MenB for first-year university students is about seven times higher than people a similar age who did not go to university.

"This vaccination programme gives young people and their families the chance to avoid that heartbreak.

"Having supported thousands of people through the Kent outbreak and other recent clusters of cases, we know how important it is to close the MenB protection gap for teenagers and young adults."

The call to get vaccinated comes as the Department of Health and Social Care and Department for Education launch a new school-aged vaccination taskforce to improve nationwide jab uptake.

The Joint Committee on Vaccination and Immunisation is also considering whether a longer-term scheme is needed.

Health Secretary James Murray said: "We’ve already worked in partnership to encourage international students to get their first MenB dose before coming to the UK.

"By coming together, we can help more young people protect themselves so they can get on and enjoy the next chapter of their education."
