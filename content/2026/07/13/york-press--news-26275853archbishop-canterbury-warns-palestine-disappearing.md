The Archbishop of Canterbury has warned that Palestine “is disappearing” as the Church of England’s parliament voted to “hear” a document which referred to a “genocidal war on Gaza”.

Speaking at the church’s meeting in York, Dame Sarah Mullally spoke of the “palpable” fear of people in that area of the Middle East, which she visited last month.

General Synod – known as the Church’s parliament – debated a motion to “stand in solidarity” with Palestinian Christians “and their fellow Palestinians in non-violent resistance to the ongoing occupation”.

The motion had originally asked Synod to “receive” various documents citing the experience of Palestinian Christians, but this was amended during debate at its meeting at the University of York to say that members would “hear” the document contents instead.

One document – known as Kairos Palestine II, entitled “A Moment of Truth: Faith in a Time of Genocide” – stated: “Zionists do not want us to remain on our land. Their plan for us is displacement, death or submission.

“The genocidal war on Gaza is the continuation of the Zionist project to seize all of Palestine, emptied of its Palestinian people. Ethnic cleansing and the denial of the right of return to those forcibly displaced are ongoing policies practised in Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza and the territories of 1948.”

Ahead of the debate on Sunday and Monday, Chief Rabbi Sir Ephraim Mirvis criticised the document, saying it “presents a one-sided account of a complex conflict, downplays the historical experiences and legitimate concerns of Jewish people, and offers little more than political activism dressed up as theology”.

The Board of Deputies of British Jews had called on Synod to “reject the Kairos documents, especially Kairos II and its false and dangerous claims, just as the Church should distance itself from any initiative from either side that perpetuates demonising myths about the other”.

The organisation added: “We call on the Church to hear, alongside as the voices of Palestinians, the voices of Israelis, and Jews in Britain and around the world, and to amplify voices of peace and reconciliation.”

Dame Sarah, addressing Synod during the debate, said: “To hear the heartfelt expressions of the lived experiences of the Palestinian Christians does not mean we agree with everything in these documents, but it does mean that we listen with compassion and stand in solidarity with them amidst the many injustices they face.”

Recalling her visit to the area last month, the archbishop said: “The fear was palpable among everyone we met, Palestinian and Israeli.

“From Gaza to the north of Israel, from southern Lebanon to the West Bank, people across the region are traumatised by ongoing conflicts.”

She spoke of settlements in the West Bank which had “expanded at unprecedented rates” with thousands of Palestinians “being forced from their land”, and told of “settler violence and social, economic, and political hopelessness”.

She added: “Put simply: Palestine, which the British government recognised last year, is disappearing.”

In her visit last month, Dame Sarah had – in the first visit of an Archbishop of Canterbury to the Palestinian Christian town of Birzeit in the West Bank – told Palestinians she will use her role to seek “the peace you desire and the freedom you deserve”.

Her pilgrimage involved meeting, praying and worshipping with Palestinian Christians in East Jerusalem, the occupied West Bank and Israel.

In her Synod debate, she said: “I am a pastor, not a politician. When I say the Palestinian people deserve their freedom, that is not a political statement, but a moral and spiritual one.

“Both Palestinians and Israelis deserve to live free from the horrors of war, terrorism, occupation, displacement, and oppression.

“International humanitarian law offers the best protection from all such violations of human rights and dignity – it must be upheld across Israel and Palestine.”

The motion also called on Synod to “reject antisemitism, anti-Muslim hostility and all forms of prejudice based on religious affiliation and ethnicity” and “pray for all victims of the current conflicts in Israel and the occupied Palestinian territory and for a lasting peace”.

Dame Sarah said the motion “rightly notes that we lament – equally, without qualification – the loss of Israeli and Palestinian lives” and affirms “our rejection of antisemitism and anti-Muslim hostility”.

She added: “More than ever we need a critical, respectful dialogue – that includes the voices of Palestinian Christians, as well as our Jewish and Muslim friends.”

## York campaigners stage 'die-in' before debate

Ahead of the debate, pro-Palestinian activists staged a “die-in” outside York Minster which they said was to draw attention to the thousands of Palestinians killed since the attacks on October 7, 2023.

Some 1,200 people were killed in Israel and more than 250 taken hostage into Gaza after Israel launched its offensive in response to the Hamas-led attack on southern Israel on October 7, 2023.

Christine Chettle, on behalf of the group York Concerned Citizens for Palestine, said it welcomed the Kairos Palestine II motion being heard.

“We are very glad that the Church of England has taken this historic step towards peace and justice. We hope that both the churches and the other faith communities in Palestine will be encouraged by it,” she said.
