## Traders join forces in York's most-photographed street to combat thieves

A generic image of shoppers recently in the Shambles in York. *(Image: Kevin Glenton)*

TRADERS in one of York's busiest and most popular streets say they have joined forces in a bid to protect their businesses from theft.

Shambles is one of the best-known streets in the city and home to a mix of international, national and independent local retailers.

It is one of Europe’s best-preserved medieval streets, with narrow cobbles, overhanging timber-framed buildings and remnants of its butcher’s-street past on display.

  


Unlike neighbouring Parliament Street with wider boulevards, the Shambles' shopping experience is tightly-packed - inside and out - and that extends to the shelves, and with it, brings concerns over thefts which are shared by other retailers.

Mike Gaskell, the chair of the local trader's association and proprietor of Little Saffrons, a gift shop about half-way down, said: "Shambles is not a highly targeted area for theft like the larger stores in York and we don't have the same challenges.

"We do get some examples of petty theft, from people who like what they see but don't fancy paying for it.

"I used to be a store manager and in my experience thefts here are highly opportunistic."

He said traders are very grateful for the support of other businesses and York's Street Rangers who patrol the city centre, who he said they all know personally and work with them in a very effective bid to prevent crime and also, manage anti-social behaviour towards shop workers in Shambles.

A WhatsApp group shared among the traders is a useful tool in the bid to prevent crime and share information quickly across the shops, he added.

Mike said: "The community really welcomes that point of contact with each other.

"I've got every single shop manager, to shop owners, involved, so everybody is aware of what is going on and sharing information."

  Phil Pinder is co-founder of The Potions Cauldron emporium in the heart of Shambles 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

He said this leads to an increased awareness, and traders are able to take action to limit or prevent incidents in their own shop, which can also help reduce examples of anti-social behaviour.

Phil Pinder, co-founder of The Potions Cauldron said: "It's a very active group and reminds me of Neighbourhood Watch schemes.

"We started pooling footage and sharing intel, we might pass on information if we hear someone is passing counterfeit notes in the shops, for example."
