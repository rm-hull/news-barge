# Hailing a 'grand day' for opening of record-breaking 167th Great Yorkshire Show

## A grand day has been hailed for the region’s biggest farming focus as a record-breaking Great Yorkshire Show (GYS) gets underway. This is England’s premier agricultural event, hosted in Harrogate in its 167th year, and set to see crowds like never before over its full four days of opening.

The showground was bathed in sunshine yesterday as celebrations began, with farmers and families alike making the most of the "glorious" weather.

And to the sound of brass bands, with neighs and cheers from the main ring and some calls of 'come-by', came the feeling of something special beginning. William Maughan, regional board chairman for NFU North, said this was a "showcase" for the very best of the farming industry. "It's a shop window," he said. "A chance to demonstrate what we do to the public. And it's such a fantastic opportunity to come together."

The weather was "glorious", he said, the atmosphere "fantastic". And, especially in times of change, such connections are crucial, he said. "It's good to get together, to share ideas and to chew the fat," he added.

This year's event, organised by the Yorkshire Agricultural Society (YAS) is set to welcome some 150,000 visitors.

With the show extended over four days in recent years, and with extra tickets selling out even as they were released, it makes it the biggest show yet. Thousands of animals are hosted on the showground, from champion pigeons to prize beasts for Grand Cattle Parades.

Children as young as three will be among those to shine as they try their hand in young handlers competitions, while on the presidents' lawn the world’s largest combine harvester impressed the punters.

The GYS is crucial not just to farmers in sharing and showing their wares but in debate from industry leaders.

And in the wake of Brexit, and with inflation, cost uncertainty and constraints around the Strait of Hormuz, said NFU leaders, it has been a "volatile" time for the sector.

A panel of experts, which featured chief officer for UK Argi-Tech Steve McClean and innovation leader Sam Hoste, himself a Malton farmer, spoke of opportunities for change.

Advances in technology were racing at pace - particularly around the environment and climate change, they outlined, with tools to measure soil moisture as just one example.

"We look at the mantra of 'having to do more with less'," said Mr McClean. "We are going to need all the tech and the best brains to deliver food systems."

Tom Bradshaw, NFU president, inset, also spoke of food resilience. With political change, he warned incoming Prime Minister Andy Burnham there was "opportunity here". Around priorities, around employment and adoption of new technologies. But he added: "We are going to have to get better at how we manage the weather." Echoing Mr Bradshaw’s hopes, Gavin Lane, president of the CLA, believes the forthcoming change in Prime Minister means the possibility for change is “not as fanciful as it sounds”.

CLA members are currently facing one of the worst arable farming periods to date as feed wheat is £175 a tonne and barely above the cost of production, and fertiliser costs mean it is cheaper to leave fields fallow than it is to plant them, he said.

The new SFI scheme has a cap and IHT is now reality and not a threat.

However, in his address to CLA members, Mr Lane was keen to push for progress being set out in a new report.

He said: “When it costs a farmer more to grow food, the system is broken. That is not a farming problem, it is a national security problem and a problem for the whole country.

“Where does this leave us? Not in despair I want to argue. This week we are producing a report on the future of the rural economy. It is forward looking, asking what the rural economy can be by 2040, and what must happen in parliament to get there.

“It shows a way forward for rural, economic growth. The rural economy is massive and not a problem to be managed - it is an opportunity to be unlocked.”

Themes focus on planning, connectivity, skills, and a tax system which rewards farmers for investing. Mr Lane added: “It is not ridiculous, it is achievable. Adapt, invest, employ, produce.

“The GYS has been going since 1838 - it has survived.

The countryside survives because the people in it endure.

"We need to make sure the next generation does not endure but thrives.”
