Families giving money to children and grandchildren for weddings, house deposits or university costs could be paying far more tax than necessary because they are unaware of generous HMRC gifting rules, say experts.

From helping a grandchild buy their first home to paying school fees or celebrating a wedding, gifting money is one of the most common ways parents and grandparents support younger generations.

But financial advisers warn many people are making these gifts without realising they can also reduce the amount of Inheritance Tax (IHT) their family may eventually have to pay.

With estates above the £325,000 nil-rate band potentially facing a 40% Inheritance Tax bill, making gifts during your lifetime can play a major role in estate planning.

Tom Mayne, Financial Adviser at Mattioli Woods, said: "Some of the most effective estate planning doesn't happen in a solicitor's office or across a desk. It happens during these moments, quietly, naturally, in the form of gifts that families were always going to make."

He added: "The difference between gifting reactively and gifting with a little forethought can be significant, both for the person giving and for the generations receiving."


## The HMRC gifting allowances many families overlook

HMRC already offers several tax-free gifting allowances, but experts say many people either don't know they exist or fail to use them fully.

One of the best known is the £3,000 annual gifting allowance, which allows every individual to give away up to £3,000 each tax year without it counting towards their estate for Inheritance Tax purposes. If you don't use it, the allowance can normally be carried forward for one tax year.

There's also the small gifts allowance, allowing gifts of up to £250 per person to any number of people each tax year.

Wedding gifts have their own tax exemptions too. Parents can give up to £5,000, grandparents up to £2,500, and anyone else up to £1,000, provided the gift is made before or on the wedding day.


## The little-known rule that could save families thousands

One of the most overlooked exemptions is HMRC's 'normal expenditure out of income' rule.

This allows people to make regular gifts from surplus income without them counting towards Inheritance Tax, provided the payments form part of a regular pattern, come from income rather than capital, and do not reduce the giver's normal standard of living.

For some families, this could include helping with:

- School or university fees
- Rent payments
- Regular contributions towards living costs
- Monthly gifts to children or grandchildren

Mr Mayne said this exemption is "perhaps the most underused allowance of all", particularly for retirees with reliable pension or investment income.

## House deposits and cash gifts need careful planning

Helping children onto the property ladder has become increasingly common, but advisers say larger cash gifts should be carefully documented.

Where gifts exceed the available allowances, they will usually become what's known as Potentially Exempt Transfers (PETs).

These gifts can become completely free from Inheritance Tax if the donor survives for seven years after making them.

Mr Mayne said: "House deposits represent some of the largest gifts families make, and they warrant careful thought."

## Grandparents could become increasingly affected

The advice comes as pension rules are also changing.

From April 2027, most unused pension funds are due to fall within the scope of Inheritance Tax for the first time, making decisions around when and how grandparents pass on wealth even more important.

Mr Mayne said gifting directly to grandchildren can also help bypass one generation of Inheritance Tax, reducing the possibility of family wealth being taxed twice within a relatively short period.

## Don't forget charitable donations

Families making charitable donations may also be able to benefit from tax relief.

HMRC says: "Donations by individuals to charity or to community amateur sports clubs (CASCs) are tax free. This is called tax relief."

Depending on how the donation is made, "the tax goes to you or the charity", including through Gift Aid, Payroll Giving, gifts of land, property or shares, or donations left in a will.

HMRC also reminds taxpayers: "You'll need to keep a record of your donations if you want to take them off your total taxable income."

## Why keeping records matters

Financial experts also stress the importance of keeping detailed records of any gifts made during your lifetime.

Executors may need to account for up to seven years of gifting history when dealing with an estate, making it easier to demonstrate which HMRC exemptions were used.

Mr Mayne said: "Thoughtful gifting is not about finding loopholes. It's about ensuring that the wealth you have accumulated over a lifetime reaches the people you intend, in the most efficient way possible."

He added: "Used well, the allowances HMRC provides are generous, but they reward planning and consistency over impulse. This is where good advice makes the difference."
