Teenage girls are at the heart of a new community-led project in York.

After five years in development, the Make Space Area — known as the Teenage Zone — has been completed at Rowntree Park in York, designed and developed through hundreds of conversations, workshops, surveys, and park walks with more than 400 local girls.

The project, led by Abigail Gaines, charity manager for the Friends of Rowntree Park and founder of Make Space York, set out to answer the question of what would encourage teenage girls to spend more time in parks.

Ms Gaines said: "As girls enter their teenage years, their use of parks drops significantly compared with boys.

  The project is led by Abigail Gaines, charity manager for the Friends of Rowntree Park and founder of Make Space York 

*(Image: Make Space York)*

"Parks offer enormous benefits for physical activity, mental wellbeing, social connection and access to nature, yet many girls tell us they no longer feel there is anywhere for them."

Rather than assuming what teenagers wanted, Make Space York spent five years listening directly to them.

Teenage girls said they wanted opportunities to move, be active, socialise, and have fun outdoors in ways that reflected their age and stage of life.

They asked for equipment designed for teenage bodies, spaces for climbing and balancing, and places to spend time with friends without feeling out of place.

The location was just as important as the equipment, with girls wanting a space set back from the busiest paths so they could relax and be themselves, but still close enough to feel safe.

  Teenage girls said they wanted opportunities to move, be active, socialise, and have fun outdoors 

*(Image: Make Space York)*

They also preferred equipment and seating spread across the area so different groups could use the space together.

The Teenage Zone is open to everyone, but visitors are asked to give priority to older children, as there are few public spaces where teenagers are actively encouraged to play.

The first phase of the area opened in May 2024 and was well received.

The latest additions, chosen directly by local girls, complete the project.

Funding for the new phase came from multiple sources, including David Skaith, Mayor of York and North Yorkshire, through the York and North Yorkshire Combined Authority's Movement, Activity and Sport Fund, as well as the TransPennine Route Upgrade and community crowdfunding, including a donation from Portakabin.

The project is already being recognised nationally and internationally.

*(Image: Make Space York)*

Ms Gaines said: "This has never just been about installing play equipment.

"It's about changing the way we think about teenagers in our public spaces.

"We often make assumptions about what young people want, but when you genuinely listen — and keep listening over a number of years rather than through a one-off consultation — you discover things you might never have expected."

Engagement with girls continues as Make Space York works with local partners to explore further improvements across Rowntree Park and beyond.
