**Why high speed trains for short distances won't work**

Can the HS2 costs overrun be even greater than those of a certain two aircraft carriers which shall be nameless, HMS Unspecified and HMS Anonymous?

Are all those multiple billions of our money well spent? Could it have been splashed out more effectively, in the railway case, on the existing network?

But it’ll all be worth it in the end. Won’t it? Think of the unique experience, worth every bitcoin. Really exciting. Onto the train. Then ‘whoosh!’ as we accelerate to ‘Warp 9’ and ‘Screech!’ as we complete our 80 mile journey 50 minutes later, saving passengers as much as an impressive half-hour.

No time really to set up your on-train office or workspace. How would the passenger fit in refreshments? Could high-speed gobbling lead to an outbreak of indigestion? May I nominate a Guinea pig for HS2 train trials and test runs? Michael Portillo.

  


Much of the scenery is subterranean or restricted; what limited stretches are visible would just be a blur. Dazed passengers, under the effects of rail-lag, would lurch off at the Birmingham or London end, heads swimming, asking for the way out right under the ‘exit’ sign, before they reorientate. The deceleration required to achieve a successful standstill over such short distances would probably have medical side-effects, ranging from hangovers, bends (especially when turning) or organ displacement.

I can see a point in seriously-fast trains but only when travelling over sensible distances and not having to brake a short time after the guard’s departure whistle has blown.

  *Derek Reed,*

  *Middlethorpe Drive,*

  *York*
