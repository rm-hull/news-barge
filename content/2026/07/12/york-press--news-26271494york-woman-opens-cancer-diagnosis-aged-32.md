A cancer diagnosis changed everything for Heather Hackney.

Heather, then 32, was sat alone in a clinic – her husband, Chris, barred from entering due to it being the pandemic – when she was diagnosed.

The diagnosis was leiomyosarcoma, a rare type of cancer that develops in the smooth muscle cells.

Heather, from York, survived the cancer, but that was just the start of her journey – she would soon have to come to terms with the loss of her fertility.

After she gave birth to her son in 2019, Heather started having pelvic problems. She had an ultrasound that found a mass on her pelvis.

Heather was diagnosed with a fibroid – non-cancerous growths that develop in or around the womb – after various scans.

The pain in her pelvis continued until, in August 2020, she passed out at home.

Heather had emergency surgery to remove the fibroid plus a blood transfusion. A sample was then sent for pathology. Heather was told this was routine and nothing to worry about.

But four weeks after the surgery, Heather was told to come into the clinic – and it was there that she was diagnosed with leiomyosarcoma.

  Heather Hackney with her family 

*(Image: Sarcoma UK)*

She was so shocked that she needed her husband, Chris, to pick her up from the clinic.

“I was in absolute shock,” she said. “Nothing really prepares you for it.”

Heather had a radical hysterectomy in September 2020, which was followed by radiotherapy ending in February 2021. Since then, tiny nodules have been found on her lungs and she now has regular scans to monitor her health.

  Heather Hackney with her family 

*(Image: Sarcoma UK)*

Heather, now 38 and who works in higher education, said she has been on an “intense journey” with her health.

“I did not really try to process my feelings about it while I was going through treatment,” she said, “and when I did finally start to accept what had happened, I became very depressed.

“I spent a lot of time working with a cancer psychologist to 'grieve' the loss of my fertility.

“I needed this safe space to work through these emotions, as it wasn't something I could really talk to my friends and family about.”

## 'Outlook changed' after having tower

Heather said her “outlook changed” after having cancer. “I had to reprioritise everything in life. I would never live as I did before cancer. I would have loved to have had another baby but that was taken away. That is a lot to come to terms with at 32.”

Heather described her husband as “a rock” during her ordeal, adding that she also turned to the national sarcoma cancer charity Sarcoma UK’s support line for advice on treatment.

“Going through a cancer diagnosis was both scary and confusing,” she said. “Sarcoma UK provided help and support when I most needed it and I'm incredibly grateful for that.”

Sarcoma UK’s support line manager, Helen Stradling, said: “A sarcoma diagnosis raises so many frightening questions and, for younger patients like Heather, the impact on fertility makes it even harder to process.

“Our Sarcoma UK Support Line specialists are there to provide clear, expert guidance on diagnosis and treatment, giving patients the knowledge and confidence they need at the most difficult time of their lives."
