# Two thirds of men work while believing they should seek medical advice, research finds

## Two-thirds of UK men continue to work despite believing they should seek medical advice or take time off to recover, while a quarter are uncomfortable discussing health concerns at work, according to new research from not-for-profit healthcare provider, Benenden Health.

Many men reported delaying seeking support because they believed the health issue would simply go away on its own, while more than a quarter didn’t think the concern was serious enough to warrant medical attention.

Nearly a quarter said they had delayed seeking help because they were too busy with work.

The findings, conducted among 2,000 working men aged 30-55 across the UK, come despite 88 per cent of HR leaders saying they feel confident supporting men’s health and wellbeing, highlighting a gap between employer perspectives and men’s experiences at work.

Dr Amy Pressland, head of talent & performance at Benenden Health said, “The workplace has an important role to play in helping men feel more comfortable prioritising their health and seeking support earlier.

“While it’s encouraging that many employers recognise the importance of employee wellbeing, our research suggests there is still a gap when it comes to targeted support for men’s health specifically.

“Many men are continuing to work through health concerns or delaying conversations until issues begin affecting their wellbeing and performance at work.

“That can have a serious impact not only on individuals, but on teams and businesses more widely.”

Benenden health said its member data also shows that men are significantly less likely than women to engage with key health services, accounting for just 24 per cent of usage compared to 76 per cent among female members across support including health coaching, GP services and mental health support.

Benenden said research among workplace HR leads also suggests many employers recognise that men are often reluctant to speak openly about their health at work, with more than four in five agreeing male employees are less likely than their female counterparts to discuss health concerns.

Just over 85 per cent said they had experienced situations where a male employee delayed seeking support until a health issue negatively affected their work.

The findings also suggest many men wait for a significant “wake-up call” before taking action.
