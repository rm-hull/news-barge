## The petition alleges that Slovenian referee Slavko Vincic delivered a "controversial and corrupt" performance and calls on FIFA to review several of his decisions during the match.

Argentina's defeat to Spain in the 2026 World Cup final, played on Sunday 19 July at New Jersey's MetLife Stadium, has sparked a wave of protests that continues several days after the final whistle.

Fan Gisela Sánchez launched a petition on Change.org calling on FIFA to replay the match, which was decided by a Ferran Torres goal in extra time. At the time of writing, the petition had gathered more than 80,739 signatures.

The petition accuses Slovenian referee Slavko Vincic of delivering a "controversial and corrupt" performance and calls on the governing body to review the decisions made during the match.

However, the organiser provides no verified evidence to support those allegations beyond videos circulating on social media that she claims show incidents favouring Spain.

FIFA's regulations allow a match to be replayed only in exceptional circumstances, such as a serious breach of the competition rules or a decision by the governing body's competent authorities before a result is officially confirmed.

A Change.org petition, regardless of how many signatures it receives, has no legal or sporting effect on the result or Spain's World Cup title.

## This is not the first petition of its kind

Similar campaigns have followed previous major finals. After Argentina won the 2022 World Cup in Qatar, more than 200,000 French supporters signed a petition calling for the final to be replayed over concerns about the refereeing of Poland's Szymon Marciniak. Argentina's response at the time was to tell critics to "stop crying".

There are few precedents, and almost all are decades old. In 2006, a World Cup qualifying match between Uzbekistan and Bahrain had to be replayed because of a technical error by the referee, not simply because of disagreement over decisions made on the pitch. Sports courts have consistently refused to order replays based on refereeing decisions, no matter how controversial they may be.

While the petition continues to attract signatures, Argentinians have chosen a different form of response in the streets. On the day of the defeat, tens of thousands of fans gathered in cities across the country to celebrate the team's run to the final, despite falling just short of a fourth world title.

## FIFA is investigating, but for a different reason

Separate from the petition, FIFA has opened a disciplinary investigation, though not into the refereeing but into the incidents that occurred after the final whistle. The governing body has appointed a disciplinary and ethics prosecutor to investigate a number of clashes between players from both teams after the match.

Argentina midfielder Leandro Paredes has been singled out in a separate petition that has gathered up to 23 million signatures. Footage appears to show him grabbing Eric Garcia by the neck and shoving Gavi during Spain's celebrations, an incident that led to his dismissal after the final whistle.

FIFA is also reviewing the behaviour of Nahuel Molina, who is alleged to have struck Rodri, and the possible involvement of Roberto Ayala, Lionel Scaloni's assistant coach, in shoving Dani Olmo.

The investigation also covers the trophy presentation, during which several Argentina players turned their backs as Spain lifted the trophy. It also revives an issue dating back to the semi-finals, when Argentina players displayed a banner asserting the country's sovereignty over the Falkland Islands after eliminating England, an incident for which the AFA has already been fined.

Coach Lionel Scaloni sought to ease tensions at the post-match press conference, saying: "We are gracious in victory, and we must be so in defeat as well." FIFA, however, has decided to proceed with the investigation and will provide further details once its report is complete.
