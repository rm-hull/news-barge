# Hillsborough Law: Why the Ministry of Defence needs a duty of candour - Victoria Sedgwick

## The advantage of working exclusively for military personnel and their families is that you gain insight and understanding into how the Ministry of Defence operates and can identify patterns of behaviour. The most common feature, which appears in nearly all of the military claims I have worked on, is a resistance by the MOD to accept accountability, learn and make changes.

This can manifest in small ways, such as not providing documents, or sometimes in more concerning ways such as a reluctance to conduct an internal investigation, or even worse, when an investigation has been carried out improperly.

This makes uncovering the truth far more challenging and significantly prolongs the process of securing justice. Changing a deeply ingrained culture is hugely difficult; however, it’s clear that this culture is at the heart of so many of the issues that serving personnel and their families have suffered over the years.

Unlike the NHS, there is no current statutory or professional obligation requiring the Ministry of Defence to be open and transparent when something goes wrong. Therefore, it’s encouraging that a duty of candour has been proposed in The Public Office (Accountability) Bill 2024-25, or ‘Hillsborough Law’ and that this extends to the MOD.

Although this duty of candour will only apply to investigations and inquiries, it does move the dial significantly on this issue.

The Bill will “require all public bodies to establish a professional duty of candour for staff, to be set out within a wider code of ethics. These obligations will be underpinned by a new duty requiring public bodies to promote the ethical conduct of their employees. Professional duties of candour will be tailored to the sectors to which they apply: meaningful to staff and responsive to the needs of those who use an organisation’s services. Authorities will be legally required to set out the consequences for staff who do not comply, including potential disciplinary sanctions up to and including gross misconduct”.

At present there is little else in the Bill that sets out how this duty of candour should be implemented by public authorities. I believe greater clarity is required by defining what the duty entails and setting out universal consequences for failing to act in accordance with this duty. Without this, there is a risk that each public authority could water down the definition and reduce its effectiveness.

After a long delay, the Hillsborough Law recently returned to Parliament to be approved by MPs and is now on its journey through the House of Lords. It appears there is now fresh impetus, and whilst the Bill should be implemented as soon as practicable, I would like to see the duty of candour strengthened and extended to ensure it delivers real change and guarantees accountability across all investigations, civil cases, and related inquiries.

The recent call by the families of victims of the 1994 Chinook disaster, for the Hillsborough Law to extend to their case, shows exactly why we need this new law and a robust approach to how duty of candour should be applied to the MOD.

Deep-seated cultural change is needed in the MOD, and this begins with a duty of candour and the willingness from the MOD to be truly transparent and, importantly, learn from its mistakes. Cultures of cover-up and blame need to end to ensure the safety of our serving personnel, veterans and their families.

Victoria Sedgwick is senior associate in the Military Claims Team at Bolt Burdon Kemp.
