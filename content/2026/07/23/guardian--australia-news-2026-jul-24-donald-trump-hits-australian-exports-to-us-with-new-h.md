Donald Trump has hit Australian exports with new trade tariffs, claiming the country is not doing enough to combat forced labour, but the Albanese government has demanded he remove them immediately.

The United States announced on Friday morning (AEST) that many Australian exports now attract a new 12.5% tariff as part of a broader regime affecting dozens of countries. However the Albanese government says Australia’s three largest export categories – including beef and gold – will avoid the tariff hit.

In a statement, the US trade department said Australia was on a list of 54 economies it deemed as having “failed to impose and effectively enforce a prohibition on the importation of goods produced with forced labor”. Other countries facing a 12.5% tariff include Brazil, China, Egypt, Israel, Japan, New Zealand, Norway, Russia and Singapore.

Economies on a separate list – including the UK, India, Indonesia, Canada and Mexico – will be subject to a lower 10% tariff after the US claimed they had only failed to “enforce” forced labour prohibitions.

Friday’s news followed through on an earlier threat from the Trump administration to level such tariffs, which came after the US supreme court earlier this year struck down Trump’s 10% global tariffs.

Trump had responded to that court ruling by immediately announcing temporary trade sanctions, which were due to expire today.

Australian government sources had previously told Guardian Australia they believed the forced labour-related tariffs had been levelled as a replacement for the tariffs struck down by the courts.

US congresswoman Linda Sánchez, a California Democrat, has denounced the pretext for the new tariffs as a sham.

“President Trump isn’t serious about combating forced labor. He’s trying to make an end-run around the Supreme Court and Congress to reimpose his illegal tariffs,” Sánchez wrote on social media.

The American trade action document claims “the United States is the only country in the world to adopt, and effectively enforce, a ban on imports made with forced labor”.

“President Trump is asking all trading partners to join the United States in eliminating forced labor from global supply chains.”

In total the tariffs apply “to the top 60 U.S. trade partners covering 99.4% of U.S. imports”, according to a trade department statement.

The Australian trade minister, Don Farrell, immediately called for the US to change course.

“These tariffs are unjustified, inconsistent with our free-trade agreement and should be removed,” Farrell said.

“Australia’s measures to combat forced labour and modern slavery are among the strongest in the world and we are recognised globally, including in the US, for our leadership.”

In a subsequent press conference, Farrell said Australian exports to the US had increased since Trump’s so-called “liberation day” tariffs, and that while he would strongly protest for the removal of the trade barriers, many of Australia’s biggest exports to Australia would not be affected.

“It’s beef, it’s gold and it’s copper… I don’t see any reason to think that’s not going to continue,” he said.

The deputy prime minister, Richard Marles, told ABC radio the US actions “make no sense”.

“Forced labour is definitely something which needs to be combated around the world, so we support that objective,” he said. “But we have laws, which are amongst the strongest in the world, and we’ve been making that point to the Trump administration.”

The US trade action document that sets out the new tariffs includes exemptions for a range of goods including beef; agricultural products such as vegetables and fruits; aircraft parts; certain coal, oil, mineral and chemical products; some metal and steel products; timber products; and machinery.

The document says it will broadly apply to goods entering from 12.01am US eastern time on 24 July (2pm Aest).

The action, as it pertains to Australia specifically, states that after an investigation, “the Trade Representative has determined to impose 12.5 percent tariffs on products of Australia” except for a limited number of exemptions.
