New EU sanctions 'hitting Putin where it hurts most' in 'largest round' of listings, foreign policy chief Kallas says

EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas has offered a bit more detail on the new package of sanctions, saying they are “the largest round in four years,” and will “hit Putin where it hurts most.”

“We have agreed on the 21st sanctions package against Russia.

It delivers sweeping measures targeting Moscow’s financial system, its military-industrial complex and energy sector, that keep Russia’s war economy running.

We are hitting Putin where it hurts most: cutting off the financial lifelines he relies on to sustain his war.

This is not the end of the road on sanctions. We are already working on the next steps. The EU must be ready to respond to any further Russian escalation.

This is our largest round in four years, totalling 218 listings.

We’ve hit more than a hundred banks and crypto operators, over 40 shadow fleet vessels, and several oil refineries in Russia and Belarus, who help keep Moscow’s war going.

We are also responding to Russia’s relentless attacks on civilians, infrastructure and cultural heritage by listing more than 50 military-industrial entities, including key actors involved in the production of Russia’s long-range drones.

This comes on top of last week’s sanctions on those responsible for the abuse and killing of Ukrainian prisoners of war and civilian detainees, alongside tech companies enabling the Kremlin’s repressions of internet users.”

EU fines Google €890m for competition breaches over search and apps

Elsewhere, there is a big breaking news story in Brussels asGoogle has been fined a total of €890m (£760m) by the EU for breaches of online competition laws by its search and app store services.

Google has been fined €460m for the search-related breach and €430m for the app store violation. Photograph: Steve Marcus/Reuters

The European Commission, the EU’s executive arm, said Google had broken the Digital Markets Act by giving priority to its own services such as shopping and hotel deals in search results over those of its rivals.

It also infringed the DMA by preventing app developers from steering consumers towards cheaper offers on websites or alternative app stores.

EU bows to pressure from Greece on LNG to get 21st package adopted - snap analysis

Jennifer Rankin

in Brussels

After weeks of delay the EU has reached agreement on the latest package of sanctions against Russia, by handing a concession to Greece.

The Revithoussa LNG terminal near Athens, in Megara, Greece. Photograph: NurPhoto/Getty Images

The EU’s 27 ambassadors reached a political agreement on the 21st round of sanctions, an EU diplomat said, after a final round of talks on Thursday morning.

Talks wrapped up quickly on Thursday morning, after discussions described as “heated” on Wednesday, as Athens upheld its veto.

Greece has secured a one-year exemption from an earlier EU sanctions measure, enabling it to transport liquefied natural gas to non EU countries, as long as the contract was signed before Russia’s full-scale invasion. The carve out protects a large Greek shipping company, Dynagas, that specialised in transporting LNG from Russia’s Yamal fields.

The Greek government sought an exemption on the phase out of transporting Russian gas due to take effect on 1 January 2027 – despite having agreed to the measure in October 2025.

The package includes an agreement to extend the current oil price cap on Russian revenues for a further 12 months, preventing Russia from taking advantage of rising oil prices as a result of the blockade of the strait of Hormuz. The EU’s oil-price cap was due to go up automatically in August, heaping pressure to find an agreement.

The package also includes the highest number of sanctions on individuals in any package in the last four years, according to the EU diplomat.

But a plan to ban Russian ex-combatants from travelling to the EU appears to have been delayed, although there is a pledge to “bring forward the necessary measures”.
