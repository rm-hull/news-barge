Leaders in Madison, Wisconsin, have identified Corey Ruiz as the man killed yesterday in a fatal police shooting that has sparked waves of protests and anger in the community.

“It does not matter who you are or what your past is, you should not lose your life as a result of an encounter with law enforcement,” Madison mayor Satya Rhodes-Conway said at a press conference on Thursday, which was later taken over for minutes on end by protesters criticizing the city and its police department.

City council president Sabrina Madison said she was pushing for “justice for Corey” and called on community members to demand police reform and a swift investigation by state officials who have taken over investigating the shooting as a potential homicide.

“If the protocol to go that quickly to shoot this gentleman in the head three times, and that is an option, I don’t know how that is an option,” she said. “My brain has not been able to put that together as an option. Because I didn’t see anything that warranted it.”

Critics argued video of the incident shared widely online showed that police “executed” Ruiz in the middle of a busy street in broad daylight.

Footage shows four cops on Wednesday working to subdue the suspect. The officers surround the man as he struggles to break free. While they have him by the arms and on the ground, one cop pulls out his gun and shoots the victim dead before reholstering his weapon and an officer puts the victim’s limp arms behind his body to handcuff him.

“This was an execution, and it never should have happened,” Fran Hong, a Democratic state representative running for governor, said of the shooting.

However, police stressed the incident remains under investigation and have begged the community not to jump to conclusions based on the video.

**What happened?**

Bystanders shared disturbing footage of the moment officers wrestled Ruiz to the ground before one of them shot him at close range. The violence unfolded Wednesday afternoon at the busy intersection of Madison’s South Baldwin and Williamson Streets around 1:34 p.m., an area surrounded by homes, bars, restaurants and shops.

The graphic video showed Ruiz, who police said was armed with a knife, being pinned to the ground by four officers after a pursuit on foot.

A “physical struggle” ensued as Ruiz managed to briefly stand up while in the grip of the officers, before one of them yelled, “Taser, Taser Taser” and pointed the stun gun at him. The Taser failed to discharge, police said.

Officers wrestled Ruiz back down to the ground while one of them appeared to step on his leg to keep him on the floor. Less than a second later, one of the officers pulled out his gun, pointed it at the victim, and fired three shots.

Ruiz was slumped on the ground and did not appear to move again.

A bystander’s screams were then heard nearby, as a woman yelled, “I saw everything, we watched it all.”

With the victim limp and face down on the ground, blood pouring from his head, the officer who shot him reholstered his weapon and then his placed the man’s hands behind his back as if to restrain him. Another officer removed the knife, throwing it to the side.

Two other officers were seen standing around the scene, before police put on gloves and appeared to administer first aid.

Ruiz was taken to the hospital, where he was pronounced dead.

## What police say happened

On Wednesday, Madison Police Chief John Patterson appealed for calm at a press conference in the aftermath, recounting the official narrative and pointing out that video of the shooting seen online only offered one side of the story.

Patterson also explained that Madison police officers do not wear body cameras, so there is no bodycam footage of the shooting.

In a statement, the department said officers were responding to a report alleging that someone had been seen attempting to break into parked cars in the city’s Marquette neighborhood.

![Madison Police Chief Patterson appealed for calm at a press conference in the aftermath, recounting the official narrative and pointing out that video of the shooting seen online only offered one side of the story](https://static.the-independent.com/2026/07/23/15/20/police-madison.png)

The officers pursued Ruiz, who was on a bicycle. “The man either fell or was pulled off the bicycle, this is still being determined, and a struggle ensued with officers in the middle of the intersection,” the statement said.

“During the struggle, the man pulled out a fix-blade knife and injured an officer. Officers continued to try to get the man into custody and a less lethal device, a taser, was used but it was unsuccessful.”

“The officer injured by the knife is the one who fired his weapon,” the statement continued. “Officers at the scene provided life-saving measures.”

A second officer was injured in the incident but police have not said how, only that they do not believe his wound was caused by the knife.

The four officers involved have since been placed on administrative leave according to procedure and the Wisconsin Department of Justice Division of Criminal Investigation has commenced an independent probe.

## What do we know about the victim?

Police have said Ruiz was in his 30s.

His race has not been disclosed by officials, but Black advocacy groups in Madison said they believed he was Black, as they denounced the violence.

“Another Black man is dead on a Madison street, and our community watched him die before we even knew his name,” said Brandi Grayson, the head of the Madison-based Black community advocacy group Urban Triage. “That is the cruelty of state violence.”

Ruiz’s death comes after the fatal shooting of Tony Robinson, who was biracial, at the hands of a white police officer on the same street in 2015. Large protests followed, but the district attorney did not file charges against police, determining the officer was justified.

## Who was the officer who shot the victim?

The name of the officer who shot the victim has not been released by Madison police.

In a statement, the department described him as “a veteran officer” and confirmed he has been placed on administrative leave, along with the other officers involved.

**How has the community reacted?**

Hundreds of people took to the streets Wednesday evening to protest the killing and to hold a candlelight vigil in memory of the victim.

Some stayed overnight. “I don’t plan to leave anytime soon because it’s important to hold this space,” said Ben Weger, a pastor of Stoughton United Methodist Church about 20 miles away.

Sabrina Thompson, of Madison, was crying at the memorial site. “It’s really unbearable,” she said through tears. “You can’t describe it.”

Hong, a Democratic primary candidates in Wisconsin’s governor’s race, also attended the vigil and spoke to demonstrators through a megaphone.

“This state sanctioned violence was an execution,” she told a crowd.

Incumbent Wisconsin Gov. Tony Evers called for “transparency and accountability” on X, expressing his sympathies for the victim’s family and saying that any protest should be conducted “peacefully and respectfully.”

“Each incident reopens deep wounds, raises difficult questions, and reminds us of the urgent work that remains to build trust, transparency, and accountability in our systems,” said Milwaukee County Executive David Crowley, who is also running for governor.

“There are questions that must be answered,” added Madison Mayor Satya Rhodes-Conway. “It’s understandable that people want answers now.”
