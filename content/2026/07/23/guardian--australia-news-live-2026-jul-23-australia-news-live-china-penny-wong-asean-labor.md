Husic says Labor should show ‘steel in its spine’ by raising taxes on gas exports

Dan Jervis-Bardy

The Labor MP Ed Husic says the federal government has the chance to show it has “steel in its spine” by raising taxes on gas exports – just hours after Anthony Albanese ruled out adopting the policy in the near future.

In a wide-ranging press conference on the sidelines of Labor’s national conference, Husic also backed an Indigenous truth-telling process and hinted he would push to strengthen the party’s criticism of Israel’s conduct in Gaza ahead of debate on Saturday.

Husic appeared alongside the Australia Institute co-chief executive Richard Denniss and Thomas Mayo, the assistant secretary of the Maritime Union of Australia, to renew calls for a 25% tax on gas exports.

Labor conference delegates are set to vote on Thursday to update the party’s platform with a commitment to extract a “fairer return” from the nation’s resources, a set of words widely interpreted as setting an expectation that the government would eventually move to raise taxes on gas exports.

Albanese rejected that interpretation in a radio interview earlier this month, ruling out any changes in the short-term as the government focuses on shoring up fuel supplies amid the Middle East conflict.

But Husic said the government would eventually have to act, such was the groundswell of public support for the policy.

He said:

Labor has a chance to be able to show, particularly to the broader public, that it’s got what it takes, that it’s got the steel in the spine to be able to make a call that’s in really the nation’s long-term interest. A lot of Australians reckon we’ve been ripped off. Labor can stand up, seize this moment, seize the money (and) also seize the agenda long term.

Jim Chalmers praises economy’s ‘resilience’ amid strong job figures

Jim Chalmers has hailed this morning’s strong jobs figures as evidence of the economy’s “resilience” through a period of great global turbulence.

Official figures this morning showed the unemployment rate held at 4.4% in June, alongside a much stronger than expected 76,300 monthly increase in the number of employed Australians.

The participation rate - which measures the share of people aged 15 and over in the labour force - climbed to 67% and the highest in nearly a year, which explained why the jobs surge did not translate into a lower jobless rate.

“Even in the face of all this global uncertainty and volatility, unemployment remains relatively low and more jobs are being created and that reflects the resilience of Australia’s economy and the progress we’re making together,” the treasurer said, even as he recognised that “people are under pressure” from high cost of living.

Still, economists predict unemployment will creep higher this year as the economy slows, and with inflation still running too hot, the resilient labour market will keep the Reserve Bank’s finger on the interest rate-hike trigger.

Cherelle Murphy, EY’s chief economist, said that this morning’s data “will not materially change the policy picture” for the RBA:

A labour market that is only gradually cooling gives the [RBA’s] board more flexibility to keep rates higher for longer, or raise them further if needed, to tame inflation.

‘I felt I had failed as a parent’: Father details impact of swastika graffiti on family home and Bondi terror attack

Former NSW Jewish Board of Deputies president, Lesli Berger, described the impact antisemitic targeted attacks have had on his family during testimony to the royal commission.

Berger recalled the shock of finding an inverted Nazi swastika graffitied on his back fence in late December 2024. The incident left his children feeling unsafe, prompting Berger to spend tens of thousands of dollars on security camera upgrades, remove his address from public records, and lock down his social media.

It’s the symbol of hatred of the political movement that wanted the extermination of the Jewish people. It’s the political movement that killed most of my family on my father’s side.

The emotional toll escalated during the Bondi terrorist attack at a Hanukkah event. His two younger children, aged 16 and 12, were home alone when news of the attack broke. Fearing their home would be targeted, the children panicked. His son called a non-Jewish friend, whose mother immediately drove over to evacuate the teenagers to her house for safety.

Fighting back tears during his testimony, Berger said the moment made him feel like he had “failed as a parent”:

He didn’t feel safe... I couldn’t believe it. Understanding everything that I had done to help the Jewish community... at that moment, I felt that it really was all for naught.

Berger told the commission that public discussions around antisemitism often overlook the trauma inflicted on families who are not direct victims of physical violence.

It has meant that as other high-profile antisemitic attacks have happened, we have felt it more keenly.

First witnesses at NSW Liberals corruption inquiry revealed

The New South Wales corruption watchdog has revealed the witnesses who will appear in the first of at least eight weeks of hearings into allegations of corruption in the state’s Liberal party.

The Independent Commission Against Corruption (Icac) will hold its first public hearings as part of Operation Rosny from Monday.

After an opening statement by counsel assisting the commission Dr Peggy Dwyer SC, the first witness will be hotelier Michael O’Hara, followed later in the week by former Liberal staffer Dylan Whitelaw, and Robert Assaf.

Icac is investigating the central allegation that Liberal figures or lobbyists including Assaf “solicited or accepted” donations, including from prohibited donors, in amounts which were undeclared or exceeded caps, to recruit or renew members to the party – a practice known as “branch stacking”.

As part of this, Icac is investigating donations allegedly made by O’Hara’s company Paslibdan Pty Ltd on his behalf.

Dylan Whitelaw, a former Liberal parliamentary staffer, was summonsed but did not appear before a parliamentary inquiry into allegations of branch stacking in the Hills Shire council which preceded Icac’s inquiry. He has not been named in Icac’s allegations.

O’Hara, Assaf and Whitelaw have been contacted for comment.

Conyer recounted an interfaith dialogue event, where a participant told her the term “proud Zionist” was viewed by some as equivalent to “proud terrorist”.

Rabbi details surge in antisemitism since 7 October

Ima Caldwell

Rabbi Allison Conyer has detailed an “exponential” surge in antisemitism across Australian demographics since 7 October 2023, the day of the Hamas attack on Israel, saying that members of her community are increasingly alienated from progressive and social justice spaces.

Speaking on the escalation of public harassment, Conyer cited accounts of drivers shouting “we’re hunting Jews” at pedestrians, swastikas painted on property and a customer telling a pharmacist “you can’t trust a Jew” while bystanders did nothing.

Conyer outlined the toll on staff and synagogues across the Union for Progressive Judaism network – a movement she said is defined by its modern interpretation of traditions, ordination of female rabbis, welcoming of interfaith families and LGBTQIA+ inclusion.

Almost all progressive synagogues in the region have faced bomb threats in recent years, she said. She recalled an example from a few weeks ago where a non-Jewish staff member answered a phone call where the caller declared “all Jews should be burned”.

Conyer expressed particular concern over hostility emerging within left-wing and social justice circles where congregants previously felt safe. She recounted the experience of a queer congregant who felt squeezed out of inclusive spaces:

In a community that’s fighting binaries, she expressed that she felt there was a binary... Whenever she tried to express just a difference of opinion, she was shut down and cancelled. This community that made her feel so safe now became a den of bigotry.

Conyer also pointed to a left-wing student targeted as “Zionist scum”, stating:

I give these two examples because this is blatant antisemitism hiding itself and using Zionism as the excuse.

Conyer highlighted breakdown of personal relationships across Australia.

Another thing that people told me over and over again is the loss of lifelong friendships … It was so bizarre that in a political situation outside of Australia, friendships were ended. There was no agree to disagree. There was no distinction between the actions of the Israeli government and all of Israel and all the Jewish people.

Workers to paint ‘size of a typical family home’ to cover up ‘Pam the Bird’ on Melbourne’s Bolte Bridge

A divisive bird in Melbourne that is art to some and vandalism to others will be no more. ‘Pam the Bird’, described by Victoria Police as a “distinct cartoon bird”, was allegedly plastered onto one of the Bolte Bridge pillars by Jack Gibson-Burrell earlier this month. He was arrested at the base of the bridge several hours later, after a considerable police response and traffic delays.

It’s about 200 square metres of painting. So to give you a better context, we’re painting an area at the size of a typical family home … It wasn’t an option to clean it off, so we actually are painting over the top of it.

Our duty is to make Melbourne look great … to make sure that we keep it clean and free from graffiti.

Rabbi says security costs and rising antisemitism forcing synagogues online

Ima Caldwell

Turning for a moment to the royal commission into antisemitism. Testifying today was Rabbi Allison Conyer, of the Union for Progressive Judaism, who said routine threats are disrupting daily life across the country’s network of progressive synagogues.

She outlined the financial and operational toll security threats are taking on Australian synagogues. “Many of our communities have received, in fact, almost all of them have received bomb threats at one point or another over the last couple of years,” Conyer told the royal commission, noting that places of worship and schools now regularly practice “evacuation and lockdown drills in case of a terrorist threat”.

The financial burden of providing physical security reaches up to $50,000 per synagogue. Conyer said that government grant processes are onerous for volunteer-run groups, and available funding quickly runs out.

I know one of our communities in Australia – because they don’t have enough money for security – has closed having in-person Sabbath services... And they’re only doing it online. So it’s back to Covid again.

Rabbi Allison Conyer arrives at the Melbourne hearings of the royal commission on Thursday. Photograph: James Ross/AAP

Security presence is also taking a psychological toll on families and children, driving up to a 25% drop in enrolment at some synagogue preschools and threatening their closure, she said.

Our extracurricular activity programs, some people are nervous because they see security guards.

How do you explain to a kid why you need a security guard, someone dressed like that, yet you’re still safe.

Conyer said that a homeless service run by her synagogue lost volunteers who felt unsafe after security grant funding was denied, describing an environment where security fears continually curtail community connection.
