If your kitchen knives have gotten to a point where they’re so dull you have to practically saw your way through a tomato, then it’s time to show them some tender, loving care. Keeping kitchen knives sharp is an important, but often ignored part of cooking at home. While it’s tempting to simply toss out cheap, dull knives (in a safe manner, of course) and buy new ones, learning how to sharpen your knives is a valuable skill that will save you money in the long run.

There are several ways to keep your favourite knives sharp. For best results, you can send them to a knife-sharpening service where an expert can restore the blade to almost new condition. You can send your knives by post or drop them off in person, or even book a mobile knife sharpening service that will come to your home.

However, this isn’t always ideal if you don’t have the budget for it or can’t be without your knives for a period of time – which is especially inconvenient if you cook most days of the week. So having a knife sharpening tool at home is really useful, as it also allows you to sharpen your knives on a regular basis. Sharp knives mean fewer kitchen accidents; dull knives lead you to put more downwards pressure onto the ingredient you are trying to slice, raising the risk of the blade slipping and therefore injuring yourself.

Whetstones are a traditional method for sharpening knives, having been around for thousands of years. These days, many whetstones come in the form of a rectangular block that needs to be soaked in water before you can start using it. German knife makers Wüsthof have several versions of whetstones that feature two sides, one with a coarse grit and the other with a fine grit. Each version has different grit levels and different functions, from heavy duty resharpening to fine-tuning.

The 1000/3000 grit version is my pick for everyday knife maintenance. Whetstones are a very gentle way of maintaining knives as they tend to shave off less steel compared to other tools, but they also require you to learn the right technique in order to get the best result. I would recommend looking up tutorials before starting, as I did. Once I got the hang of the angle at which to hold the knife and how to pull it over the stones, I found it produced a fine, sharp edge – but it does take quite a bit of time and practice.

However, whetstones are a great way to keep knives sharp and in good shape for longer, and are friendlier for most types of knives, even finer Japanese knives.
