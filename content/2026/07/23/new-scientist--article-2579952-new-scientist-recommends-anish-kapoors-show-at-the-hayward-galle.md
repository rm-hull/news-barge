*Mount Moriah at the Gate of the Ghetto*

Anish Kapoor’s latest exhibition at London’s Hayward Gallery explicitly invokes religious themes, but for me, it also raises the kind of questions about reality and the nature of space-time that scientists immerse themselves in.

Take his gigantic, 4.5-tonne *Mount Moriah at the Gate of the Ghetto*, named for the biblical mountain in Genesis where God commanded Abraham to sacrifice his son. Kapoor inverts the mountain, suspends it from the ceiling of the gallery and soaks it in shining red pigment that looks like blood and exposed flesh – or is it lava?

Viewing the piece, you feel as if you have been transported to another world, or even ventured inside a living planet. To me, it was also a reminder of the ancient relationship between geology and biology.

Another room contains Kapoor’s piece *Ha Makom*, which takes the form of an immense rocky, crimson landscape with a rectangular black portal in the centre. The work draws on the presence of the divine, according to Kapoor. The title derives from a phrase meaning “the place” in Hebrew and is also influenced by Kapoor’s visits to Uluru in central Australia, which he has described as “the most religious place I’ve ever been to”. The black void looks like an alien artefact, like the monolith in* 2001: A Space Odyssey*. I found myself longing to enter the portal to see what is on the other side.

*Ha Makom* is one of several pieces involving the wonder pigment Vantablack, a substance made from carbon nanotubes that absorbs 99.96 per cent of light. With Vantablack, Kapoor creates voids and what appear to be rifts in space-time. The effect is to leave us dizzy and confused; it’s a taste of what a physicist might experience when trying to understand the fabric of reality.

Other works showcase Kapoor’s use of polished steel, making massive curved, reflective surfaces that not only defy inspection, but seemingly Newtonian laws too. One convex, tinted, circular mirror appears to be a flaw or pore in reality, and you find yourself staring into it and feeling a deep sense of both mystery and longing.

And then there are Kapoor’s startling, even shocking, sculptures representing human organs. Some are contained in massive transparent plastic wraps, bulging and dripping with blood. Others are piled on huge steel trays. To describe them as visceral is to seriously understate things.

This must-see exhibition provokes awe, wonder, laughter and revulsion. Kapoor, like all our greatest artists, depicts his understanding of the world, of reality, in ways that complement scientific investigations. The show runs until 18 October 2026.
