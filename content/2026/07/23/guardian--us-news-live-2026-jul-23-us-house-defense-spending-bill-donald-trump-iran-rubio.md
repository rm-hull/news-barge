Outcry after $1.15tn defense bill passes in the House – US politics live

Donald Trump’s decision to attack Iran without first seeking congressional approval has sparked further controversy over the National Defense Authorization Act

Outcry over the $1.15tn defense policy bill passed in the House

Hello and welcome to our live coverage of US politics.

The National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) typically passes in Congress each year with bipartisan margins. But the $1.15tn defense policy billpassed in a near-party-line vote in the House of Representatives on Wednesday has lawmakers on both sides of the aisle in an uproar.

That’s because the NDAA’s path to enactment this year has been complicated by the president’s decision to join Israel in attacking Iran without first seeking authorization from Congress – a war that has already cost $37.5bn, according to defense secretary Pete Hegseth. Last week, Senate Democratsblocked the advancement of that chamber’s version of the NDAA in protest of the war.

“While millions of Americans are struggling to afford basic necessities, Congress is choosing to authorize over $1tn for the Pentagon, once again funneling billions of taxpayer dollars to defense contractors instead of investing in the needs of working people,” Ilhan Omar, a Democratic representative from Minnesota, posted on X.

She continued: “The American people are sick and tired of funding atrocities abroad and want this war with Iran to end. Yet year after year, the Pentagon fails its audit with virtually no accountability, while Congress continues to increase military spending.”

Further complicating matters, Mike Johnson, the Republican House speaker, acceded to the demands of rightwing lawmakers and inserted into the legislation the Save America Act, a Trump-backed proposal to ban mail-in ballots and impose new identification restrictions on voters at the polls and when they register. “This budget resolution is our best shot at enacting as much of the Save America Act into law as possible,” Johnson -posted on X.

“This means the NDAA is now tied to Trump’s attempt to steal elections by making it more difficult for millions of Americans to vote,” Jerry Nadler, a Democratic representative from New York, posted on X. “The NDAA is a costly, bad bill that funds an illegal war and undermines our democracy.”

Section 219 of the NDAA – which establishes the US-Israel Defense Technology Cooperation Initiative – also had Republican representatives protesting the bill’s passage. “Section 219 is a betrayal of our sovereignty,” Kentucky conservative Thomas Massiesaid on X. Far-right firebrand Lauren Boebert also said she sat out on the NDAA vote because of Section 219.

Today in the House, Democratic lawmakers are hoping to pass a war powers resolution to try and curb the Trump administration’s military action in Iran, ahead of the lower chamber’s August reccess.

This will be the first vote on a resolution since the fragile ceasefire between Washington and Iran collapsed earlier this month.

A reminder that both chambers passed war powers resolutions in June, with little acknowledgment from the White House. This week, Pete Hegseth said that Donald Trump had complete authority continue the conflict with Iran, while he asked Congress for supplemental funding for the war.

“Let me be clear: This body has already spoken. The War Powers Resolution passed with bipartisan support in the House and the Senate. The Trump administration was required to end its hostilities against Iran. It has not,” said Representative Gregory Meeks, the top Democrat on the House armed services committee.

Meanwhile, the Senate is also preparing to hold a vote on its own war powers resolution today.

Global oil prices have risen tomore than $100 per barrel for the first time since May as violence escalates in the Middle East.

Brent crude, the global benchmark, increased by more than 6% after Iran’s Houthi allies attacked shipping in the Red Sea.

Donald Trump said he would exact “major military punishment” against the Yemen-based group as well as Iran, which he said bears responsibility for the Red Sea attack.

In a short while the Senate health, education, labor and pensions committee will also vote onErica Schwartz to be new Centers of Disease Control (CDC) director. The beleaguered agency hasn’t had a dedicated leader since its last director, Susan Monarez, was pushed out by health secretary Robert F Kennedy Jr in August last year.

The committee will also vote on Keith Sonderling to be the new labor secretary, after Lori Chavez-DeRemer resigned amid a series of misconduct allegations – including having an affair with a subordinate and drinking on the job.

Provided the committee confirms their nominations, consideration for Schwartz and Sonderling will then head to the wider Senate for a full vote.

Donald Trump is in Washington today. He’ll be at the Environmental Protection Agency at 2:30pm ET to talk about the ratepayer protection pledge – an agreement signed a pledge by major tech companies that says they will bear the cost of the electricity generation required to power their datacenters. Experts, however, have called the pledge “toothless” and question how it will be enforced.

Later, the president will welcome the Los Angeles Dodgers, the World Series baseball champions, to the White House at 4:30pm ET.

In addition to passing the National Defense Authorization Act – a key plank of the president’s plan to spend $1.5tn on the military in the 2027 fiscal year – House Republicans on Wednesday agreed to begin the process of writing a $95bn bill to fund the war with Iran.

The passage of this budget resolution paves the way for the creation of a reconciliation bill, which can advance through the Senate under special rules that circumvent the Democratic filibuster and requires only a simple majority to pass.

Like the NDAA, the budget resolution will also likely be used by the House administration committee to pass the Save America Act into law, which will ban mail-in ballots nationwide and impose enhanced strict identification requirements on voters when they cast ballots and register.

The House passed the National Defense Authorization Act in a 216-212 vote, with all but six Democrats opposing its passage and seven Republicans breaking ranks to oppose the measure.

Josh Brecheen of Oklahoma. “While I support providing for a robust national defense, I could not in good conscience vote for this legislation as it authorizes a $263.4bn (29.6%) increase over the authorized level for the previous fiscal year,” Brecheen posted on X.
