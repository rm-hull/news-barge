## 'I feel incredibly lucky' - York woman's experience after pioneering medical trial

Two years after completing treatment, tests continue to show no evidence of cancer and Christine has been able to continue travelling and seeing family *(Image: Provided)*

A YORK woman has shared the ‘huge difference’ that pioneering clinical research has made in treating her cancer without the need for invasive surgery.

Christine Skinner, a 69-year-old retired professor and former nurse and midwife from York, has praised the APHRODITE trial – funded in part by Yorkshire Cancer Research – for treating her rectal cancer.

She was diagnosed in early 2024 after noticing mucus and changes to her bowel habits, with further investigations uncovering the disease.

After discussing the side effects and potential impact of surgery - including the possibility of living with a temporary or permanent stoma bag - Christine decided to take part in the APHRODITE trial.

The trial, which was conducted by the University of Leeds Clinical Trials Research Unit, saw her receive a higher dose of radiotherapy being tested in the study as she underwent treatment at St James's University Hospital in Leeds.

She was monitored throughout by specialist teams - receiving ‘very few side effects’, which resolved shortly after her radiotherapy finished.

Christine said: "I remember walking into the hospital with my friend feeling incredibly nervous. When I was told the cancer had spread to my lymph nodes, I was frightened.

“Like many people, I immediately started questioning myself. You wonder whether you should have acted sooner, even though my symptoms weren't the more commonly recognised signs of rectal cancer, such as bleeding.

  Christine Skinner took part in the APHRODITE trial after being diagnosed with rectal cancer in early 2024 

*(Image: Provided)*

"Other than physically going to the hospital each day for radiotherapy, I wouldn't really have known I was undergoing cancer treatment. The only side effects I experienced came towards the end of my treatment, but they were soon resolved.”

She also praised the standard of care received from everyone, adding that they were ‘outstanding’, made her feel ‘supported and reassured’ and that the quality and professionalism made her feel ‘incredibly proud of the NHS’.

Two years later, Christine is in remission – with tests showing no sign of cancer - and is able to live life as normal: travelling, spending time with her family, and enjoying her retirement. She continues to have scans to confirm the cancer has not returned.

She said: "I've had as normal a quality of life as I could possibly have hoped for. My cancer diagnosis hasn't stopped me travelling, exercising or doing the things that matter to me.

“I’ve just welcomed a grandchild into the world and can be an active hands-on grandma thanks, in part, to this trial. I feel incredibly lucky.”

"If I'd been diagnosed 10 years earlier, my treatment options might have looked very different and the opportunity to take part in a clinical trial like APHRODITE may not have existed.

“The boundaries are being pushed all the time and that's because of research taking place here in Yorkshire. I'm incredibly grateful I had the opportunity to take part. It happened at exactly the right time for me, and it has made a huge difference to my life.”

  A retired professor and former nurse and midwife, Christine sought medical advice from her GP after noticing changes to her bowel habits. 

*(Image: Provided)*

Professor Ane Appelt, co-lead of APHRODITE and Visiting Professor in Radiotherapy Physics at the University of Leeds, said that the trial had allowed doctors to explore radiotherapy options for people who surgery is not an option and let them receive innovative treatment while maintaining their quality of life.

She said: "The trial was developed in response to challenges we were seeing in clinical practice and the needs of people affected by cancer in Yorkshire.

“We worked closely with people with cancer to shape the trial, from designing information materials to improving communication throughout the study. Their insights helped ensure the research remained focused on the needs and issues that matter most to people undergoing cancer treatment.

“This work was able to happen thanks to support from Yorkshire Cancer Research, which gave me the time and freedom to pursue new ideas and develop research at the University of Leeds alongside my clinical responsibilities at St James’s University Hospital.”

Dr Simon Gollins, Co-lead of APHRODITE and Consultant in Clinical Oncology at North Wales Cancer Treatment Centre, added: “In the APHRODITE trial we saw higher rates of tumour disappearance with a small increase in side effects.

“This is an important step towards offering treatment that is both more effective and gentle for people with a rectal cancer diagnosis. The APHRODITE trial is one of the first studies to show that increasing the radiotherapy dose using standard NHS equipment can significantly improve how rectal cancer responds to treatment.

  Professor Ane Appelt 

*(Image: Provided)*

“If longer-term findings confirm these results, this approach could help expand treatment options, offering more people the chance to avoid major surgery while maintaining their quality of life.”

Dr Stuart Griffiths, Director of Research, Policy and Impact at Yorkshire Cancer Research said: “The APHRODITE trial is a clear example of how Yorkshire Cancer Research is helping to make Yorkshire a research-active region. By working with pioneering cancer experts, the charity is bringing innovative clinical trials to people in our region, so they can be among the first to benefit from the latest breakthroughs in cancer treatment.

“Research shows people treated at hospitals where high levels of research take place often do better than those treated in hospitals with little or no clinical research. That is why we are committed to working alongside hospitals and universities across the region to make Yorkshire a thriving hub for cancer research.

“As Christine’s experience demonstrates, access to innovative treatments through clinical trials can help people live longer, healthier, cancer-free lives.”
