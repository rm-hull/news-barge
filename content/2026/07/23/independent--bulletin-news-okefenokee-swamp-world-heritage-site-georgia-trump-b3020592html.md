The Okefenokee swamp is poised to join awe-inspiring U.S. landscapes including the Grand Canyon and the Everglades as the nation’s 27th World Heritage Site (AP Photo/Stephen B. Morton)

The honor faces strong resistance from local residents in the surrounding conservative counties of Ware, Charlton and Clinch, who express fears that the United Nations will gain control over their land and infringe on property rights.

Local officials, including Ware County Commissioner Barry Cox, cite constituent concerns about land seizure and UN rule, with some opposition fueled by anti-UNESCO groups and a Republican primary question that critics say was misleadingly worded.

The U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service, which manages the Okefenokee National Wildlife Refuge, clarifies that World Heritage Site designation does not transfer ownership or management authority to the United Nations, but rather increases global significance while the refuge remains under USFWS control.

The resistance is rooted in strong localism and an “America First” sentiment, with political scientists noting parallels to historical anti-federal or anti-foreign sentiment, and concerns about the influence of countries like China and Russia within the UN.
