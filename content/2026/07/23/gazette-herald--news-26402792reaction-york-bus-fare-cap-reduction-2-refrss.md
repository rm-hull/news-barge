A RETURNING reduced fare cap will give people "even more reason to catch the bus", according to the Mayor of York and North Yorkshire, and local bus campaign groups have backed the plans announced by Downing Street yesterday.

Single bus fares in England will be capped at £2 from January, after a nationwide ceiling that ran from January 2023 to December 2024 was increased to £3 by the Government from January 1, 2025.

Announcing the plans yesterday (Wednesday, July 22), Prime Minister Andy Burnham said good affordable transport links are essential, and no-one should be priced out and left behind.

  


The restored cap will be backed by £454 million in funding and remain in place through to 2027, the government announced.

Reinstating the £2 maximum fare will partly be funded by switching grants for international climate projects to repayable loans instead.

David Skaith, Mayor of York and North Yorkshire, said the scheme would benefit people across the region.

He said: "This is great news that will help save people money and give them more reasons to take the bus whether it’s to get to work, school, a hospital appointment or spend time with friends and family.

"It’s going to make a huge difference to the price of fares.

"Alongside our £1 fare for under-19s this is going to make a real difference to people's pockets at a time when many of our residents need it more than ever."

Local bus campaign group York Bus Forum welcomed the return to the £2 fare cap, with a spokesperson saying: "This has previously been shown to encourage bus use which benefits everyone by reducing traffic congestion.

"In particular, we are pleased that the cap will be in place until at least the end of next year, since longer-term interventions make it much easier to cater for an increase in passenger numbers."

Many of the single-use multi-stop journeys within York are likely to be affected by the new cap, as will single journeys to places such as Whitby, Scarborough, Hull, Richmond and Grassington which will see single fares reduce by one-third.

Chancellor of the Exchequer John Healey said: "This action to ease the cost of living is funded by savings made elsewhere, so there’s no burden to British taxpayers, just pounds going back into their pockets."
