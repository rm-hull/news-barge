## 'Like playing on concrete': York area football clubs issue stark pitch warning

Tadcaster Albion's chairman Andy Charlesworth says clubs are paying a growing price to keep pitches playable. *(Image: Image: David Lindsay)*

Football clubs near York have warned that increasingly hot, dry weather is leaving pitches ‘like concrete’, increasing injuries and forcing clubs to spend thousands to make them safe.

Pocklington Town AFC and Tadcaster Albion have told an investigation by not-for-profit Round Our Way about pitch issues that swing from being waterlogged in winter to baked hard during prolonged dry spells.

They are among a number of coaches and club officials across the country who say the harder surfaces can leave players suffering greater impact on joints and increase the risk of injury while placing significant financial pressure on stretched grassroots organisations.

  York area football clubs issue stark pitch weather warning - including Tadcaster Albion which has often been struck by flooding from the local river, leaving the pitch unplayable at times. 

*(Image: Tadcaster Albion)*

The Clubs Against Floods campaign has joined calls for the government to help clubs adapt and build resilience with proactive, rather than reactive, funding so they can focus on growing the game and getting more people playing.

Campaign coordinator Freddie Daley said: "As the UK and Ireland gear up to host the UEFA EURO 2028, the government should also be looking at how the legacy of the tournament can deliver greater investment and resilience for the grassroots game."

Richard Bower, secretary of Pocklington Town AFC, said: "We've invested years and thousands of pounds improving our pitches, but two extremely hot summers virtually wiped out much of that progress.

"Last August we had to postpone a tournament because the pitches were rock hard and there was almost no grass cover left."

Roger Harding, director of Round Our Way said: “It’s worrying that so many grassroots clubs are struggling with our weather yo-yoing from extreme rain to extreme heat thanks to climate change.

"Sports clubs are at the heart of local communities so we need solutions that will keep players safer from injuries and protect club finances. Perhaps it’s time for more of the vast money made at elite level to be given to local clubs surviving on a shoe string.”

Last year, Pocklington Town AFC had to postpone part of an annual tournament after pitches became so hard and grass cover so sparse that club officials considered them unsafe, with one player suffering a serious elbow injury during the event.

Clubs say increasingly extreme weather is also creating significant financial pressures with many spending more on irrigation, fertilisers and specialist pitch treatments as grass struggles to recover during increasingly dry summers.

Andy Charlesworth is chairman of Tadcaster Albion FC, which was founded in 1892 and whose home ground is next to the River Wharfe, meaning it has often been under water, leaving the club with mounting bills.

He said: "We've gone from worrying about frozen and flooded pitches in winter to trying to keep grass alive in summer. Last year we ran sprinklers 24 hours a day for six weeks, costing around £2,000 in water alone, yet the pitches were still rock hard. It's like playing football on concrete. Clubs are spending hundreds of pounds more on fertiliser and maintenance just to stay afloat."

Andy says Tadcaster have to spend an additional £750 on fertiliser every time drought conditions damage grass growth, while treatments such as to drain the pitch when it becomes waterlogged can cost around £800. The club now applies fertiliser three times a year compared with just once annually five years ago.
