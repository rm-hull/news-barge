Search the Public Notice PortalA former RAF base near York is “simply the wrong place” to house asylum seekers, the country’s mayor has said, after the government announced the plans are back on the table.

Labour’s mayor for York and North Yorkshire, David Skaith, criticised the Home Office’s proposals to house large numbers of asylum seekers at RAF Linton-on-Ouse, saying the site should instead be developed into permanent housing.

He said housing asylum seekers at the former RAF base would put the site's future development “on hold and further delays the homes, jobs and economic investment that local people have been waiting years to see”.

  York and North Yorkshire mayor David Skaith 

*(Image: Supplied)*

“With a serious shortage of housing across our county, RAF Linton-on-Ouse is one of our biggest opportunities to help meet local need and support economic growth,” Mr Skaith said.

The mayor said it was “frustrating” that, years after the site closed in 2020, its “economic potential remains unrealised”.

“Local people are rightly asking why a site capable of delivering thousands of homes is still sitting idle while housing pressures continue to grow.”

Mr Skaith said he will be “making it clear to North Yorkshire Council, the Home Office and the Ministry of Defence that RAF Linton-on-Ouse should be used to build homes, not house asylum seekers”.

  Campaigners in Linton-on-Ouse in 2022

It comes after the Home Office last week announced it is seeking planning permission to use RAF Linton-on-Ouse to house asylum seekers as part of a government effort to reduce the use of hotels as accommodation.

Plans to house 1,500 asylum seekers at the base in Linton-on-Ouse were previously considered in 2022, but the controversial scheme was abandoned following local opposition and a legal challenge from the council.

## Bringing more than 1,000 extra people into area could overwhelm local emergency services, says deputy mayor

York and North Yorkshire’s deputy mayor for policing, fire and crime, Jo Coles, said she was concerned that bringing more than 1,000 extra people into the small village could overwhelm the abilities of the emergency services to manage the area.

“The safety of the public and wider community cohesion must be at the heart of any decision to house asylum seekers anywhere,” she said. “The scale of this proposal, combined with the site's location and limited infrastructure, raises serious and legitimate concerns about the impact on policing, emergency services and wider community safety.”

  York and North Yorkshire's deputy mayor for policing fire and crime, Jo Coles 

*(Image: Gavin Priestley)*

The deputy mayor added that “North Yorkshire Police, North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue service and other local partners agencies must have confidence that they have the resources and operational capacity required to respond effectively to any increased risks”.

Ms Coles said she has written to the asylum minister and the chief constable of North Yorkshire Police to “seek urgent assurances on all of these public safety issues and the resourcing implications for council tax payers”.

In addition to RAF Linton-on-Ouse, the government is proposing to house a total of 3,750 asylum seekers at Ministry of Defence sites in Bicester in Oxfordshire and Barnham in Suffolk.

The government is also looking to extend the use of existing sites in Crowborough, East Sussex until 2030 and Wethersfield in Essex) beyond 2027.

The initiative comes in the wake of a significant decrease in the number of asylum seekers temporarily housed in UK hotels.

According to Home Office figures published in May, the number stood at 20,885, marking a 35 per cent drop from the previous year.

At its peak, in September 2023, the number of asylum seekers in hotels was as high as 56,018.

The issue of housing asylum seekers in hotels became controversial last year, sparking protests outside some sites.

Last week the Home Office announced the closure of 20 more hotels that were previously used to house asylum seekers.

This move is part of a broader strategy to shift from using hotels to former military sites for accommodating asylum seekers.

Labour has pledged to halt the use of asylum hotels by the next election.

Border security and asylum minister Alex Norris said: "We promised to close every asylum hotel and hand them back to communities, and that is exactly what we are doing.

"Twenty more hotels have closed, and hotel numbers have more than halved since their peak.

"Instead, we’re moving asylum seekers into ex-military sites that are a far cry from the hotels the last government left us with.

"This is a system being brought back under control – and we will not stop until the job is done."
