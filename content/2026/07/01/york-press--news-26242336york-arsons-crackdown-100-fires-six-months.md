The police and fire service have united to crackdown on arsons in York after more than 100 incidents of fires being started deliberately in the city over the past six months.

North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service’s watch manager, Simon Wray, said the spate of arsons has meant longer waits at the scenes of serious emergencies, as crews work to extinguish small fires elsewhere.

“What may seem just like an innocuous fire in the middle of a field – people might not think it’s going to spread – that still requires resources to attend,” he said.

“In the area if there is a house fire, someone needs rescuing or if there is a road traffic collision and its serious … unfortunately they are going to be waiting longer for those resources to arrive because they’re tied up at these anti-social behaviour fires.

“We’ve really got to stress to people who are taking part in this activity, they are directly putting the lives of their community at risk.”

  North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service watch manager Simon Wray, right, with his crew at Scarcroft Road allotments in York on Tuesday (June 30) 

*(Image: Dylan Connell)*

Speaking from the allotments at Scarcroft Road, which, like Clifton Backies, St Nicks nature reserve and areas of Tang Hall, has been targeted by arsons, Mr Wray said it was mainly groups of young people behind the fires.

He said the fires are “popping up quite frequently across the city”, which has resulted in the emergency services working to address the issue.

It comes after a similar crackdown by the fire service and police last year, which both services said had a positive outcome.

  Temporary police sergeant Ben Ambler with PCSO Sue Court at Scarcroft Road allotments in York on Tuesday (June 30) 

*(Image: Dylan Connell)*

Temporary police sergeant Ben Ambler, from North Yorkshire Police, said children as young as 10 to 17 were involved in setting the fires.

He said some of the children involved in the arsons have been referred for education around the dangers of fires, while others had faced enforcement action from police.

Sgt Ambler said “multiple different groups across the city” are involved in the fires, which showed more needed to be done to educate children early on about the dangers.

  Police and firefighters patrol Scarcroft Road allotments on Tuesday (June 30) after announcing a crackdown on arsons in York 

*(Image: Dylan Connell)*

He urged people to “think about the risks” when considering starting a fire, adding: “It might seem fun. It might seem exciting. But there are a lot of risks around it.

“A couple of months ago we had a young person who ended up in the hospital with really serious burns because they were suspected of starting a deliberate fire at an allotment. They have to live with the injuries as a result of that – from a couple of seconds of starting a fire."

Keeley Smith, youth and school engagement manager for North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service, said children had started setting fires due to boredom and peer pressure.

“Young people are bored – there’s not as much youth provision anymore,” she said.

“They’re egging each other on and they are looking for things to do on an evening.”

  Keeley Smith, youth and school engagement manager for North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service 

*(Image: Dylan Connell)*

Ms Smith said the fire service’s work with schools to make children aware of the dangers early on has been beneficial.

“I think it’s all about early intervention – it’s about getting there before the behaviour escalates,” she said.

“Sometimes when there is fire setting, there are other behaviours that are involved as well.”

She added: “Any parents or guardians need to sit down with their children and talk to them about the risks of this kind of behaviour.”

  *Anyone concerned about a young person playing with fire they can complete a FireSafe referral on* *North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service’s website.*
