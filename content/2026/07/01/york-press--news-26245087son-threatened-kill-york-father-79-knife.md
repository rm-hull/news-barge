A SON held a knife to his 79-year-old father’s throat in his own home and told him: “This time I am going to kill you, you ……”, York Crown Court heard.

Richard Manners' father was petrified and believed his 48-year-old son would stab him, said Calum McNicholas, prosecuting.

His dad had allowed his son to move in with him and his wife when Manners had problems with his job and his previous partner.

“I don’t feel safe in my own home,” the father said in a personal statement. “I am absolutely terrified of my son. I don’t want him in my home.”

Defence barrister Rachel Webster said the son was suffering from depression and anxiety, was on medication and likely to commit impulsive acts linked to his use of alcohol.

“He needs to get off his backside and get a job,” the Recorder of York, Judge Sean Morris, said. “That will sort his mental health out.”

Manners, now of the Changing Lives hostel for the homeless in York, pleaded guilty to threatening a person with a knife in a private place.

“He does have a grudge against his father,” said the judge. He deferred sentence for six months until January 6 so that Manners could show that he could change.

“Everything is in your own hands whether you go to prison or not. It is entirely down to you,” said the judge. “You are a skilled man, you can do it, instead of moping in your room for three years drinking yourself silly.”

He ordered Manners to get a long-term job, sort out his drinking problems, make significant progress in getting permanent accommodation and stay on his medication.

If he did all that, the judge said he would probably suspend the prison sentence. He also forbid the son to contact either parent, go to the street in north York where they live or any other address they have.

Mr McNicholas said the father had had to go to his doctor several times recently and believed his ill health was down to the son’s behaviour.

On a day between March 15 and April 14 last year, the son had held a knife two inches from the father's throat, pointed at the father and said: "This time I am going to kill you, you ......"

The father's dog growled and the son backed away.

Ms Webster said the son and father had “never got on” and for no acceptable reason, matters had escalated until the son got the knife.

He had lost his job and accommodation in Derby and then moved back in with his parents. That meant he had lost his independence and had effectively, in his forties, to start all over again.

He was now working with Changing Lives to better himself and his employment chances and find his own accommodation.
