## York's much-missed Little Admiral is coming home at last – but there's one sad catch

Painstaking repairs have been carried out to the Little Admiral. Photo supplied

YORK'S smallest and quirkiest landmark is poised to make a welcome return – but in a different guise and in a new location.

The Little Admiral – the carved wooden figurine that has sat atop the church clock in Coney Street for centuries – is poised to make a reappearance.

The much-loved figurine has been absent from its iconic perch on the St Martin Le Grand church clock since 2021 when it was removed for urgent repairs.

  The Coney Street clock - with the Little Admiral. Photo by Kevin Atmore


In the meantime, The Press has kept readers up to date with progress of its renovationand repeated the many pleas from readers for its return.

And today we can share the news that the Little Admiral is coming home – but in a slightly different shape and form from before.

The Little Admiral has been absent from his post for a number of years, feared to have fallen victim to deathwatch beetle.

But now, after painstaking conservation work by experts from York Archaeology, the Little Admiral has returned to public display, but this time within the church of St Martin Le Grand – rather than on its clock.

  The restored Little Admiral in his new home inside St Martin Le Grand church in Coney Street. Image supplied


It marks the end of an era for the much-loved figure – arguably York's smallest and most quirky landmark.

The figurine shows a naval officer taking an observation of the sun. The gilded head on the other side of the clock represents 'Father Time'.

There has been a clock at the church since the late 17th century, but the figurine dates from 1778 when it was placed on top of a new clock at the church.

When this clock was replaced in 1856 the Little Admiral was transferred to the new time-piece.

It has been restored several times over the subsequent years, including following the bombing of Coney Street in 1942 by the Nazis, and during the 1966 renovations of the church.

  Little Admiral photographed on top of the St Martin le Grand clock in Coney Street


In recent years, the wooden figure had been treated for possible infestations, but on closer examination, it appeared that the British climate was mostly to blame for his deterioration.

Morgan Creed, senior conservator for York Archaeology, said: “The Little Admiral is made up of a number of parts – a carved wooden body and cast iron structural elements.

"We couldn’t visually assess any damage or corrosion to the iron parts, so after the figure had been frozen – to stop any insect infestation – it was x-rayed to give us a better understanding of what was going on inside the figure.

"Whilst it was encouraging to see that the ironwork was still in good condition, the wood itself had been somewhat battered by the elements, with deep cracks appearing. Protective paintwork had failed, allowing fungi to penetrate and start breaking down the wood from within.”

  May 1966 : Photo of St Martin le Grand Church with the Little Admiral


After a thorough cleaning and removing earlier repairs (including screws and adhesives), the wood was then treated and filled to help restore its stability.

The conservation work revealed earlier layers of paint, highlighting that the original colour-scheme had been changed over time.

Once the ironwork and wood were ready, they were primed and painted with traditional linseed paints, and parts re-gilded with gold leaf, restoring the original colours and majesty of the iconic figure.

“Sadly, the condition of the Little Admiral is such that it can’t go back onto the clock without risking further damage, so it is now on display within St Martin Le Grand, where admirers will be able to see it for years to come,” said Anthony Hammersley, church warden of St Helen with St Martin.

  1981 - Little Admiral on top of the church clock as new twin faces are fitted


“The model has now been digitally scanned, and the images are to be used to create a bronze cast version of the Little Admiral to sit atop the clock once again – something that will resist even the hottest sun and coldest winter.”
