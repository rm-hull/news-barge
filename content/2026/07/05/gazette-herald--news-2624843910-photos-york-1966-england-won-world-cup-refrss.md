As football fans root for the 2026 squad ahead of Monday's clash against Mexico, our archive photos offer a glimpse of life in York the last time England lifted the World Cup.

With England’s 1966 triumph fading further into history, it feels timely to stir up those memories again of a time that feels both distant and familiar.

Our latest collection featuring 10 photos of York in 1966 when England won the World Cup gives a flavour of what everyday life was like that year.

  **Recommended reads:**

In Walmgate, Bowes Morrell House stood as a reminder of the city’s long commercial heritage, while nearby two derelict shops hinted at the slow ebb and flow of trade on one of York’s oldest streets in contrast to today's stretch of cafes, pubs and restaurants.

  1966 - Bowes Morrell House in Walmgate. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Just a short walk away, change was more dramatic: the demolition of the Tower Cinema on New Street marked the end of an era for picture-goers, even as television - no doubt tuned to that famous final - became the new centrepiece of entertainment.

  1966 - Demolition of The Tower Cinema in New Street. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Elsewhere, York’s social life was going strong. The Ebor Vaults pub and Hunt’s Ebor Brewery reflected a city still rooted in traditional industry and community.

  1966 - The car park at York Races again, for another busy Ebor Festival. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Meanwhile, the bustling car park at York Races during the Ebor Festival showed crowds gathering for one of the season’s biggest events.

  1966 - The Ebor Vaults pub and Hunt's Ebor Brewery. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Meanwhile, in Goodramgate, older readers may still remember the Craftsmen shop which offered a glimpse of independent retail long before modern chains arrived.

Culture and creativity were never far away. The York Mystery Plays - which have just been in the limelight again on it four-year cycle in the city - brought history vividly to life on the streets, while this photo of York group Roll Movement suggested a city beginning to tune into the sounds of a changing decade.

  1966 - York Mystery Plays. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Not all change was about leisure. A new health clinic opening in Water Lane, Clifton, pointed to a growing focus on public services and wellbeing in post-war Britain.

  1966 - The health clinic that was opening in Water Lane, Clifton. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

Together, these scenes form a portrait of York in 1966: a city balancing heritage with change, tradition and modernity, just as the nation itself celebrated a rare moment of footballing glory.
