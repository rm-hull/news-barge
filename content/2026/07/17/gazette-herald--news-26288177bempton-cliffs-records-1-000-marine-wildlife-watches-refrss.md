A wildlife project is shining new light on the unseen marine life beyond one of the UK’s most famous cliffs in East Yorkshire which has included a "staggering" number of dolphin sightings.

RSPB Bempton Cliffs recently completed its 1,000th marine mammal watch as part of the Shorewatch project, a citizen science scheme led by Whale and Dolphin Conservation (WDC).

Since launching in March 2025, 20 trained volunteers have carried out regular 10-minute watches from the reserve’s clifftops.

  RSPB Bempton Cliffs 

*(Image: Ellen Leach)*

Susanna O’Neill, senior administrator at RSPB and a Shorewatch volunteer, said: "Reaching 1,000 watches is an incredible achievement and a testament to the dedication of our volunteers.

"The number of bottlenose dolphins we’ve seen has been staggering.

"It has been somewhat of a surprise but just goes to show what more eyes on the sea can reveal.”

In total, 505 marine mammal sightings have been recorded, comprising 2,384 individual animals, including 2,221 adults and 163 young.

  RSPB Staff and Shorewatch Volunteers at Bempton Cliffs 

*(Image: Ellen Leach)*

Among the species spotted were 1,896 bottlenose dolphins, 341 harbour porpoises, 50 minke whales, 96 grey seals, and a single humpback whale.

Ms O’Neill added: "This project has introduced thousands of visitors to the marine wildlife on East Yorkshire’s coast and to see how close the dolphins come to the cliffs has been amazing.

"By collecting long-term data on the mammals using these waters, we can better understand how environmental changes are affecting them and ensure conservation decisions are based on robust evidence."

Shorewatch originated in Scotland in 2005 at the hands of WDC and has become one of the UK’s most valuable citizen science programmes for cetaceans.

  Common Bottlenose Dolphins 

*(Image: Richard Ball)*

Bempton is the first English site to join the network.

Alice Walters, WDC Shorewatch data coordinator, said: "The excitement of local sightings should not be mistaken for a sign that all is well in our seas.

"While the waters around the UK remain vital habitats for whales, dolphins and porpoises, these species face increasing pressures from climate change, pollution, underwater noise, entanglement, disturbance, and changes in prey availability.

"The high cliffs at Bempton give excellent visibility, and the dedication of the volunteer team has been incredible.

  Yorkshire Belle and Passengers 

*(Image: Ellen Leach)*

"Reaching 1,000 watches highlights the importance of citizen science in gathering vital evidence to understand how cetaceans use our coastline and how best to protect them.

"Together we’re not only collecting valuable data but inspiring people to care about marine life on their doorstep and the need for healthy, resilient seas."

The reserve is also becoming increasingly popular for the marine mammals recorded from its coastline through the Shorewatch programme.
