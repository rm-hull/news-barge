A new suicide prevention scheme is encouraging open conversations at farm gates to support mental health.

The project, ‘Go To The Gate, Chat To A Mate’, aims to improve mental wellbeing and suicide prevention within the agricultural community.

Launched by the charity Papyrus, the campaign debuted at the Great Yorkshire Show and urges farmers to check in on one another and talk openly about mental health.

James Parkes, area manager at Papyrus, said: "Farmers already notice when something is not right.

"The ewe standing apart from the flock.

"The crop that doesn't look quite right.

"That instinct for noticing applies to people too.

"If you're worried about someone, check in.

  David Skaith, Mayor of York and North Yorkshire, meeting James Parkes and Chloe Fisher from Papyrus at the Great Yorkshire Show 

*(Image: Papyrus)*

"And if they say they're fine, ask again.

"That second question is often where the honest answer lives.

"If something they say worries you, you can ask directly: ‘Are you thinking about suicide?’

"Asking does not plant the idea.

"It opens a door.

"You do not need to have the answers.

"You just need to not walk away."

The scheme, supported by Mayor of York and North Yorkshire, David Skaith, and part-funded by the York and North Yorkshire Combined Authority, focuses on suicide prevention among men and boys in rural areas.

Papyrus will raise awareness at events including the Aldborough and Boroughbridge Show at Newby Hall and the Tockwith and District Show.

The charity is seeking volunteers to help engage with rural communities.
