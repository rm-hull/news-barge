Many of us have become pretty protein-obsessed recently, and like the best protein powders, bars have increased in variety and popularity. Offering an easy way to boost your intake of this key macronutrient, protein bars are great for busy schedules.

“Protein is important for keeping your body on top form,” Holland and Barrett nutritionist Emily Foster told *The Independent*. “It builds new cells, repairs old ones, fuels muscle growth and even provides energy. Whether you're smashing workouts, bouncing back from surgery or just aiming to stay strong and healthy, protein has your back.”

But not all protein bars are that healthy, despite their branding. Fitness coach and TV show host, Joe Wicks, attracted attention after creating a satirical “killer” protein bar for his Channel 4 documentary *Joe Wicks: Licensed to Kill* where he exposed how ultra-processed foods (UPFs) are disguised as health products by using 96 legal ingredients.

Sports nutritionist and author of The Complete Guide to Sports Nutrition, Anita Bean agrees we should pay attention to what’s on the label. “Not all protein bars are created equal,” she says. “So it’s worth looking beyond the headline protein figure and paying attention to the ingredient list.”

She generally encourages people to get most of their protein from whole foods such as dairy products, eggs, fish, lean meat, soy foods, beans and lentils, adding: “In general, I favour bars made with recognisable ingredients and minimal unnecessary additives, and view them as an occasional convenience food rather than an everyday essential.”

With all that in mind, keep reading for a list of the best protein bars recommended by a nutritionist, and taste-tested by me.

Crafted from peanuts, nut butter, oats and seeds, Pip and Nut bars offer 10g of plant-based protein per bar. If you’ve had a bad experience with chalky, bland protein bars before, these will completely change your opinion of the snack – they are that tasty. The peanut butter is soft and has a hint of salt, while the soya protein crispies add that sought-after crunch. Don’t expect a super rich dark chocolate taste, but Pip & Nut is part of Tony Chocolonely’s traceable Open Chain which has a mission to end exploitation in cocoa. If you’re not a peanut butter fan, however, this isn’t the bar for you.

I asked an expert to give us some insight into this bar’s all-important ingredients list. “Although soy protein is used to increase the protein content, the focus on nuts and other familiar ingredients gives this option a more food-based feel than many traditional protein bars,” says Bean.

If you’re looking for a gut health-oriented protein bar, this one from Bio & Me contains 10 different plant ingredients. The ingredients list includes oats, nuts, seeds, dates and seaweed, to add 19g of fibre (per 100g), as well as a boost of protein.

“While each ingredient is present in relatively small amounts, together they contribute to the bar’s fibre and plant diversity, which are important for supporting a healthy gut microbiome,” says Bean. “Providing protein from soya alongside a range of plant foods, they offer a more rounded nutritional profile than many conventional protein bars.”

The cocoa bars were my favourite for their rich chocolate flavour and lovely crunch. While they are a little dry, they are light and easy to eat.

I’m impressed that BodyMe has made a protein bar with an organic ingredients list (bar the sea salt). With flavours like chia vanilla, cacao mint, and lemon turmeric, the bars are just as adventurous (if not more) taste-wise as competitors. The lemon turmeric, while not the most appetising in appearance, has a warming turmeric and bright lemon taste and a soft texture. Packed-full of wholefood components, the bar has an impressive 16g of protein and only contains naturally-occurring sugar.

B-Corp certified From is best known for its plant-based protein powders, but the protein bars are getting some attention now too. The most stylish bar by far, I tested the Form chocolate peanut protein bar – which provides 15g of plant-based protein (pea and pumpkin seed proteins) and comes recommended by Bean. “Although they contain a small number of additional ingredients to enhance texture and flavour, they are a convenient plant based option for those looking to boost their protein intake on the go,” the expert noted.

Wrapped up in minimalist matte packaging, the chocolate peanut protein bar is something to be desired. It's very chocolatey so it feels more like a dessert than a protein snack. With the crunchy peanut pieces and crack of the smooth chocolate topping, the texture is satisfying but a little dry. If you’re looking for a low-sugar, low-carb plant based option, this one ticks both boxes with just 1g and 7g per bar respectively.

If you’re after a bar that’s high in protein without filler ingredients, Veloforte’s crunch bar is one of the best. It comes recommended by Bean for its high-protein content, who notes: “The inclusion of collagen helps raise the overall protein content, although collagen is not a complete protein and the bars are not suitable for vegetarians or vegans.”

My favourite is the cocoa and stem ginger flavour as I enjoyed the lovely heat provided by the ginger. The ingredient list is also jam-packed with healthy ingredients, including almond paste, dates and ginger, and I found the bar very satiating. My only criticism is the texture from the soy protein crisps isn’t as crunchy as described – it tastes a little stale.
