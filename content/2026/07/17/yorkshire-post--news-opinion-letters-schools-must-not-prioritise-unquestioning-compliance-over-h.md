# Schools must not prioritise unquestioning compliance over humanity - Yorkshire Post Letters

## From: Catherine Nolan, Wakefield.

In the 1990s, under the leadership of Geoffrey Smith, the school was widely respected for its ethos. His approach emphasised encouraging respectful behaviour through positive relationships, developing children's self-esteem and moral character, and nurturing qualities such as respect, honesty, responsibility, kindness and perseverance. Discipline was intended to educate and support, rather than to rely primarily on punitive measures.

Today, I believe the ethos at Outwood Grange Academy has moved in a very different direction. In my view, it is more reminiscent of a Margaret Atwood novel, characterised by authoritarianism, rigid control and enforced conformity.

![Outwood Grange Academy in Wakefield. PIC: James Hardisty](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOjBiMzIzZTE1LWQ4MzQtNDU3ZS1hZTMzLTBkMzk5ZTU5ZTRmZDoxY2ZjOWFhMC1hYTMxLTQwMzEtYjYyMC1lMzQ0ZWY1MjBmYmU=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



A culture that places compliance above compassion risks undermining the very purpose of education: helping young people to grow into confident, thoughtful and resilient adults.

As reported by BBC News on 30 June 2026, there has already been intense public concern about schools that have adopted highly punitive behaviour systems and Outwood Grange is highlighted as a school causing such unease.

Following the tragic death of a pupil at a school in Devon, serious questions were raised about the impact that excessively harsh disciplinary practices can have on vulnerable young people.

While every such tragedy has complex causes and should never be attributed to a single factor without evidence, it is essential that policymakers examine whether school cultures are always placing pupils' wellbeing alongside high standards of behaviour.

The Department for Education and Ofsted should ensure that behaviour policies promote discipline in a way that is fair, proportionate and consistent with their children's welfare. High expectations and kindness are not mutually exclusive; indeed, the best schools achieve both.

The Department for Education and Ofsted must reign in these practices before more children are harmed by systems that prioritise unquestioning compliance over humanity.
