Search the Public Notice PortalPART of the Second World War heritage of York is set to be relocated from one side of the city to another to help bring history to life for a new generation.

A Stanton air raid shelter in the grounds of The Retreat in Heslington Road will be moved to Murton Park, a visitor attraction off Murton Lane, where it will join the educational experience exploring life on the wartime home front.

The Retreat is a Grade II* listed 18th century estate in 37 acres of landscaped grounds founded by Quaker philanthropist William Tuke and currently being redeveloped into a mixture of 120 heritage and newly built homes by developer PJ Livesey.

  


  Typically assembled from around 18 bolted segments into a 50‑person shelter, Stanton shelters served as lifesaving refuges for factory workers, service personnel and civilians during bombing raids across wartime Britain. 

*(Image: Supplied)*

Lucy South, heritage events and engagement manager for PJ Livesey, said: "We're thrilled that the Stanton shelter will have a new home.

"We have a 45-year history of breathing new life into the items we find, and this will be a great example of passing on heritage to future generations and having an educational use."

The project will involve ex-British Rail engineer Nick Beilby, who was brought on board after a special York Civic Trust commemorative plaque was unveiled at another PJ Livesey development in York - the former Cocoa Works in Haxby Road.

Its last surviving member Ken Cooke - who worked on the Rowntree site for nearly 50 years - was a guest of honour at the April ceremony.

  The Stanton shelter in The Retreat in Heslington Road - one of many made from pre‑cast, steel‑reinforced concrete arch sections, manufactured by the Stanton Ironworks near Nottingham. 

*(Image: Supplied)*

Nick said: "We’ll carry out an engineering inspection, write a report covering the construction and how to dismantle it safely and how to transport it to Murton Park and place it in the Second World War experience there.

"It will probably be craned onto a low loader after being unbolted into the various sections and will need some new foundations.

"I’m delighted to play a part in this becoming part of the educational experience at Murton."

The shelter, which is behind bushes on The Retreat’s 30-acre grounds, was known to PJ Livesey thanks to historical site plans but had not been put into use for any purpose by the developer.

It is hoped the shelter will form a key part of Murton Park’s educational offering, which already includes an ARP (Air Raid Precautions) shed and a classroom setting designed to help students understand the experiences of wartime evacuees.

Pupils visiting the site take part in hands-on activities exploring evacuation procedures, air raid precautions, and daily life during the war.
