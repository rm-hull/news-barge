WHEN we take on a pet, there’s a risk involved. Anything can go wrong, but if we’re lucky we get to have them until old age. And then we might have an awful decision to make, which is in the end the kindest thing we can do for them.

We know that one day they will no longer be there. We can prepare for it. But my God, when it comes it is heartbreaking.

Last week my sister’s beloved dog, Harry, died and to say he has left a hole in her life doesn’t even come close.

She knew it was coming. He was 11 and had been slowing down a bit. Earlier this year he had a couple of seizures and we feared the worst, but with medication he rallied and still seemed to have plenty of energy. It was only a couple of weeks ago that he was trotting alongside me on a walk, proudly holding in his mouth a frisby he’d found in some undergrowth.

But you have to be realistic with an older pet and for a while now my sister, Sophie, has been trying to prepare herself for ‘when the time comes’. The time came last week, when Harry suddenly became listless and frail. He was, it turned out, very ill. Sophie and her partner went to the vets to say goodbye to him, something I can’t even bear to think about, and she has literally had a pain in her heart ever since. “It’s the same pain I felt at Dad’s funeral,” she said.

We know about grief. Less than four months after our mum’s funeral we were organising one for our dad. A decade on, I still feel the pain of losing both parents so close together.

But losing a pet is a bereavement too. Sophie got Harry, a lovely Weimaraner puppy, a few months after our dad died. Harry has been her constant companion ever since. Her whole life revolved around him - walks, mealtimes (which he knew like clockwork - maybe it was his breed, but that dog had German efficiency when it came to his daily routine) and when she got home he was there at the window. He had his own place next to her on the sofa, he’d let out a contented sigh when she wrapped his blanket around him, and he came on family holidays to the coast. He loved the beaches in Northumberland, racing into the sea.

Her sons were youngsters when Harry came into their lives. They’re grown men now. He was a big part of my life too. I took him for a walk practically every day and often looked after him when Sophie was away. I catch my breath now, walking into her house, without that familiar greeting - that beautiful grey ‘Velcro dog’, tail wagging. The space where his bowl used to be. His bed still in the corner. The house feels eerily empty. “I just want him back,” sobbed Sophie.

Anyone who has lost a pet will know the sadness. I remember a former colleague, a no-nonsense, cynical old-school hack, was visibly shaken when he came to work the day after his dog died. “I don’t know what to do without him,” he said, wiping his eyes.

Our dad was heartbroken when our old spaniel died in his sleep. When we later tried to persuade him to get another dog he refused. “I’m not going through that again,” he said.

My friend was bereft after her dog was put down. “I cried more than I did when my mum died. I probably shouldn’t say that out loud,” she said.

Part of the reason why Harry meant so much to Sophie was because he helped not only with grieving for her parents, but also the dog she had before him. Ollie was knocked down and killed on a moorland road (the driver didn’t stop) while she was out with him on a walk. It was, she says, the worst day of her life.

There is something very profound about the loyalty and unconditional love of a pet. Of course, there’s often food involved (Harry could turn on the charm when he wanted a treat) but they understand us in a way that humans sometimes don’t. I was in tears once after getting some bad news - Harry looked at me, came over, nudged me with his head and stood by me, Velcro style.

It is heartbreaking to lose a pet, but we can take comfort from knowing they had a good life. Harry had a happy life, and was very much loved. Now he’s very much missed. I’d give anything to take that beautiful boy on one more walk.
