## Higher parking charges add to York high street woes, businesses hear at meeting

Castle Car Park, in York. *(Image: Newsquest)*

Higher parking charges are hitting York city centre businesses, amid a raft of other challenges, says York BID executive chairman Andrew Lowson.

Andrew highlighted a range of initiatives central and local government is doing to help, including a £300 million high street innovation fund at Thursday’s York BID AGM.

High streets were a ‘bell weather’ for the wider economy, he told the meeting, and incomes had been stagnant for 15 years. Inflation was set to rise again, with sales down, employment down, prices up, wages up a little and profits down.

With price rises for inputs like energy, along with higher national insurance and living wages, this was why businesses say it is ‘tough going', he said.

Andrew presented figures for June 2026, which were better than the earlier published May figures that have gained local attention, which showed a drop in sales and footfall of more than eight per cent compared to a year ago.

Instead, spending data for the year to date showed a drop in sales of 3.5 per cent compared to the same period last year, which was marginally better than the national average and better than the six per cent recorded in Bath and Chester.

However, restaurants were seeing diners visiting less often and families might enjoy two attractions instead of three when they visit.

  A busy city centre, but the figures are down. 

*(Image: NQ)*

Food and drink, plus fashion retail, were affected the most, with year to date sales down five per cent compared to 2025.

Locals still liked York, but they are spending less due to ‘macro issues.’

Andrew agreed with BID associate director Angela Horner of Browns department store, that higher parking charges were putting people off from coming to the city centre.

Angela had earlier told the AGM that one small business in Goodramgate is ‘emotional’ over the issue, saying it is “facing potential closure” as a result.

Andrew says businesses tell him you can park in Malton and Helmsley for £2-3 a day and he was keen to work with City of York Council on the parking issue.

In questions after his speech, Andrew explained the council was keen to get cars out of the city centre, but a “more phased approach” was needed.

A sudden jump to £5 per hour was “putting people off” and one business he had raised with the city council said it was difficult to survive, with trade down 65 per cent as its regular customers did not come in anymore.

Andrew suggested incentives such as a £2 Tuesday, plus other special deals on the off-peak periods.

His message to the council was: “Let us help you with a solution. Meet these long-term objectives but we don’t want to lose people.

“I don’t want doom and gloom, but they are being impacted on this.”

Earlier, Andrew said towns and cities need to work in partnership, getting the basics right and the city clean and attractive.

The BID was helping with measures such as city street rangers, taxi and riverside marshals, and running Rejuvenation Days to give areas a clean and lick of paint.

There was street art and the Colour and Light Show to give a boost when footfall is lower. Ghosts in the Garden also helped with the experience economy.

  Cllr Pete Kilbane of City of York Council 

*(Image: Pic supplied)*

York BID has a Shop York digital promotion, there is also Restaurant Week twice a year for two weeks.

“All these things draw people into the city centre,” he said.

York BID was also championing regeneration, including at York Central and Coney Street Riverside.

It has also helped Panda Mami off Bridge Street deal with rough sleepers near the buffet restaurant, and improved the surrounding area with better cleaning, planters and street art.

Riverside lighting for Kings Staith will also make the area safer and support businesses there.

A play park for children will also be in Parliament Street for the summer.

Andrew added that if he was Prime Minister, he would deal with energy costs and inflation, reform business rates, make it affordable for businesses to employ young people, increase planning/ regeneration teams at local councils, tackle crime and highlight the challenges of running a business and the general need for business support.

But he concluded: “We have a lot of great things going for us in York.”

Responding to our earlier story on the May figures, City of York Council said the high street faced many challenges with people more cautious about discretionary spending and the June figures showed York was performing better than the national average.

City of York Council executive member for economy, Cllr Pete Kilbane said: “There are significant factors beyond parking charges influencing consumer spending.

Mr Kilbane added: “While we have seen a small drop in people driving and parking in York city centre, we’ve also seen an increase in people travelling to York by bus and cycling into the city centre.

“And while it’s easy to form quick judgements based on a month or two of data, a more thorough assessment would consider other factors that influence consumer spending like the expansion of shopping online, all forms of travel and as we’ve seen recently, the weather."
