# 'Time is now' for more devolution, Burnham warned

## The time could not be better for the incoming Prime Minister to move further and faster on devolution, a leading think tank has said on the day Andy Burnham took leadership of his party.

With Mr Burnham long being an outspoken supporter of increasing the range and depth of power devolved to regions, the success or otherwise of a devolution agenda is likely to be a key yardstick against which his premiership is measured, something only heightened by his opening remarks as Labour leader yesterday.

Now, the Institute for Public Policy Research North (IPPR North) has said the time for a gradual phasing of powers has passed, and a dramatic shift is now required to help boost regional opportunities and in turn help the national economy.

The report, published yesterday, argues Mr Burnham risks a ‘two-tier’ England, with wealth, health outcomes and job opportunities increasing in disparity due to a patchwork of mayoral models across the country.

Further, it says reducing these inequalities could help rebuild trust in politics and improve life chances in areas of the country where change has stalled.

If found Mr Burnham must ‘ enact his aims quickly and more widely, or risk maintaining a state where opportunities depend on geography rather than need.’

The experts argue that leaving large gaps in devolved regions risks worsening gaps between the regions themselves, with more than a quarter of the population across the country still not yet covered by Mayoral Strategic Authorities (MSAs).

With the creation of the Hull and East Yorkshire combined authority, the East of the county became the last part to come under a regional mayor.

But with swathes of England not coming under a regional mayor, the report argues any new powers granted would see these people fall further behind.

It said: “With political trust already in crisis, and public confidence in the state and political institutions at historic lows… devolution must be about rebuilding trust in democracy from the bottom up, where government prioritises ensuring every person in England feels politics is something done with them, not to them.”

It sets out three priorities it believes the new Burnham administration must focus on to ensure the success of a ‘place-based policy agenda’

These are: Driving economic growth and reducing regional inequalities; Democratic renewal and rebuilding trust in politics; and Rewiring the state and embedding devolution.

Decentralisation has been a key theme under successive Governments, with the Conservatives introducing many mayoralties under their tenure, and Sir Keir Starmer increasing both the funding and powers regional mayors received.

Under the latter, there has also been a growing willingness to allow increased devolved tax raising and spending powers to mayors.

But IPPR North argued these ‘incremental’ changes mean Whitehall still dominates decision making, slowing down key infrastructure decisions such as the West Yorkshire Mass Transit project.

The report argues: “If Andy Burnham wants devolution to be his government’s lasting legacy and truly deliver ‘the biggest rebalancing of power the country has ever seen’, [he needs to] set out a bold approach from day one in office.”

Mr Burnham’s accession was broadly welcomed by leaders in the north, many of whom have also be campaigning for years for increased devolution.

Henri Murison, Chief Executive of the Northern Powerhouse Partnership, said:

"The Manchester model has demonstrated what can be achieved through long-term leadership, strong civic institutions and collaboration with universities and business.

"Over the Pennines, the productivity transformation of the site of the Battle of Orgreave thanks to the Advanced Manufacturing Park, termed Rotherhamism, shows the path for re-industrialisation again here drawing on private investor Harworth, the university and local councils.

"The North has a central role to play in the UK's future prosperity. By giving places the control of future tax revenues and wider investment they need to succeed, there is a real opportunity to accelerate growth, raise productivity and improve living standards - not just in the North, but across the entire country."
