A North Yorkshire Councillor who heavily relies on buses for commuting is 'delighted' that a Conservative Party policy has been adopted by the new Labour Prime Minister.

Cllr George Jabbour, who had successfully campaigned for the inclusion of the £2 bus fare cap in the Conservative Party manifesto for the last General Election, has pledged to continue fighting for better public transport connections.

Cllr Jabbour, who represents Helmsley and the surrounding villages, referred to a recent 'productive' meeting that he held with the Buses Team of the York and North Yorkshire Combined Authority, which has acquired the responsibility of the strategic oversight of public transport in the county from North Yorkshire Council.

Cllr Jabbour said: “Having campaigned relentlessly to champion the £2 bus fare cap, I used every opportunity ahead of the last General Election to talk about its benefits, including in the media and during the meetings that I had with the then Prime Minister, Rishi Sunak.

“Therefore, I am delighted that the £2 bus fare cap, which was initially introduced by a Conservative Government, has now been reinstated by the new Labour Prime Minister, Andy Burnham.

“At the same time, I continue doing everything possible to improve local bus links across Ryedale and North Yorkshire. For instance, I recently met with the Buses Team of the York and North Yorkshire Combined Authority at County Hall in Northallerton. We had a productive conversation, and I look forward to positively collaborating with them to deliver for our communities and for local residents."

Andy Burnham, who became PM on Monday, July 20, visited Bath on his third day in office to highlight the government’s response to the cost of living, where he announced that he would be capping bus fares across England to £2.

During the visit, the Prime Minister said the cap would “really help people with the cost-of-living pressures they’re facing”.

“It’s there for the whole of 2027, and I think it will put money in people’s pockets,” he added.

The bus cap across England is set at £3 until the end of March 2027 although some areas – including London and Greater Manchester, where Mr Burnham kept the £2 limit as mayor – have lower top rates for single tickets.

A previous £2 cap had been in place between January 2023 and December 2024 under the Conservatives but the incoming Labour administration argued it was not affordable.

Reinstating the £2 maximum fare will partly be funded by switching grants for international climate projects to repayable loans instead.

The measure is backed by £454 million of funding, including money to allow devolved governments to take similar action.

Some £400 million of that funding will come from shifting investment in international climate finance to loans, with £54 million from savings in the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero budget.
