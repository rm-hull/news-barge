# Dear Prime Minister, here are some modest, cost effective policy ideas that can get Britain back on the right track - Daxa Patel

## A Letter to the Rt Hon Andy Burnham MP, Prime Minister. Congratulations, Sir, on taking on this important and demanding role. You are acutely aware that you are the seventh Prime Minister in ten years.

The stakes are high, both at home and abroad, and people are hurting. Your in tray will be overflowing with competing priorities. Yet beneath the noise lies a quieter truth. Many people in Britain feel forgotten. They are not asking for grand reforms. They are asking for signs that their lives matter.

As a Yorkshire soul raised by a loving father, writing to another Northerner who knows the weight of that bond, I know you care. In your first address to the nation, you said political leaders must raise their game. You were right. And we all must. You also spoke of the need for a renewed sense of unity. I would add that we need a stronger sense of belonging and pride in what it means to be part of this country we call our home. If we are to rise, we must rise together.

Out of a hundred issues that deserve attention, I offer five ideas that are modest in cost, quick to implement and capable of making a disproportionate difference. Each could restore dignity and confidence without requiring years of structural reform.

The first is national courtesy and civic pride. It is not a matter of legislation but of tone. Britain has become a place where people rush, tempers fray and public servants absorb the strain of collective frustration. A light touch national effort that highlights respect, volunteering, neighbourliness, mentoring and gratitude would cost very little. It would remind us that citizenship is strengthened when contribution sits alongside entitlement. When leadership signals courtesy and civic pride, people respond. When we treat one another with dignity, we rise together.

A practical example is a national ‘Say Thank You Week’, where schools, businesses and communities recognise carers, teachers, refuse collectors, NHS staff, bus drivers, shopkeepers and volunteers. It costs little but strengthens connection.

The second is loneliness among older people. Thousands are not asking for money. They are asking for connection. Loneliness is a public health issue. The cost of isolation is borne by the NHS, social care services and families struggling without adequate community support. Simple interventions work. Coffee mornings. Walking groups. Telephone befriending. Intergenerational projects where schoolchildren visit care homes to read, sing or spend time with residents.

You will know from your own father’s experience with dementia that singing can be profoundly beneficial. Supporting small community organisations would reduce GP appointments, improve wellbeing and restore belonging. When we bring generations together, we rise together.

For instance, every primary school could partner with its nearest care home. Once a month, pupils spend an hour reading, singing, gardening or simply talking with residents. Children gain confidence. Older people regain companionship.

The third is Britain’s litter and neglected public spaces. Dirty streets, overflowing bins, fly tipping and broken street furniture send a daily message that nobody cares. Clean streets are psychological. They tell people their environment matters. The Government could increase penalties for fly tipping, require councils to publish cleanliness scores, establish national volunteer clean up weekends and encourage businesses to adopt local streets. Pride follows. When our shared spaces are cared for, we rise together.

A simple model is a ‘Pride in Our Place Saturday’ four times a year, where residents, businesses and community groups spend two hours clearing litter or refreshing tired public spaces.

The fourth is reducing pressure on general practice by expanding social prescribing. Many people seek GP appointments for issues that could be eased through community activity or simple human connection. Social prescribing works, but it remains inconsistent.

Instead of a GP repeatedly seeing someone who is lonely or mildly anxious, the patient could be referred within a week to a walking group, gardening project. One weekly walk can be more powerful than another ten minute consultation.

The fifth is youth micro volunteering and practical work placements. Many employers say young people lack experience. Many young people say nobody gives them a first opportunity. A national programme of micro volunteering and short work placements would build confidence, skills and hope. Local businesses could offer two-week placements supported by simplified paperwork. Councils could encourage participation through modest business rate incentives.

For example, every local business could offer one young person a two-week placement each summer. A florist, café, solicitor’s office, veterinary practice or garage could participate. One first opportunity can become a first job.

The common thread is simple. Each proposal tells people that the Government has noticed how everyday life feels. People often say they feel invisible rather than poor. They are frustrated by systems that do not work, places that appear neglected and increasing isolation. A Government cannot solve every problem overnight. It can, however, restore confidence by improving the things people encounter every day.

Daxa Manhar Patel is a leadership coach, author and solicitor.
