York Minster has said the solar panels on the ancient building’s roof were “designed with fire safety as the primary requirement”, after it was revealed a huge blaze at Malton hospital was most likely caused by solar panels.

A Minster spokesperson said the Chapter of York worked with its insurer to “the system's design adequately mitigated fire risk to the historic fabric of the building”.

  Damage caused by the fire at Malton Hospital 

*(Image: Mark Wilson)*

It comes after North Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service said the fire that tore through Malton Hospital’s Springwood ward was most likely started at one of the solar panels on the roof of the building.

The Tees, Esk and Wear Valleys NHS Foundation Trust (TEWV), which runs the 15-bed hospital unit, said it has not been possible to determine any further details about the cause due to the damage sustained to the solar panels during the fire.

York Minster has previously said fire was a top priority when installing its solar panels given the cathedral’s history of rooftop fires – most recently in 1984 when lightning struck the cathedral’s South Transept.

  The devastating fire which ripped through York Minster's South Transept on July 9, 1984 

*(Image: Supplied)*

Its spokesperson said the 184-panel array, which generated more than 42,000 kWh of clean electricity in its first six months, was designed with key fire protection measures in place.

The measures include ‘SolarEdge power optimizers’ on each panel. These allow panel-level control and reduce electrical fault and fire risk compared with conventional string systems.

  York Minster 

*(Image: Dylan Connell)*

There is an ‘automatic voltage reduction’ – meaning that when the solar system or the grid is shut down, the voltage at each panel is automatically reduced to a safe level, helping protect maintenance personnel and firefighters from electric shock.

Connector-level temperatures are also monitored on the panels. A system is in place that continuously monitors connectors and can detect abnormal heating, helping identify loose connections or faults before they develop into a fire.

The panels are also connection with the Minster's fire alarm system, which allows the solar panels to be shut down automatically or manually during an emergency.

Speaking to mark sixth months since the panels were installed last year, Alex McCallion, director of works and precinct for the Chapter of York, said: "This is an incredibly important building – it’s the symbol of this great city, the largest Gothic cathedral north of the Alps, so it has huge international significance; however, it is also a building with an unfortunate history of fire.

"One of the reassuring aspects of SolarEdge technology is the significant reduction in risk.

"We worked very closely with our insurance company, EIG, to reassure them, and us, that we were able to address the possible risk to the fabric of this beautiful building by installing solar."
