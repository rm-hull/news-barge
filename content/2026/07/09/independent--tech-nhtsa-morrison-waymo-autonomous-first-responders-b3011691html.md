America's top road safety regulator has issued a stark warning to self-driving car companies, demanding they swiftly address a "clear pattern" of autonomous vehicles interfering with law enforcement and other first responders.

Jonathan Morrison, who leads the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), detailed numerous instances in a letter to the industry in which autonomous vehicles (AVs) have driven into active emergency scenes.

He cited other incidents where vehicles "blocked the paths of ambulances and firefighters, or failed to recognize and respond to basic safety conditions like flashing lights, flares, smoke, fire, and traffic cones."

![Jonathan Morrison, who heads the U.S. vehicle regulatory agency, said in a letter to the industry that NHTSA has documented multiple instances of AVs driving into active emergency scenes](https://static.the-independent.com/2026/07/09/01/Waymo_Arrest_35762.jpg)

"Let me be clear: the inability to detect and appropriately respond to such situations represents a functional insufficiency," Morrison stated in the letter.

NHTSA announced plans to convene meetings with vehicle developers by month's end to seek solutions and urged AV developers and operators to prioritize resolving the issue. The agency emphasized, "An AV that cannot safely interact with first responders is a danger to the general public."

While NHTSA refrained from identifying specific incidents or companies mentioned in the letter, local reports from Texas highlighted a late-May incident in Dallas in which a Waymo self-driving vehicle reportedly obstructed a fire truck's path to an apartment blaze.

Waymo did not immediately comment on the Dallas incident, though other videos have surfaced showing its vehicles blocking ambulances and navigating through active police scenes.

Both NHTSA and the National Transportation Safety Board are currently probing separate incidents involving Waymo's autonomous vehicles.

These include instances of Waymo vehicles illegally passing stopped school buses with activated lights in Texas, and a January 23 incident in Santa Monica, California, where a self-driving Waymo struck a nine-year-old girl in a school zone as she ran from behind a double-parked SUV.
