# FOI request uncovers multi-million pound costs of North Yorkshire's 'appalling roads'

## New data revealed by North Yorkshire Council after a Freedom of Information request highlights the scale of pothole problems facing drivers in Harrogate and the wider county

An investigation by Accident Claims Advice – [https://www.accidentclaimsadvice.org.uk/](https://www.accidentclaimsadvice.org.uk/) – shows that a total of 18,181 severe highway defects were officially reported by the public to North Yorkshire Council between the 2023/24 and 2025/26 financial years.

North Yorkshire officially logged 6,500 individual road defects during the 2023 financial cycle alone.

While the following year offered a very brief reprieve with 5,023 recorded craters, the underlying structural integrity of the routes clearly continued failing at pace.

By the 2025 to 2026 period, the volume of reported cavities aggressively surged once again, reaching a devastating peak of 6,658 recorded incidents.

North Yorkshire Council responded to the Freedom of Information Request CN41643 dated June 24, 2026 saying it had processed the request as an 'Environmental Information Regulations' request as opposed to a Freedom of Information Request, because environmental information was exempt from disclosure by virtue of Section 39 of the Freedom of Information Act 2000.

The figures released illustrated the multi-million pound costs to the public finances of road defects and potholes in North Yorkshire.

During the initial 12 months alone, a staggering £46.5 million was spent trying to salvage North Yorkshire's rapidly degrading and heavily utilised carriageway network.

This financial burden steadily climbed, hitting nearly £46.9 million the following year before rising to £53.3 million in the latest figures.

A major portion of this spending went on temporary fixes just to make the deteriorated routes immediately passable.

The cost of those short-term solutions added to £20.5 million overall, the data acquired by Accident Claims Advice – [https://www.accidentclaimsadvice.org.uk/](https://www.accidentclaimsadvice.org.uk/) – showed.

Temporary repair costs started at £7.3 million during 2023, and although they dipped slightly, they remained consistently above £6.6 million every subsequent year.

The state of the roads is such that it is currently costing taxpayers almost £150,000 in compensation payouts.

Since 2023,North Yorkshire Council has paid out £146,188 in successful financial settlements to victims directly affected by the appalling driving conditions.

But North Yorkshire Council argues this is not a problem of its making.

Last month it said it was being forced to introduce a new policy on pothole repairs across the county’s vast network of roads to counter an “unexpected multi-million pound budget shortfall”.

North Yorkshire Council said it set to receive £63.8 million for highways maintenance in 2026/27, an increase from £57.8 million in the current financial year.

However, due to the changes, we will see a reduction for highways maintenance of at least £20 million as funding is due to fall over the following three financial years compared to what would have been received directly from the Department for Transport.

Despite the financial pressures, the council is investing about £28 million on carriageway works on the 5,800 miles of roads in North Yorkshire, which is England’s largest county.
