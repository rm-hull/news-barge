  *David Skaith, regional mayor for York and North Yorkshire, makes the case for more devolution and relocating the House of Lords to York*

AFTER the local elections, I said that things had to change with the leadership of the country, but that I didn’t think it was appropriate to change leaders and fall into the default position of the last government. I watched Keir Starmer’s speech on the Monday after those bruising results and was left disappointed that he had missed a crucial opportunity to send a clear message to the nation that he understood and would do things differently.

That was the final straw for many Labour MPs, and two months later he announced that he would go.

Two years after the Labour government was elected, with many achievements under our belt, it is time for a change of leadership.

One that speaks more clearly on the issues that matter most to people across this country and sets out a vision for a rewired economy and a new political culture.

A new political culture that puts place before party, problem-solving before point-scoring, and long-term plans before short-term fixes.

## Devolution

One of the greatest strengths of devolution is that it allows local leaders to focus on the priorities of the places they serve, rather than the day-to-day politics of Westminster.

Yet, on this occasion, the two are closely linked because the likeliest candidate to become our next Prime Minister is my former mayoral colleague, Andy Burnham.

I joined other Labour mayors last week as he set out his vision for the country at the People’s History Museum in Manchester.

## The big transfer

Andy’s plan centres on the biggest transfer of power out of Whitehall in modern times.

Britain is one of the most centralised countries in the world, yet Greater Manchester has shown what can be achieved when decisions are made closer to the communities they affect.

Despite resistance from central government, Andy has built the fastest-growing regional economy in the UK.

That success has been built on partnership.

A mayor’s most important role is often bringing together the public, private and community sectors alongside our colleges and universities.

That is the model we have followed since I was elected.

And that is why a more powerful Mayor’s Office, with the funding and flexibility to match, can only benefit people across our county.

## A stronger focus

A stronger focus on reindustrialisation, housing and tackling the cost of living crisis will help us move faster on our ambitions for York and North Yorkshire.

Last week, alongside my Combined Authority Cabinet, we approved three new Mayoral Development Zones at Scarborough, the Selby Growth Zone and York Central.

Together, they represent our biggest opportunities to deliver affordable homes, create jobs and regenerate our town centres.

## More powers needed

Greater powers and resources, particularly a major council house-building programme and housing-led regeneration, will be crucial to delivering these plans.

They would allow us to go further and faster in delivering the changes local people have waited decades to see.

I have made no secret of the fact that fixing our county’s broken bus network is made harder by the way government funding is allocated.

Our geography makes the challenge greater than in many urban areas.

More established mayoral authorities can transfer capital funding into day-to-day revenue spending.

We cannot do that yet, but reallocating just 4% of our transport funding would stabilise the network, improve fares and help us build the publicly controlled bus system needed to restore reliable services.

## Moving the House of Lords

The commitment to transfer power out of Whitehall is also why I support moving the House of Lords to York.

I can think of no more appropriate home for our nation’s second chamber.

The idea has been discussed since 2020, and York has both the historic credentials and the connectivity to make it work.

Alongside a new 'No. 10 in the North', it would send a powerful signal that we are serious about rebalancing power and decision-making away from London and the South East.

The more decisions about York and North Yorkshire are made locally, by local people and for local people, the better.

Because when I make decisions about funding, I have to look directly into the eyes of the people who will benefit.

That is something a civil servant or politician in Westminster can never do.
