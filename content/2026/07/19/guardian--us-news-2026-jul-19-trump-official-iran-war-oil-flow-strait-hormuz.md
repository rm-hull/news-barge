A Trump administration official on Sunday grappled with mounting criticism over the length of the US war with Iran after months of promised diplomatic breakthroughs, short-lived ceasefires, and the deaths of three more American troops bringing the total number of American military members killed in the conflict to 17.

Appearing on ABC’s This Week, the US energy secretary, Chris Wright, defended the Trump administration’s strategy after being confronted with his earlier claims that the war which began in February would have ended by now.

Wright argued that the mission has since shifted toward ensuring oil shipments can move through the strait of Hormuz.

“We did a pause to try to get an agreement with the Iranian people to allow oil to flow without them attacking it,” Wright said. “They’ve proven unwilling to do that, and we’ve pivoted. Now, what we’re doing is assuring that that oil and gas and other products can flow through the strait of Hormuz with or without Iranian cooperation, and we’ve continued to degrade their offensive military capabilities.”

On the same program, a prominent Democrat continued his party’s sharp criticism of the Trump administration’s handling of the nearly five-month conflict.

US senator Mark Warner of Virginia described the conflict as a “war of choice” that was launched without an imminent threat and argued the administration has failed to accomplish its previously stated objectives.

“Regime change, how are we doing there? We got a worse crowd than before,” Warner said of Iran. He said the enriched uranium that the US wanted to get from Iran to prevent it from building a nuclear weapon is “still there”, adding: “Matter of fact, it would take a minimum of 15,000 troops on the ground for a week to try to take these canisters, which are quite volatile, out of their bunker.”

Warner also remarked, “Getting rid of Iran’s missile and drone capability – obviously, we’ve not been successful there” because the conflict continued on Sunday. He said: “The continuation of this war, at this pace, will be a continuing disaster.”

In addition to two deaths in Jordan on Friday, the US Central Command announced that another service member was killed on Saturday “during a controlled detonation of unexploded ordnance” in northern Iraq. The unexploded ordnance was from a “downed Iranian one-way attack drone”, Centcom said in a statement on social media on Sunday.

The latest casualties in Iran for the US military came as a renewal of the campaign against Iran entered its second week, leading to intensified fighting around the strait of Hormuz. The US launched retaliatory strikes on Sunday against Iran’s Revolutionary Guards (IRGC) after the killings.

After the deaths of the two US troops on Friday, Trump’s defense secretary Pete Hegseth vowed the military campaign in Iran would continue, writing on social media that the service members’ “sacrifice only stiffens our resolve”.

Donald Trump himself also escalated pressure on Iran Sunday, with the president urging Congress to include Iran in pending Russia sanctions legislation. The move would further weaken the memorandum of understanding between the two countries in June aimed at resolving the Iranian conflict that included an agreement to end sanctions for Iran.

Alluding to the unexpected death on 11 July of US senator Lindsey Graham, his ally, Trump wrote on his Truth Social platform, “Republicans should add Iran to the Russian Sanctions Bill. That’s what Lindsey wanted to do, and it was going to happen. IMPORTANT!!!”
