Donald Trump has claimed that Andy Burnham “will be opening up, all the way, the invaluable North Sea Oil” and move the “horrible looking windmills”.

The celebratory post on TruthSocial claimed that the people of Scotland were “dancing in the streets” over the incumbent prime minister’s alleged position change on the North Sea, yet to be confirmed in policy.

Labour’s deputy leader Lucy Powell dismissed speculation around this as she told BBC’s Laura Kuenssberg: “I think what Andy’s talking about is taking a more pragmatic approach and working with the industry to make sure it can contribute to that transition and to the mix that is needed over the long term.”

The North Sea has remained a point of division between the US president and Sir Keir Starmer, as energy security minister Ed Miliband remained reticent around increased drilling in the oil fields.

Previously Trump said of Mr Burnham: “I hear he’s extremely liberal, extremely, so that means he probably won’t open up the North Sea.”

Mr Burnham will enter 10 Downing Street on Monday as speculation continues to grow around who will be in his Cabinet.

## Burnham to set out plan to 'give Britain more breathing room', in first Downing Street address

*The Independent’s political correspondent Millie Cooke reports:*

Andy Burnham will argue that Britain faces a “moment not for triumphalism but for honesty and reflection” and set out a plan to “give Britain more breathing room” in his first address as prime minister.

Speaking outside No 10 on Monday, the new prime minister will say that he is "acutely conscious" of being the sixth person to walk up Downing Street in just ten years, arguing that the “unprecedented turnover in national leadership underlines the need for a more stable and responsible politics”.

Mr Burnham will say now is a moment of "reflection and resolution", arguing the UK must be "honest about the challenges it faces while demonstrating that it can once again be recognised around the world for effective government".

## Former head of British Army condemns idea of Wes Streeting as defence secretary

Lord Dannatt, former head of the British Army, has expressed disappointment at the idea of Wes Streeting coming into the Ministry of Defence, claiming the former health secretary knows “the square root of nothing” about defence.

He told Lewis Goodall on LBC: “Defence is a complex portfolio, it takes time to understand it. He's got no background in it. Dan Jarvis has got a very good background in it. John Healey had experience in that role.

”I would be very disappointed to see someone like Wes Streeting coming in who knows pretty much the square root of nothing about defence and would have to start from square one, and that will just take time, energy from people to brief him before he's in a position to start making decisions.”

He added that Dan Jarvis, set to be left without a Cabinet role as Andy Burnham steps in, “could do a really good job” if given the funds to step GDP investment up to 3.5 per cent by 2035.

## Thames Water creditors open to talks with Andy Burnham on more ‘public control’

Investors in Thames Water have said they are willing to discuss greater “public control” of the utility amid reports Andy Burnham could temporarily nationalise the struggling company.

Turnaround specialist Mike McTighe, who is working with the largest group of Thames Water’s creditors, said UK and international investors were willing to work with incoming ministers to “rebuild confidence” in the debt-laden firm.

Mr McTighe said: “We are keen to meet new ministers as soon as possible to discuss how we can work together in the best interests of customers, including by enhancing public control of the company’s operations.

“We remain ready and willing to recapitalise Thames Water, return it to investment grade, and begin the long process of turning it around.

“We urgently need Government engagement to begin that process.”

## Lucy Powell sets outline of what 'authentically Labour' means ahead of Burnham's arrival at 10 Downing Street on Monday

The deputy Labour leader was sure to outline what Andy Burnham meant by Labour becoming “authentically Labour”.

Lucy Powell said on Sunday with Laura Kuenssberg: “It's not trying to out-Reform Reform, it's not trying to out-Green the Greens.

“It's about giving people, ordinary people, security in work, security in their home, a home they can afford, and live in a community that they can feel proud of and.

“Knowing that their children are gonna have a opportunity for the future.

”These are the sort of basic essentials that ordinary people aspire to, want to have, and they want to know they've got a government on their side, and it does mean taking on some of the vested and private interests that have for too long governed this country.

“We have seen an over centralised political base, which has meant that too many communities have been left behind, and we have seen an economy that's been overly-privatised and and that the utilities and the essentials of life don't work in the interests of ordinary people, so giving people that agency as well.”
