Search the Public Notice PortalFew would say Coney Street remains York’s main shopping destination. The buzzing street was once the bustling heart of the city centre but in recent years empty units have not been uncommon in the area. So is it time for a complete rethink of the area?

Max Reeves certainly thinks so. He’s the development director of York-based developers the Helmsley Group which is now starting its major redevelopment of Coney Street by opening the area up to the nearby riverside.

“It’s going to have a dramatic impact on this part of York and the retail scene,” he says. “We think the investment that we’re putting in can really create the kind of retail destination that people want to see in order to bring them into the city centre.”

The development is in its first stages – renovating shop fronts at the St Helen’s Square end of the street – four years after the plans were first submitted.


Under the plans, greater access will be created to the River Ouse by knocking through existing buildings, such as Boots, and creating a riverfront area called Waterloo Place.

The backs of existing retail units Next and WH Smith will be removed for a new riverside walkway to be created, according to the plans.

A six-storey building will then built, fronting on to the River Ouse, with businesses on the ground floor and student accommodation on the upper floors.

“It’s a really exciting time for what we’ve got going on in Coney Street,” Max says. “We’re on site at the moment with zone one [the work refurbishing shopfronts], with the first retailers starting to fit out this month and opening in September.

“We will be working our way down the rest of the street for the summer.”

Max is speaking after the latest figures from York BID were released which paint a grim picture for York: an 8.2 per cent slump in sales in May, compared to the same month last year, and an 8.8 per cent drop in footfall. The national decrease over the same period was 4.5 per cent.

Sector performance also shifted significantly, York BID found, with fashion sales down 30.4 per cent, general retail down 10.2 per cent and food and drink down 7.5 per cent.

City of York Council disputed the figures as "inaccurate", but its executive member for economy, Cllr Peter Kilbane, admitted that “retail is going through a challenging period”.

But, like Max, he stressed that York’s high footfall from tourists and locals means the city remains a good place to do business.

Max also points to what he says is continued interest in York from businesses, shown by Federal café – which has built up a huge following after opening in Manchester then Leeds – and beauty store Space NK both deciding to open in Coney Street.

Despite retail taking a hit in May, according to the York BID figures, Max says he does not see the future of York city centre as mainly hospitality venues.


The plans for Coney Street, he says, are “very much retail led”. “Those figures don’t necessarily reflect the demand that we’re seeing from retailers wanting to come into York.

“The figures for the year aren’t great, but overall York is such an attractive destination that there’s still massive interest in the city from all sorts of different retailers.”

“Footfall is always going to ebb and flow in any city centre,” Max adds, “but York fundamentally remains an extremely attractive place for retailers.

“We’re seeing that with the retailer demand and retailers planning to come into York’s centre – particularly with not only what we’ve got going on in Coney Street, but other retail blocks within York.”

A fresh approach is, however, needed in Coney Street, Max says, because the “retail market has completely changed”. “People want to come in and have experiences. York is a beautiful place and has a real attractiveness to it, but we can’t rest on our laurels in terms of options that other people have to visit.

“We need the investment in the city centre – not only from private landlords like ourselves … but also the public sector.”

Max says work on the Coney Street riverside development is “still going to be a long time from here”, but he insists the wait will be worth it.

“Some of the longer-term aspirations on the full riverfront will take longer still to come forward, but fundamentally we’re going to see some big changes over the next 12/18 months,” he adds.

## Development will have huge benefits, says trader

Thomasina Cass, store manager of the Poetry Pharmacy which opened in Coney Street in March, feels the redevelopment will bring more people to the area.

She says the opening of the bookshop has gone “really well” but admits there was a slight drop in trade during May, which she put down to poor weather and people not spending as much money.

The Poetry Pharmacy, Thomasina says, enjoys steady footfall from York residents and tourists in Coney Street, but “the middle of the street tends to get forgotten”.

She feels creating Waterloo Place behind where Boots currently is could draw more footfall to Coney Street’s middle section.

“I think that will really, really help,” Thomasina says. “When you go to other cities with rivers, they have that riverside location accessible.

“It’s always felt weird to me that in York we’ve got a big swathe of it that’s not accessible because of these big buildings.”

The Helmsley Group has warned that disruption is inevitable during the development but said it will work to keep this to a minimum.

Thomasina says communication between the developers and traders “is key” as more work prepares to get underway.

“But I think some disruption is necessary for the outcome at the end,” Thomasina says. “And I can see the benefit being really, really big.”
