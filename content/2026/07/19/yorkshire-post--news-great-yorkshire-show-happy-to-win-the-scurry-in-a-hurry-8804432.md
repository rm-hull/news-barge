# Great Yorkshire Show: Happy to win the scurry in a hurry

## Having been pipped to the post at last year’s national championships, surpassing the speed and accuracy of all rivals at the Great Yorkshire Show was particularly sweet for Bitter and Twisted scurry team driver John Cornell, claiming first and second places.

Reacting to the win, John said: “Amazing. I came second in the championship last year with Bribery and Corruption, who had a bad day, but Bitter and Twisted were on fire today.”

John, who had made a six-hour trip from Hampshire to compete, described one half of the winning pair of large ponies, known at home as Ronnie and Reggie, as “neurotic”. He said: “Reggie will be in a stable with hay and straw in it and will worry about being in that stable, I’ve had him nine years and he’s always been like that. But when he goes he’s so powerful, so quick, and Ronnie goes with him.”

He put down their accuracy through the cones around the scurry course to getting rid of reins and putting loops on. He said otherwise, when the ponies start off they raised their heads and pull the reins out of the driver’s hands and by the time they have been gathered up three or four cones can have been hit.

![Scurry winners Bitter and Twisted fly around the course with driver John Cornell.](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOmY0ZTNhYTI0LTQ4NzMtNGViYi1hMTY1LTg5MzRkNTFjYzkyMzpkZDEzZTI0YS01NmQ0LTRjYjItYjdjMS01NGI0ZjMwNGYzNGQ=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



"They know where they’re going”, he said. “You could almost leave them to run on their own.”

He said scurry was “a mad adrenaline buzz”. “I also drive a team outdoors competing, but it’s not the same buzz”, John said. “With scurry it’s as fast as they can go and it’s you and them. There’s no brakes, gizmos or gadgetry on these. It’s a little carriage that doesn’t weigh a lot and is as basic as basic can be.” Asked if he ever felt scared as the ponies turned sharply at pace, he said drivers needed to have faith in their back-stepper as they kept the carriage on the floor. He said: “If you didn’t have them on the floor and go as fast as we go you’d tip over. I don’t even think about it, you’ve not got time, you’re too busy thinking about where you’re going and making micro adjustments. I think it’s the best fun you can have with a pair of ponies by a long way.

The first two days of the show saw Gerard Welford, of Saltburn, land the Yorkshire Post Challenge Cup as Cleveland Bay champion with his bay filly foal Mulgrave Holly and two-year-old Fordelhill Elena, owned by Scott Greenhill, whose family’s involvement in breeding and showing stretches back to the 1880s.

Doncaster-based equestrian breeder Jane Turnbull, who specialises in native colored horses, claimed the hotly-contested coloured ridden championship and Jane Strickland bettered her second placing with grey Irish Draught Appleton Aristocratic Lady at Hambleton District Show earlier this year taking the breed championship at Harrogate.

![John Whitaker competing in the Rudding Park Great Yorkshire Championship on Unick Du Francport.](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOjZmZTg0MTM0LWNkNzktNDE5OC1iNzg2LTMzYmFmNDYwZGYwNzpjYzYwOWM2Ni05MDY2LTRjZTEtODc5YS0yNmM2NjkxMjQzOTk=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



In the Novice Hunter Championship, Leon King on board Sue Tennant’s heavyweight champions Silver Shadow was pipped to the post in a tight contest with Rob Walker riding Jill Day’s lightweight champion Shanaghan Ricochet. Affectionately known as Chips, the crowd was told the five-year-old had “a very bright future ahead”.

Rob, who originally hails from Etton, near Beverley, said: “We always aim for the Great Yorkshire. There’s probably no better main ring in the country to show a hunter off. You can cover the ground and show the gallop off, glide along at trot, it’s lovely. We won here with him in the youngstock championship as a three-year-old and we broke him at four. He’s just been to a couple of easy shows around the country to bring him on.”

Meanwhile another East Yorkshireman, Leon King, of Fraisthorpe, near Bridlington, said he was “over the moon” with the reserve title, which represented by far his best result of the season with seven-year-old Silver Shadow. He said: “Everyone wants to win the Yorkshire, and being reserve champion is amazing because the lightweight section was very strong.”

Upper Cumberworth equestrian legend John Whitaker said he would compete on all four days of the show, including in the Rudding Park Great Yorkshire Championship and Cock of the North on Unick Du Francport. He said: “I always look forward to the Yorkshire. It really helped my career in the early 70s and gave me a chance to ride against the top guys, Harvey Smith and David Broome.

![John Whitaker competing in the Rudding Park Great Yorkshire Championship on Unick Du Francport.](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOmFlZGUxMWFlLTQwOTMtNGYxMy04NjQxLTFmYzczMWQzNjdmMTozY2M4N2MwMC0yYjU0LTRmYjEtYjY1YS05MmFhOTI1NTZmMGY=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



"You could see what you have to do and one day in the early 70s I actually beat them, it gave me confidence and set me on the right path. I owe a lot to the Great Yorkshire Show. It’s a great atmosphere. When I go around the world or country and they say which is the best show and I say the Great Yorkshire, they think I’m biased. When they actually see it they say ‘no, you’re right!”.
