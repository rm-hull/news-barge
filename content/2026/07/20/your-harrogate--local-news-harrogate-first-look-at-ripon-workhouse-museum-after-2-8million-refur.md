                                    Ripon's Workhouse Museum has reopened after a £2.8 million redevelopment that promises to immerse visitors in the realities of Victorian life.

The museum opens to the public tomorrow (21st July) following a major restoration project that has transformed one of Britain's most complete surviving Victorian workhouses into an interactive visitor experience.

Visitors can explore the newly restored Residents' Block, where paupers once ate, slept and worked, while discovering the stories of people who entered the workhouse as a last resort.

The reopening marks the completion of the Inspiration for a Fairer Future project, funded by The National Lottery Heritage Fund alongside support from The Wolfson Foundation, Garfield Weston Foundation and North Yorkshire County Council.

As part of the project, the Residents' Block has undergone extensive conservation after years of being used as a care home and council offices. Restoration work has included repairing the roof, replacing rotten windows and reinstating original historical features.

Revamped areas include the Old Men's Day Room, Kitchen and Dining Hall, while the original workhouse cellar has been opened to the public for the first time.

Dr Laura Allan, curator at Ripon Museums Trust, said:

"The cellar is particularly exciting as it’s the only part of that building that had been untouched since it was turned into council offices.

"It has the massive original meat block table where the workhouse pigs would be butchered, trough and sink.

"It's like glimpsing behind the curtain, seeing how it truly was."


The new visitor experience includes hands-on activities designed to show what life inside the workhouse was really like.

Guests can choose to spend time inside a recreated vagrants' cell, sweep the yard, pick oakum from old ropes and discover whether they could have coped with the jobs carried out by Victorian paupers.

Families can also dress in replica workhouse uniforms, while younger visitors can search for hidden mice in the kitchen, use traditional weighing scales and take part in interactive activities throughout the museum.

Alexa Vernon, Director of Ripon Museums Trust, said:

"We've transformed the workhouse into a powerful, immersive experience where visitors can step into the shoes of real people who lived through extraordinary hardship.

"Their stories challenge us to think about fairness, dignity and opportunity, both in Victorian Britain and today.

"This project is about much more than restoring a historic building. It is about preserving important heritage, creating opportunities for communities and young people to engage with history, and inspiring visitors to reflect on how we can build a fairer future.

"We want people to leave feeling not only informed, but also more compassionate, thoughtful and connected to the stories of those who came before us."


The redevelopment also introduces a new Discover Space, where visitors can explore exhibitions, creative activities and discussions linking Victorian history with the present day.

Helen Featherstone, Director, England, North at The National Lottery Heritage Fund, said:

"The redevelopment has secured the future of one of Britain's most complete surviving Victorian workhouses, preserving both the building and the stories it holds for future generations.

"We’re incredibly proud to have been able to support the project thanks to money raised by National Lottery players and it is now creating opportunities for volunteering, learning and skills development, ensuring the museum remains relevant and accessible to all."


The Workhouse Museum is one of three museums run by Ripon Museums Trust, alongside the Prison & Police Museum and Courthouse Museum, with all three telling the story of poverty, crime and justice in Victorian Yorkshire.
