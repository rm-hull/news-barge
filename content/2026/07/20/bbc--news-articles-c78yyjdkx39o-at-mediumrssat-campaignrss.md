Visitors must now pay a £2 fee online before coming to the blue lagoon

**Owners of a "blue lagoon" that went viral on TikTok during the Covid pandemic have warned people not to enter the site, particularlyduring the current hot weather.**

Brombil Reservoir in Margam in Neath Port Talbot attracted thousands of visitors, which caused traffic problems and littering.

To reach the 30m-deep (100ft) reservoir, visitors must walk up a steep 900m (2,953ft) public footpath.

Leisure Estate Investments Ltd, which bought the secluded site at auction for £20,000 in 2024, are now charging £2 a visit as "a temporary deterrent" and have installed barriers on its dam face.

It is concerned about people swimming, paddleboarding, littering and damaging structures around the site.

Although the reservoir can be accessed via public footpaths, the barriers were installed to stop people from walking on the dam face - but these have since been removed by trespassers.

The owners said the £2 levy was introduced in an effort to manage unauthorised visits and help fund clean-up operations, as part of its efforts to discourage public access because of "inherent safety risks".

A spokesperson for the company said: "We have strictly banned [people] from going there and walking on the face of the dam.

"Our long-term vision has always been to build a sustainable public-private framework so the community can safely enjoy this asset.

"However, managing a critical piece of infrastructure presents immense structural, environmental, and financial challenges that cannot be indefinitely sustained by a private entity alone."

![Brombil Reservoir is a blue lakes surrounded by steep green hills](https://ichef.bbci.co.uk/ace/standard/976/cpsprodpb/a75e/live/9f4d58b0-71cf-11ef-b282-4535eb84fe4b.jpg)

The reservoir has stunning blue waters and is surrounded by a hillside forest

The reservoir is a flooded 19th Century colliery that was repurposed to supply water to Port Talbot Docks, and is now famous for its striking turquoise colour.

It plays a vital role in flood protection, helping shield downstream residential areas, and has a "unique aquatic ecosystem" that has faced "severe environmental degradation caused by fly-tipping and littering", Brombil Reservoir Ltd said.

After the landmark went viral on a TikTok video with more than one million views, nearby residents claimed they had been "suffering" because of the traffic and litter problems.

To reach the 30m-deep (100ft) reservoir, visitors must walk up a steep 900m (2,953ft) public footpath, which is also the only way down.

In an effort to combat vandalism, including the destruction of iron security gates on deep-water chambers and persistent littering, the company introduced the £2 charge.

It said it was to "manage unauthorised access and fund clean-up operations. It is not a commercial ticket".

The company added its goal was to safely open the site for public use, saying it recognised the benefits of "blue spaces" for health and well-being.

![A wooded area with a small clearing that is filled with litter - there are bin bags, carrier bags and drinks cans strewn across the floor](https://ichef.bbci.co.uk/ace/standard/1248/cpsprodpb/1e85/live/df888bf0-843f-11f1-b16e-0f7ab380f520.jpg)

Due it its secluded location up a steep hill, some visitors choose to dump their rubbish at the reservoir, rather than carry it back down with them

Since taking ownership in 2024, Brombil Reservoir Ltd said it had invested in structural surveys, engineering assessments, landscaping and waste clearance.

It added that compliance requirements from Natural Resources Wales (NRW) for high-risk dams had increased significantly, requiring tens of thousands of pounds in ongoing safety investment.

Matthew O'Brien of NRW's reservoir safety group said the Reservoirs Act 1975, meant owners were responsible "for ensuring their reservoirs are properly maintained and managed".

He added that these rules were designed "to ensure that reservoirs are managed safely and help reduce the risk of flooding to people and property downstream".

Owners of regulated reservoirs pay an annual fee of £276 towards NRW's regulatory costs, external.

The company said it had repeatedly sought support from Neath Port Talbot council and local politicians to help fund and preserve the reservoir, but had received no response.

It added: "We genuinely want to work constructively with Neath Port Talbot council, NRW, and our political representatives to deliver a safe, biodiverse, and secure site."

Brombil Reservoir Ltd warned that without public sector support or a partnership agreement, it could be forced to "decommission the reservoir".

It added: "This would unfortunately eliminate the area's primary flood defence and destroy a vital ecological habitat.

"We sincerely hope local authorities will join us at the table to find a sustainable, shared way forward."

The council said it had"offered advice where appropriate" to the reservoir owners.

"As the site is privately owned, decisions regarding its operation and investment are a matter for the owner," it added.

"Any future arrangements should ensure that visitor activity is managed responsibly and does not place undue pressure on local communities.

"We remain open to constructive dialogue with the owner."
