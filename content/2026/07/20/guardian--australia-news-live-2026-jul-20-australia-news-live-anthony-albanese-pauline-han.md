Jewish community security group requirements have doubled since 2023, royal commission hears

Jewish community security group protection requirements have doubled in the three years since the start of the conflict in Gaza, the royal commission on antisemitism and social cohesion has heard.

A volunteer for the Victorian Community Security Organisation (CSG), which like other CSGs in Australia, monitors threats to the Jewish community and provides security for schools, synagogues and events, has appeared under the pseudonym ACO at today’s hearing.

ACO, who holds a leadership role in the Victorian CSG, said national combined data showed the total number of volunteer hours had gone from 86,000 in 2024 calendar year to almost 114,000 hours in 2025.

Asked by counsel assisting, Tamara Philips, how much they estimated the total commitment per person across volunteer and paid members had increased since October 7, they said:

It’s fair to say that the requirements have nearly doubled in that period of time. Certainly, when it comes to the protection of events in our community; those numbers have doubled. When I look at the number of staff we have, that’s tripled.

When I look at the number of synagogues we’re protecting, that’s well over double the numbers that we used to protect. And whilst our volunteer numbers they do continue to grow, it’s nowhere near what we need in order to meet the operational requirements for our community.

South Australian electorate renamed after Lowitja O’Donoghue

Lowitja O’Donoghue, a trailblazing Aboriginal nurse turned Indigenous rights advocate, has been honoured with the renaming of a federal electorate, AAP reports.

The vast division of Grey, which covers the majority of South Australia, is being renamed as O’Donoghue, the Australian Electoral Commission announced on Monday.

O’Donoghue, a member of the Stolen Generations, was the first Aboriginal woman to be trained as a nurse and served as the founding chairperson of both the National Aboriginal Conference and the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Commission. Her advocacy was pivotal to Australia’s native title legislation.

O’Donoghue was a Yankunytjatjara woman, with the electorate of Grey taking in most of the Anangu Pitjantjatjara Yankunytjatjara lands.

Lowitja O’Donoghue in October 2011. Photograph: Alan Porritt/AAP

The AEC determined the name change despite 235 of the 310 community submissions arguing to retain Grey, named for Sir George Grey. The AEC found the arguments for change were “well detailed, informative, convincing and ultimately persuasive. This contrasted with most of the submissions objecting to the renaming of the electorate.”

The commission noted Sir George spent “a comparatively small part of his life” in Australia, with longer service to the British empire in South Africa and New Zealand, and the AEC decided “the time had come to recognise the outstanding contribution to Australia and its people made by a distinguished Australian”.

The decision comes after an AEC proposal last week to rename the southern Tasmanian seat of Franklin to Tongerlongeter, after an Indigenous warrior who fought colonisation, then took on a leadership role for Aboriginal Tasmanians exiled to Flinders Island.

The AEC has opened consultations on the Franklin switch, which comes after a radical re-drawing of the electorate’s boundaries.

Dreyfus says he feels ‘freer as an MP’ to support Jewish community after leaving cabinet

Mark Dreyfus has described feeling “freer as an MP” to represent the Jewish community after he was replaced as attorney general by Michelle Rowland.

He said one of the reasons he had given evidence to the commission was the “unique position” his role as a Jewish member of the federal cabinet had put him in after the start of the conflict in Gaza. He said:

I wasn’t in a position to respond fully, or perhaps at all, to the attacks and demands that have been made of me by some Jewish Australians, simply because I was a cabinet minister and the constraints of the office meant that I was not in a position to respond or comment.

Dreyfus said since leaving the cabinet in a reshuffle after the 2025 federal election, “I’ve been much freer as an MP”.

I was able to respond after the Bondi attack … able to respond publicly, able to appear in the media, able to give speeches, able to make representations to cabinet ministers about what the government might be able to do, and it’s certainly, to some extent, been liberating compared to what I experienced, the constraints I experienced as a cabinet minister.

Dreyfus also described an incident in May 2024 in which his office was covered with red paint and “Who does AG Mark Dreyfus serve?” written on the front pavement as a “deeply wounding attack”.

He said:

No one ever should make that suggestion about a member of the Australian parliament, let alone someone who had been sworn to administer a portfolio as the attorney general of the commonwealth, but it invokes all of those old antisemitic tropes about dual loyalties and treachery to Australia, all of which are false. It’s a deeply wounding attack … and probably intended to be.

Dreyfus tells royal commission of ‘breach of sense of safety’ for Jewish Australians

Penry Buckley

The federal MP for Isaacs and former attorney general, Mark Dreyfus, has told the royal commission on antisemitism and social cohesion about the “breach of that sense of safety” experienced by Jewish Australians since 7 October.

The fifth block of hearings, starting in Melbourne this week, is examining security arrangements for the Jewish community, including the role of Jewish community security groups (CSGs) and their coordination with law enforcement agencies.

At the start of this morning’s hearing, counsel assisting Richard Lancaster SC said evidence to the commission would show “a substantial and ongoing threat to the safety and security of the Jewish community in Australia”.

Dreyfus, today’s first witness, has told the commission about his father, who arrived in Australia from Nazi Germany 1939 as an 11-year-old, later followed by Dreyfus’ grandparents. He said:

It’s a great thing that that refuge was provided in Australia, and I think that’s a knowledge shared by Jewish Australians that Australia did that, and it’s it informs in part the shock that I have tried to relate in my witness statement to the commission about the breach of that sense of safety that was created by the providing of refuge.

Dreyfus told the commission that antisemitism in Australia had not “come out of nowhere” but said he had been shocked by an upsurge in antisemitism since Hamas’ 7 October attack, including an incident in which a man approached him outside Victoria’s state library in April 2024 and called him a “genocidal Zionist”.

Dreyfus said:

No one had ever hurled abuse at me because I am Jewish. This was a new development in my engagement in Australian politics and I know I’m not alone.

Parents were told asbestos in play sand was low risk unless ‘pulverised’. But regulators hadn’t tested it themselves

Schools, retailers and government agencies assured parents that asbestos-contaminated play sand posed a low risk to their children’s health based on a supplier’s own assessment which was not independently verified, the Guardian can reveal.

Consumers were sold tens of thousands of boxes of potentially contaminated sand over a period of at least five years and the products were used in hundreds of schools and childcare centres in Australia and New Zealand.

Until this month, the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) advised toxic asbestos fibres were “unlikely” to be released into the air unless the sand was “processed by mechanical means such as crushing or pulverising”.

This advice originated from a risk assessment that Educational Colours itself commissioned, which did not involve any additional testing or even a visual inspection of the contaminated products, and was never made public.

The supplier’s safety claim became the official position of the Australian and New Zealand governments even though they had no additional evidence to support it.

Nearly 2.2 million Australians 15 years and older are living below the wellbeing poverty line, with the share of people reporting very low life satisfaction doubling over the past decade.

In 2025, 9.7%, or about one in 10 Australians, rated their general satisfaction with life at four or below out of 10, according to Guardian Australia’s analysis of the Australian Bureau of Statistics’s (ABS) general social survey.

That compares with one in 20, or 4.8%, reporting very low overall life satisfaction in 2014.

The initial surge of Australians reporting dissatisfaction with their lives came during 2020’s Covid lockdowns and has persisted over the intervening years amid soaring cost of living.

The ABS data echoes other surveys, such as Westpac’s consumer confidence index, which has languished at around 50-year lows since failing to recover from the pandemic blow.
