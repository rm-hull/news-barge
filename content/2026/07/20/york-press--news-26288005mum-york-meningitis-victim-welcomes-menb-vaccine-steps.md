A York mother whose 19-year-old daughter died from meningitis has said she is “so pleased” that the government has been recommended to offer a mandatory vaccine to teenagers against the deadly disease.

Alison Goude’s daughter Livia Wilson, of Woodthorpe, died in her sleep in 2022 after unknowingly contracting meningococcal meningitis – a life-threatening bacterial infection that can cause meningitis, sepsis, or both.

Alison, who has long called for meningitis vaccines for all teenagers on the NHS, said she welcomed government steps to offer teenagers a free vaccine against meningitis B (menB).

  Alison with her daughter Livia when she was young 

*(Image: Family)*

Government advisers have recommended that youngsters should get a jab around the age of 15, with catch-up programmes for those who would otherwise miss out.

It comes after a series of outbreaks of meningitis, with the most serious being in Kent earlier this year which left two teenagers dead.

The Joint Committee on Vaccination and Immunisation (JCVI) is recommending a booster jab around age 15 for those who had a menB jab as a baby (those born on or after May 1 2015). The first cohort this recommendation applies to will turn 15 in 2030. Children who missed the jab as a baby will be offered two doses.

JCVI also said it “strongly supports” giving two doses of the menB vaccine to children born on or before April 30, 2015. They would get this when they are around age 15. This group were not offered the vaccine on the NHS as babies and so have missed out on protection as infants. Offering jabs when they hit their teens protects them at the point they are next most at risk.

And because it could take a while for any programme to be implemented, the JCVI would like to see a catch-up programme to ensure anyone who misses a vaccine does not lose out.

JCVI’s recommendation is separate to the one-off programme announced last month for time-limited MenB vaccines in response to recent meningitis outbreaks, which about a million teenagers and young people are expected to be eligible for.

Year 13 pupils and those under 25 starting university this autumn are being offered the menB vaccine on the NHS this summer.

Appointments will begin on July 20 at pharmacies, with a second dose given in August to ensure full protection.

Alison, who has spearheaded campaigns to raise awareness of meningitis and to fund research into the disease, said providing MenB vaccines for all teenagers on the NHS would save lives.

“I’m so pleased to hear about this recommendation, which is what so many of us have been campaigning for, for some time now – especially all of us parents who have lost our child to this killer disease,” she said.

“To know now that if this is approved – which we really, really hope is – that this child age group, that is also at a higher risk, can also now be protected with the vaccination.

“That this could reduce and hopefully stop the loss of our children to this killer disease is what it’s all been about.

“I just hope the government really, really considers all those whose lives have been affected from this horrible disease and makes the correct decision.”

  Livia Wilson 

*(Image: Family)*

Data suggests the menB vaccine is highly effective, providing strong protection for at least five years after vaccination.

Professor Wei Shen Lim, chairman of the JCVI, said: “We encourage everyone who is eligible to book an appointment online to receive the vaccine at a local pharmacy, in time for the second dose to be received before heading off to university or college.

“For protection, two doses of the menB vaccine are necessary, at least 28 days apart.

“Additionally, JCVI has now also provided Government with a recommendation and additional considerations for a future routine menB adolescent vaccination programme for those aged around 15 years.

“(The Department of Health) will now consider this with a decision to be made in due course.”
