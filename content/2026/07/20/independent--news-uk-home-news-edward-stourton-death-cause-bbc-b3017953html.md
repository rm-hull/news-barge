Broadcaster and journalist Edward Stourton has died aged 68, the BBC has announced.

Mr Stourton’s career spanned more than 40 years, including 10 years fronting the broadcaster’s prestigious Radio 4 *Today* programme.

He was diagnosed with prostate cancer in 2014, but continued to work until recently.

The BBC said that he died on Saturday, surrounded by his family.

Jonathan Munro, the interim chief executive of BBC News and Current Affairs – who also worked as Stourton’s producer when he was ITN’s diplomatic editor – said it was “far too soon to lose Ed”.

![‘He leaves a huge gap’: Stourton worked for many flagship BBC programmes, including ‘Today’ and the ‘World at One’](https://static.independent.co.uk/s3fs-public/thumbnails/image/2008/12/13/23/101923.jpg)

Speaking about his time on ITN, Munro described his late colleague as “unflappable then and ever after”.

“Many years later, I was listening to his superb analysis of the new Pope for the BBC – another of his many areas of expertise. Not many broadcasters can present *Today*, BBC One’s* One O’Clock News, The World At One *and* Sunday* – but he did. He leaves a huge gap.”

Mohit Bakaya, controller of BBC Radio 4, said: “Ed was an absolutely outstanding broadcaster. He brought intelligence, authority and an extraordinary hinterland to everything he did.

![The veteran newsreader, top left, died on Saturday ‘surrounded by his family’](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/20/08/35f0cc47fc63ae7dec6b1a6e0b99e3d4Y29udGVudHNlYXJjaGFwaSwxNzg0NjE5MTQ5-2.33477993.jpg)

“When Ed was behind the mic, whether on *Sunday* or the various news programmes he regularly presented for Radio 4, you felt you understood the world that little bit better. Our thoughts are with his family and friends at this sad time.”

Mr Stourton was born in Nigeria and was one of the founding members of the Channel 4 news team when it was formed in 1982.

Starting out as a scriptwriter, he went on to cover foreign news at the broadcaster, also enjoying a stint as Washington correspondent, where he reported on the final years of Ronald Reagan’s presidency.

He then went on to join the BBC in 1988 before returning to ITN two years later as diplomatic editor, reporting from around the world including Baghdad during the Gulf War, Bosnia during the siege of Sarajevo and from Moscow in the final days of the Soviet Union.

Stourton went back the BBC in 1993 and fronted the *One O’Clock News* for six years and presented his own Radio 4 phone-in programme,*Call Ed Stourton*, before joining the* Today* programme in 1999.

![Stourton was a journalist and broadcaster for more than 40 years](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/20/8/55/edward-stourton.jpeg)

He fronted the flagship current affairs programme for a decade, later becoming a regular contributor.

Mr Stourton also worked as BBC Two’s world affairs strand correspondent and presented editions of *Panorama*, as well as a series of Radio 4 programmes including* The World At One, The World This Weekend*and* Analysis*.

Along with his four-decade-long career as a broadcaster, he authored a number of books, including 2013’s *Cruel Crossing* and his 2023 memoir *Confessions* – in which he revealed he had been diagnosed with prostate cancer nearly a decade earlier.

His most recent book, *Made In America* (2025), explores US history and how it has led to the era of Donald Trump. It was written to coincide with the 250th anniversary of the signing of the Declaration of Independence.

Mr Stourton is survived by his wife Fiona and his four children.
