A beloved York park which was created to honour war heroes has turned 105.

Rowntree Park is marking the milestone, since it first opened to the public as a green space born out of remembrance, philanthropy, and a vision for healthier lives in York.

As part of the anniversary, we're sharing 10 photos and the history of York's Rowntree Park at 105.

The park was officially opened on July 16, 1921, gifted to the people of York by the Rowntree family as a “perpetual memorial” to workers from the Cocoa Works who were killed or affected by the First World War.

Joseph Rowntree said he hoped the space would “afford many rest and recreation from the turmoil and stress of life” and bring “health and happiness” to future generations.

  Frost and snow in Rowntree Park, in March 1970. 

*(Image: Press)*

From that day on, the park was managed by York City Council.

Plans for the park began shortly after the war, with 17 acres of land secured in 1919 for £1,500.

The project, funded and overseen by the Rowntree Village Trust, also provided much-needed employment at a time of hardship.

  Aerial photo of Rowntree Park taken by Kieran Delaney, Press Camera Club 

*(Image: Kieran Delaney, Press Camera Club)*

Designed by architect Fredrick Rowntree, the park included pioneering flood defences, with 19,000 yards of drainage pipes, sluice gates, and a flood wall installed to manage the Ouse.

From the outset, it was carefully laid out to encourage recreation for all ages and a healthy lifestyle.

A shallow serpentine lake, deliberately no more than two feet deep, was created with children's safety in mind, while facilities ranged from playgrounds and a wading pool to tennis courts and bowling greens.

  Boating at Rowntree Park (Image Friends of Rowntree Park) 

*(Image: Friends of Rowntree Park)*

Early plans also set aside space for hockey at the north and a cricket ground at the south, with different areas tailored to children (a wading pool), young people—with a playground—and adults, all linked by paths and benches.

There was also a café which first opened in 1921 in the Rowntree Park Lodge, as it is today.

  Rowntree Park, captured by Sue Gabbatiss, Press Camera Club. 

*(Image: Sue Gabbatiss)*

The lodge, or park keeper's building as it was also known, was also designed by Fred Rowntree.

Its aim was to be a focus for the park on its central access to take advantage of the high ground above the lake.

The café was on the middle floor with the sun terrace while the lower floor was used as a boat house for boating on the nearby lake. The top floor was the park keeper's living quarters.

In 2012, the café underwent a makeover and became a 'Reading Café' operating under the city's library service.

It was a welcome surprise to witness library services expanding in York, particularly when libraries were closing across the UK.

Billed as the first of its kind in the UK, the plan was that the café would pay for itself through coffee and cake.

The park continued to evolve, with additions including a swimming pool and aviaries in the 1920s.

It later expanded to incorporate Clementhorpe allotments in the late 1920s and land at Butcher Terrace in 2000.

  Summer 1974 in the Rowntree Park swimming baths. 

*(Image: Press)*

Fast forward to today and the park's original design is still recognisable—although some features have disappeared over time, others have been restored and new additions such as the skate park, basketball court, and a community orchard reflect modern-day use of parks.

Rowntree Park remains one of the city’s most cherished green spaces, still fulfilling its founding aim more than a century on.

  Rowntree Park, photographed by Isaac Savage in 2025. Press Camera Club 

*(Image: Isaac Savage)*

Now managed by City of York Council with an active group of supporters, The Friends of Rowntree Park, who are heavily involved in its daily work.

  Ice skating in Rowntree Park in the big freeze of 1933. 

*(Image: The Press)*

The Friends of Rowntree Park marked the occasion by sharing old photos of the park on its social media pages, with the words: "One thing has never changed though: this park belongs to the people of York.

"Today, alongside the City of York Council, hundreds of volunteers help care for Rowntree Park through the Friends of Rowntree Park.

"Together we plant and garden, care for wildlife habitats, litter pick, restore landscapes, run events and activities, fundraise for improvements and work to ensure the park continues to thrive for future generations.

"For 105 years, Rowntree Park has been a place to walk, play, meet friends, connect with nature, remember, celebrate and simply pause from the stresses of everyday life.

"We hope Joseph and Seebohm Rowntree would be proud to see that their vision continues to live on through the community that loves and cares for this special place.

"Here's to the next 105 years."
