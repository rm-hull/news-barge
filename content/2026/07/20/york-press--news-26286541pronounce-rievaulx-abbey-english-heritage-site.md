With the school summer holidays here, many families from near and far will be setting their compass for the abbey-in-a-valley north of York that launched a thousand mispronunciations.

In this corner of England, “Rievaulx” in North Yorkshire is one of those names locals roll off the tongue without thinking - and quietly enjoy hearing visitors have a go.

For newcomers, it’s right up there with Harome and Great Barugh on the list of “places you’re sure you’re saying wrong”.

  **But just how do you pronounce it?**

If you grew up around Helmsley and the surrounding villages (and have a good grasp of French), you’ll know Rievaulx is pronounced “Ree-voh” – two neat syllables, no hard consonants, and absolutely no “x” at the end.

Behind the tricky name is a place many local families treat as a fixture: school trips, Sunday walks, and that first slightly soggy picnic among the arches.

  Rievaulx Abbey original stereogram. Picture : English Heritage 

*(Image: English Heritage)*

Rievaulx Abbey, tucked away in its green valley, began in the 12th century as a serious Cistercian enterprise: monks, prayer, sheep, and rather a lot of wool. Over the centuries, stone by stone, it grew into one of the great medieval abbeys of England, before Henry VIII’s dissolution turned cloisters into rubble and monks into memories.

  The Abbey in 1918, covered with vegetation and half buried beneath spoil. 

*(Image: Submitted)*

Fast‑forward a couple of hundred years and the ruins had become exactly the sort of “romantic” sight that poets and painters loved - ivy‑draped arches, sheep grazing where choirs once sang, and those classic, moody Yorkshire skies hovering over it all.

In July 1802, the poet William Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy visited Rievaulx Abbey during their travels around northern England - with Dorothy describing the visit in her journal.

“I went down to look at the ruins: thrushes singing, cattle feeding among the ruins of the abbey; green hilloes about the ruins; these hilloes scattered over with grovelets of wild roses and covered with wild flowers,” she wrote. “I could have stayed in this solemn quiet spot till evening without a thought of moving but W. was waiting for me!’”

  Labourers at work among the ruins of Rievaulx Abbey in the early 20th century. 

*(Image: Submitted)*

For generations of people living nearby, the romantic ruins are simply part of the landscape: somewhere you go when the weather’s decent, the dog needs a walk, or visiting relatives need impressing.

Up above the abbey sits Rievaulx Terrace, which adds its own layer of local pride. Completed in 1757 for Thomas Duncombe II of Duncombe Park, it’s a perfectly staged Georgian promenade, with the land shaped into gentle curves, trees placed for effect and two neoclassical temples built to frame the view of the ruins below.

  Rievaulx Abbey. Picture: English Heritage 

*(Image: English Heritage)*

Inspired by Duncombe’s Grand Tour travels, it’s “Picturesque” with a capital P: a scene arranged as carefully as a painting, only with handy seating and a good line of sight to the abbey.

Today, English Heritage’s joint ticket means you can wander the abbey floor in the morning, imagining the chanting, the industry, the slow slide into romantic ruin, and then head up to the terrace in the afternoon to see it all from above, framed by those temples and their elegantly laid‑out interiors.

A joint ticket costs from £33.46 for a family of two adults and up to three children. Members of English Heritage can visit for free.

By the time you’re back in Helmsley waiting for the Coastliner back to York, you’ll have taken in a slice of serious history and enjoyed one of North Yorkshire’s most famous views.
