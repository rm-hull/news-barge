One of Yorkshire’s most remote pubs has become a destination in its own right, drawing walkers, drivers, and history lovers to a ridge more than 1,300ft above sea level.

The Lion Inn, on Blakey Ridge between Rosedale and Farndale, stands at the highest point of the North York Moors National Park.

To some, it is a legendary Coast to Coast stop and to others, it is a place for open fires, huge portions, and views across one of the most remote sections of North Yorkshire.

The site has been linked with travellers for centuries, with ancient waymarks, burial mounds, and old routeways all found nearby.

Black Howe stands close to the riggway leading towards Old Ralph’s Cross, while Cockpit Howe and Loose Howe point to even earlier prehistoric activity in the surrounding landscape.

The inn’s own history is wrapped in local tradition.

Some accounts date it to the 16th century, while older stories claim a sign once stood outside bearing the words “traveller’s rest”.

Today, The Lion Inn describes itself as a "16th-century free house at 1,325ft, with views over Rosedale and Farndale."

The pub, at Blakey Ridge near Kirkbymoorside, has a 4.3 rating on Tripadvisor from more than 1,000 reviews.

It is listed as a Travellers’ Choice 2025 venue and is ranked second of eight restaurants in Kirkbymoorside.

Reviewers often praise its remote location, cosy atmosphere, open fires, and hearty pub food, although some say service and food quality can vary at busy times.

One recent visitor called it “an amazing pub on Blakey Ridge”, adding that it had “a wonderful situation”, “a great menu” and “delightful staff”.

Another described Sunday lunch as “amazing”, writing that the pub felt “old-worldy” and “lost in the middle of the moors”.

For long-distance walkers, the inn has particular significance.

One Coast to Coast walker said: “This place is legendary for anyone doing the Coast to Coast trail.

"You simply have to stay here.”

They praised the camping, hot showers, food, and large field, adding that there is “not much other accommodation around here”.

Another walker, arriving wet and tired after poor weather, said the open fires and staff were a “welcome sight”.

They described the food as “very hearty with massive portions” and said they would “wholeheartedly recommend” it for a meal or overnight stop.

Food is a recurring theme in reviews, with visitors mentioning steak pie, fish and chips, curry, parmo, Sunday roasts, Yorkshire puddings, ales, and large portions.

One reviewer said the portion sizes were unlikely to disappoint, while another praised a pint of Timothy Taylor’s Landlord and fish and chips overlooking the moors.

The pub’s own story includes changes brought by mining, farming, roads, and tourism.

Once tied to remote routes and local trade, it later benefited from better highways and the arrival of the car and now welcomes thousands of visitors every single year.
