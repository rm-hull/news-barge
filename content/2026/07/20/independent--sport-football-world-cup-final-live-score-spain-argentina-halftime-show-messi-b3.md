Spain won the World Cup for the second time in their history as they claimed a 1-0 extra-time victory over 10-man Argentina before ugly scenes on the pitch after full time marred the celebrations.

Ferran Torres’ smashed finish in the 106th minute was enough for Spain to add to their 2010 triumph but Argentina – who offered little throughout the match – reacted badly after the final whistle, with players brawling, Leandro Paredes sent off after grabbing Eric Garcia by the throat and reports of punches being thrown.

Donald Trump then unsurprisingly inserted himself into things post-match as he lurked towards the edge of Spain’s trophy lift to ensure he was in the photo, despite Gianni Infantino trying to usher him away, after presenting the Spanish players with medals and the trophy. However, the US president was audibly booed when he first walked on to the pitch with Infantino.

After a dire spectacle for 90 minutes, with neither side mustering any real chances, Enzo Fernandez stupidly got himself sent off just before the end of normal time when he crunched Pau Cubarsi on halfway and was deservedly shown a second yellow card, having earlier been booked for dissent.

Spain then finally made the breakthrough when Torres calmly hammered home at the start of the second half of extra time and it meant Lionel Messi was denied on surely his final World Cup appearance, with the aftermath further souring the occasion for the 39-year-old football icon.

## How long was half-time at the World Cup final and did Fifa break the rules?

Half-time at the World Cup final lasted 27 minutes and 19 seconds after the ‘Super Bowl style’ show delayed the resumption of the match between Argentina and Spain.

Organisers Fifa had assured both teams before the final that the half-time show, curated by Coldplay’s Chris Martin and featuring Madonna, Shakira, BTS, Justin Bieber and Burna Boy, would only last 11 minutes, with the time needed to assemble and dismantle the stage at the MetLife Stadium in New Jersey taking the total length of the expected break to around 20 minutes.

However, the delay to the World Cup final was actually much longer, which meant Fifa went even further in breaking their own rules. In the laws of the game, governed by Ifab (the International Football Association Board), it states half-time breaks should “not exceed 15 minutes”. There were over 27 minutes between referee Slavko Vincic’s whistle at the end of the first half and the start of the second.

## Five reasons why the World Cup final was so dull

**Finals usually are like this!**

Sure, this was not the 2022 World Cup final between Argentina and France. But the reason that match should be remembered as the greatest football match of all time is because it took place within what is usually the suffocating constraints of the biggest sporting event on the planet. When the stakes and pressure are so high and the margins between immortality and defeat are so fine, it does not tend to result in a thriller. England’s 6-4 win over France in the third-place match on Saturday night would never have played out like that if it was the final. An exciting World Cup as a whole does not guarantee a final of the same ilk. So, actually, this was a typical World Cup final, one to follow Germany 1-0 Argentina (after extra time, 2014), Spain 1-0 Netherlands (after extra time, 2010), Brazil 0-0 Italy (3-2 on pens, 1994).

## Five reasons why the World Cup final was so dull

**The weather**

The 3pm ET kick-off time in New Jersey (8pm BST) was undoubtedly scheduled with the European TV market in mind as well as the local and South American audience. But starting in the middle of the afternoon and the hottest part of the day (27C / 80.6F) would not have helped the players, either. The New York New Jersey Stadium is a giant outdoor bowl exposed to the elements, unlike the semi-final venues in Atlanta’s Mercedes Benz Stadium and the Dallas Stadium in Texas which feature giant retractable roofs and benefit from air conditioning.

**A ‘ bobbly, dry’ pitch**

Concerns were first raised about the state of the ‘New York New Jersey Stadium’ pitch weeks ago. Brazil star Vinicius Jr said following the 1-1 draw against Morocco that “because of the weather and the heat, the grass dries out quickly and the game ends up being very slow”. Sound familiar? On ITV, Ian Wright led criticism of the World Cup final pitch following the goalless first half, calling the playing surface “ridiculous, poor, terrible” as well as “bobbly and dry” - hardly ideal conditions for slick attacking play and silky dribbling.
