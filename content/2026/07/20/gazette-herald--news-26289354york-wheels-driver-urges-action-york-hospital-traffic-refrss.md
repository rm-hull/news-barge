A CHARITY volunteer has called for action to tackle congestion around York Hospital, describing a nearby roundabout as the city’s biggest traffic bottleneck.

As a driver for York Wheels, Dougie Weake regularly takes elderly and mobility-impaired residents to hospital appointments.

Many of Dougie’s passengers are over 90-year-old and are forced to sit in long queues to the hospital, often missing appointments as a result.

But City of York Council said it was working to address the traffic problems, and some new measures were already funded and being brought forward.

Dougie said the congestion was “a huge problem for us at York Wheels".

  


“We often have to plan an hour ahead to account for traffic near the hospital," he said.

“Many of our passengers have hip and knee problems and sitting in a car for that length of time, especially in a heatwave, is just terrible for them.”

  Dougie Weake said the congestion around the hospital is "terrible" for the elderly and vulnerable users of York Wheels - a charity service for those who are unable to use public transport 

*(Image: Dougie Weake)*

Dougie, who volunteers with the charity three times a week, said his “heart sinks” when a user has an appointment during hospital visiting hours, when traffic is at its busiest.

He said: “We try to pick out the best routes to ensure our users get there on time – it’s a real problem.

“Once they are done with their appointments, some patients are left sitting at the hospital for me while I navigate the traffic – turning what was a simple appointment into a four-hour commute, and that’s if they get there on time at all.”

Dougie said the hotspot for traffic lies in the roundabout turning off from Haxby Road.

  Congestion is a "huge problem" for York Wheels charity driver Dougie Weake 

*(Image: Google Maps)*

He explained: “It’s one in and one out. I’m often stuck in gridlock traffic as far back as Gillygate.

“And it’s not just us, the taxis and buses transporting patients are also in these queues, along with residents and local workers.

“The roundabout is the biggest bottleneck in York.”

City of York Council's executive member for transport, Cllr Kate Ravilious, said she is keen to address the issue and has discussed a range of measures to ease congestion.

These include providing clearer public transport information in hospital appointment letters, promoting NHS staff car-sharing schemes, securing more funding for existing bus services and transferring some patient appointments to hospitals closer to home.

  Cllr Kate Ravilious said the council was working with the hospital and bus operators to explore options to reduce gridlock traffic near the hospital 

*(Image: City of York Council)*

She said: “The council is working with York Hospital’s Trust and bus operators to explore solutions – some of which are funded and will come forward.

“We already have mayoral funding and are looking to set up a car sharing app or system that helps link workers who are travelling to the same area at the same time.

“The council also works with the hospital to give supported bus fares to NHS staff, something that many workers already use.

“We recognise, however, that not everyone can take the bus, and for those that can, congestion can be just as bad.”

## 'Previous hospital Park and Ride services were stuck in the same traffic jams'

Cllr Ravilious said the council were committed to improve the bus network to the hospital.

She said: “I think there would be a lot more people who would use the bus if there was a good transport option to get to the hospital.

“Park and Ride buses to the hospital could be part of the solution but will only work if they are accompanied by other measures that help to cut congestion.

“Previous hospital Park and Ride services were stuck in the same traffic jams as everyone else and failed to attract sufficient passengers to make them viable in the longer term.”

The council are also looking at other helpful measures, Cllr Ravilious said, such as working with the hospital to move appointments such as blood and hearing tests to community venues.

She added: “We’re moving towards a community-based service for some outpatients requiring certain tests – things that can be done without specialist equipment.

“Distributing these services across the city will help ease traffic going in and out of the hospital.”
