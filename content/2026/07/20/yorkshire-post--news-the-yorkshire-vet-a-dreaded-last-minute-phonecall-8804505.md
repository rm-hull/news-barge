# The Yorkshire Vet: A dreaded last-minute phonecall

## Just before closing time, there was the usual last-minute phone call which makes everyone groan. In times past, this would fill the on-call vet with either excitement (for the first few years of his career) or despair, depending on what the nature of the job.

A heifer calving after a whole day of trying to give birth naturally, or (more often than you’d imagine) a lame bull at the furthest flung farm in the middle of the moors. “And no, it can’t really wait until morning,” was the usual answer. “He’s very lame.”

After a busy day, it was the last thing that anyone wanted when all that you all really needed was your tea! But I thanked my lucky stars neither of these scenes would the case this evening. I became worn out with endless days and nights of emergency and on-call work a few years ago, which luckily coincided with the rise of specific Veterinary Emergency Clinics, offering out-of-hours cover over a larger geographic area. Vets and nurses can specialise in emergency care, but the biggest benefit is a veterinary team on hand which is not exhausted after a full day of work. The standard of care is certainly more energetic nowadays.

That said, we will always wait for the last-minute emergency. What would this evening’s problem be? Would it wait until morning, or could the injured dog or cat or rabbit be poorly enough to need to be rushed down? Our practice manager had received a text message from a client seeking help and had phoned the practice to ask for the last vet in the building to return the call about a Labrador called Buster. That vet was me, so I called the number. It took me a while to work out the exact nature of the problem and how serious Buster’s situation was. In real life, when the patient is in the practice, honed observational skills give us the first clues; can the dog walk, for example is a good starting point. And after that, we ask a series of focused questions to help make a diagnosis. But on the telephone, it’s easy to get distracted by a conversation that goes off at tangents. This evening, I listened to reports that Buster had refused his usual ice-cream treat, how he could still get on the sofa, but did need some assistance in the shape of a small stool which had recently been placed next to it. Finally, just as I felt I was getting a good feel for the case and the dog’s demeanour, one final comment sent me close to laughter. “And his legs are a bit too close together,” his owner reported, which wasn’t a clinical presentation that I’d come across before, but surely it couldn’t be too serious?

As the clock ticked by and the convoluted story continued, I had one more question to conclude my telephone triage, “Would you like me to see Buster this evening. I’m still at the practice if you’d like to bring him down?”

“Oh no, love. He’s fine really, but I just thought it was a bit odd about his legs being close together.” I felt confident that Buster was not in dire straits and we agreed that a check over in the morning would be in order. I booked an appointment, said goodbye to Buster’s owner, locked up and set the alarm. As I turned the key and headed to my car, I pondered how many times I’d done this over the years. Closing the door on a busy day, felt like it had been a frequent occurrence. At least this evening, I’d be having tea at a sensible time. Maybe Buster would be too.
