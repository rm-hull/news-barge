Andy Burnham is set to become prime minister as he prepares to enter Downing Street.

He is widely expected to deliver a speech outside 10 Downing Street in which he will pledge to bring "breathing room" to struggling households and more stable and responsible politics.

Allies say Mr Burnham will stress the need for “tangible” political stability and improvements in people’s daily lives while urging the nation to face up to the difficulties ahead with “reflection and resolution.”

Ahead of Mr Burnham's appointment, outgoing Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer is expected to make his own remarks outside No 10 before travelling to Buckingham Palace to officially resign.

Mr Burnham will then be invited by the King to form a government.

Both speeches are likely to proceed undisturbed after police banned the use of amplified music outside Downing Street during the handover.

Following his address, Mr Burnham is expected to begin naming his first Cabinet.

While he has kept details of his selections under wraps, senior figures including Ed Miliband and Shabana Mahmood are believed to be in consideration for the role of Chancellor.

Mr Burnham’s rise to the country’s top job marks a rapid turnaround, coming just weeks after his return to Westminster via the Makerfield by-election.

Unlike typical leadership transitions, he takes office without a full contest and has yet to set out a detailed plan for his premiership.

Mr Burnham emphasised a commitment to devolving power from Westminster and pledged to establish a “No 10 North” base in Manchester.

He has also promised a wider reset of government priorities and has already signalled that he will scrap Sir Keir’s digital ID scheme to focus resources on addressing the cost of living crisis.

In an interview with The Times, Mr Burnham said he had already spoken with Treasury officials about his early plans and underlined the need to help people “feel better.”

He said: "The breathing space point is a really serious one."

He revealed that complaints about frozen tax thresholds made during the Makerfield campaign had “lodged in his mind.”

Reports suggest that his first week in office could see new policies rolled out in that direction, potentially including action on energy bills.

Environmental campaigners have urged him to stick to Labour’s 2024 manifesto, which promised not to issue new licences for North Sea drilling, arguing they would not reduce energy bills.

Mr Burnham could also face a decision on whether to temporarily nationalise Thames Water, which is currently burdened by £20 billion of debt.

He has previously expressed support for greater public control of essential services.

Thames Water’s main creditors have said they are willing to discuss the issue with ministers to avoid the company entering the special administration regime.

Social care reform is another likely priority for Mr Burnham.

Liberal Democrat leader Sir Ed Davey has called on Mr Burnham to work with other parties to tackle the social care crisis, calling for protection against “catastrophic costs” and a universal safety net for family carers.

Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch has written to the incoming prime minister urging him to back new oil and gas licences, rule out tax rises, and work together to cut the welfare bill.

Mrs Badenoch said: "You will enter office without having set out a clear plan on any of the issues facing our country.

"You have refused calls to come to Parliament for questions from MPs, and you have not submitted yourself to serious media scrutiny.

"This is not a promising start."

Despite the criticisms and calls for collaboration, Mr Burnham enters office facing a complex set of urgent challenges—economic, political, and social—all against a backdrop of public demand for change.
