Meta Platforms is set to face trial in Tennessee on Monday, confronting allegations that Instagram's design intentionally fuels a youth mental health crisis.

This marks one of several upcoming legal battles testing claims that the company's social media platforms were deliberately engineered to be addictive.

Tennessee's lawsuit accuses Meta of violating the state's consumer protection law, asserting the company knowingly designed a product that drives teenagers to compulsive use while misleading the public about its safety.

Filed by Attorney General Jonathan Skrmetti's office, the suit claims Meta failed to disclose extensive internal research indicating Instagram could harm teens.

It further alleges the company continued offering features it knew were dangerous without warning users.

The state contends that founder and CEO Mark Zuckerberg was repeatedly alerted by Meta employees about research showing negative impacts on teens but declined to fund efforts to mitigate these harms, instead making misleading public statements about the amount of harmful content on the platforms.

Skrmetti is seeking financial penalties and a court order compelling Instagram to modify aspects of the platform deemed detrimental to teens' mental health, specifically citing features like autoplay, Instagram's Reels videos, notifications, and designs that cause content to disappear.

In response, a Meta spokesperson stated on Friday that the company has already implemented built-in controls to safeguard the hundreds of thousands of Tennessee teens who use social media daily.

"We want them to do that in a protected space, which is why we’ve spent a decade building safe, age-appropriate defaults for teens alongside simple tools for parents to set the right boundaries for their family," the spokesperson said.

The company has consistently argued that the state's claims of harm are based on user-posted content, and that Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, a federal law, shields it from liability for third-party content.

Jury selection for the first phase of the trial will commence in Nashville on Monday. This initial phase will determine if Meta violated Tennessee law.

Should a violation be found, the case will proceed to a second phase where a judge will consider monetary penalties and potential mandated changes to Instagram, with Tennessee’s consumer protection law allowing fines of up to $1,000 per violation.

The trial, expected to last seven weeks, is scheduled to coincide with at least two other trials against Meta in California, as the company navigates thousands of similar lawsuits in both state and federal courts.

The scope of these legal challenges is vast, with nearly every state in the country having filed claims against Meta regarding its platforms’ alleged impact on children.

A significant federal trial involving claims from 29 states, alleging violations of federal law protecting children's data and additional state law claims from California, Colorado, Kentucky, and New Jersey, is slated to begin on August 18 in California.

Separately, Meta and other social media companies face thousands of lawsuits from individuals and school districts. A trial against Meta and Snapchat parent Snap Inc, stemming from a 15-year-old Florida boy known as R.K.C. who alleges social media damaged his mental health, is set for July 27.

The companies have broadly denied these allegations, maintaining they have sought to protect children and should not be held liable for claims they argue are based on user-generated content.

This Tennessee proceeding marks the second state trial to challenge Meta on these grounds.

New Mexico’s lawsuit earlier this year resulted in a jury finding that Meta had misled consumers about the safety of its Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp platforms, awarding the state $375 million in damages. A judge is currently weighing whether to order the company to implement changes and pay additional damages in a separate bench trial concerning New Mexico’s public nuisance claim.
