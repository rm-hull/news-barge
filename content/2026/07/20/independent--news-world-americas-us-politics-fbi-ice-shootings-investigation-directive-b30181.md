U.S. federal agents have been told in recent days the FBI would no longer investigate shootings involving immigration agents, according to a *New York Times* report.

Such investigations – though intended to determine whether Department of Homeland Security agents were attacked – sometimes yield evidence implicating such agents in violent encounters, people briefed on the decision told the *Times*. 

The change follows two fatal shootings by ICE agents in Texas and Maine in the last two weeks, sparking renewed pressure on the Trump administration and public scrutiny of the agency’s practices.

On Thursday, FBI managers around the U.S. received a written notice informing them the bureau would no longer investigate claims of assaults made against Department of Homeland Security agents, according to the people reportedly familiar with the matter who spoke on the condition of anonymity.

Some ICE agents around the country reprtedly have been informed of the change by their FBI counterparts, which would mean investigations would be done internally.

A joint statement by the Justice Department and the Department of Homeland Security, however, denied such a change had been implemented.

“The relationship between D.H.S. and D.O.J. in investigating assault on federal officer cases has not changed, and F.B.I. will continue to investigate in accordance with agency policy,” the statement said.

“This administration has been clear, anyone who assaults law enforcement will be prosecuted to the fullest extent of the law,” it added.

According to guidance circulated among FBI and ICE agents, the change would likely leave Homeland Security Investigations – an arm of ICE – to investigate cases involving same agency’s officers.

Over the past year, amid growing public opposition to the Trump administration’s immigration crackdown, White House and Justice Department officials have urged prosecutors to bring charges against as many cases of assault on federal officers as possible.

Federal law allows for felony charges to brought against anyone who “forcibly assaults, resists, opposes, impedes, intimidates or interferes” with a federal law enforcement officer.

Trump administration officials have argued such legislation means anyone who makes physical contact with an agent should be charged with felony assault, a crime punishable by eight years in prison.

“It’s abhorrent to ever, ever touch a law enforcement officer, which is why anytime anybody does that and it’s a federal officer, we’ll prosecute them,” the acting attorney general, Todd Blanche, told CNN in May.

Deaths in ICE custody have also motivated public concern. Such deaths have soared to 52 during the first 500 days of Trump’s second administration, with the most deadly pre-January 2025 month of the last decade (May 2020) exceeded five times since Trump returned to office, according to a report by Human Rights Watch.
