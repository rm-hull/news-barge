Family and supporters of Nolan Xavier Wells gathered in a Mississippi church on Monday to mourn and celebrate the 18-year-old, whose death following a 4 July boat trip with friends has captured national attention and prompted calls for an independent investigation.

The ceremony began with tearful remarks from Wells’s parents, who described their son as a warm and gentle presence, a gifted athlete and a role model to his younger siblings.

“He had a way of making people feel seen, welcomed and valued,” said Christine Wonsley, the teenager’s mother. “He could lift your confidence with just a few words and make you feel like you belong.”

![people lining up in front of a casket](https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/89626da6c3bd1c9a181c4df0475c870fbc590e47/0_0_5310_3540/master/5310.jpg?width=445&dpr=1&s=none&crop=none)

In a fiery eulogy, the Rev Al Sharpton, a well-known US civil rights leader, then promised a $50,000 reward for any new information about the circumstances around Wells’s death. The teenager, who was Black, traveled with a group of white friends to Horn Island, off the US Gulf coast, but did not return with them.

“There’s too many unanswered questions,” Sharpton said. “We can’t have someone in law enforcement saying there was no foul play before there was an investigation.”

He then invoked the history of racist violence in Mississippi, including the lynching of Emmett Till and assassination of Medgar Evers.

“The reason why a lot of people in the nation are looking at this Nolan situation is that we’ve looked at history in Mississippi,” Sharpton continued, to loud applause in the church. “Some of us remember what happened in Mississippi.

“Before we can medicate and heal today, we’ve got to look at your history.”

The family’s attorney, Ben Crump, then led the crowd in a chant of “Nolan’s life mattered,” as Wells’s mother sobbed into the shoulder of her husband, Elmore Wonsley.

Wells’s death has captured national attention, galvanizing Black leaders and touching off fraught discussions of racial tension and mistrust of law enforcement in the deep south. Among those in attendance on Monday were Spike Lee, the director, and Terrell Owens, the former football player. Tyler Perry, an actor and producer, helped pay for the funeral.

John Ledbetter, a Jackson county sheriff, has said Wells’s friends are cooperating with the investigation, which is continuing. He said initial interviews suggested Wells chose to stay on the island and that his friends believed he would get a ride with someone else. His body was found off the island’s coast two days after he was last seen.

The still-unknown circumstances leading to Wells’s disappearance have fueled rampant online speculation, along with questions from his parents about why he was left behind.

Crump has said another witness reported Wells planned to leave on the boat with his friends, contradicting the sheriff’s statement. He also alleged that messages appeared to have been deleted from the phone, which was found with Wells’s friends, along with his keys.

The attorney, who is leading his own investigation into the death, said he would work alongside the local prosecutor to inspect the device. The prosecutor has agreed to present the results of the investigation to a grand jury once it is completed, Crump said.

“Our lived experiences tell us that we must question everything, everybody’s role, law enforcement’s role,” Crump said. “That is the lived experience as Black people in America.”

Wells, who would have turned 19 in August, played wide receiver on the football team at Southwest Mississippi Community college in Summit, Mississippi, and had aspirations of playing at a high-level, division one program.

“He was very sociable with everyone, didn’t meet a stranger,” his coach, Les George, told WAPT. “He would pop up at my office and come sit on the couch just to hang out and talk.”

In a joint statement, members of the Congressional Black caucus called for an independent and transparent investigation into Wells’s death, adding that “too many questions remain unanswered”.

“We hope the outpouring of concern and support from people across the country in recent days brings them some comfort in the days ahead,” they said.
