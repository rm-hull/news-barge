French human organisations seek court ban on trading with or investing in Palestinian occupied territories

Patrick Wintour

A court order directing the French government to ban companies from trading or investing in the Palestinian occupied territories is being sought today, in a novel action by five human rights organisations that tests the enforceability of international law.

The legal request to France’s highest administrative court argues the French government has failed to implement an advisory opinion issued two years ago by the International Court of Justice (ICJ), that declared the Israeli occupation of the Palestinian territories was unlawful and countries should not do anything to aid the occupation.

Palestinian, French and European Union flags fly outside the town hall in Vaulx-en-Velin, an eastern suburb of Lyon, France. Photograph: Anadolu/Getty Images

The five NGOs taking the action are the International Federation for Human Rights (FIDH), Jurists for the Respect of International Law (JURDI), the International Centre of Justice for Palestinians (ICJP), La Ligue des Droits Humains (LDH), and Law for Palestine.

The French foreign minister Jean-Noël Barrot on Monday called for “the end of imports of products from illegal settlements into the European Union”. The human rights groups argue however that France has a national legal obligation and cannot simply wait for the creation of a consensus inside the EU, where there is an impasse on the issue.

The ICJ, in a landmark ruling in July 2024, found that all states are under an obligation not to recognise the Israeli occupation of the Palestinian territories as lawful nor to aid or assist it.

The UN general assembly in September 2024 also resolved that countries had an obligation to distinguish between the territory of the state of Israel and the Occupied Palestinian Territory, in order to “take steps to prevent trade or investment relations that assist in the maintenance of the illegal situation created by Israel in the OPT“.

My friends left Ukraine after the Russian invasion. Now I wonder if we live in the same world

Diana Berg

A Ukrainian artist, writer and cultural manager from Mariupol, and the founder of Platform TU, an independent cultural initiative now operating in exile after Russia’s full-scale invasion.

Over the years of working with western colleagues, I had learned to accept that they don’t fully understand what war feels like from the inside. They would ask me to find someone in occupied Mariupol for an interview, or seem genuinely puzzled that I couldn’t just log on at a time that suited them. I made my peace with this long ago – they don’t have to understand. But this time, it was a Ukrainian who asked.

People take shelter inside an underground parking during Russian missile and drone strikes, amid Russia's attack on Ukraine, in Kyiv, Ukraine. Photograph: Valentyn Ogirenko/Reuters

It was winter, the kind where every online meeting had to be carefully scheduled around power outage timetables – and sometimes around shelling timetables, as we had already begun to joke among ourselves in that dark way. The question about rescheduling the meeting came from a colleague who had left for Europe in 2022, and it hit me almost physically, precisely because she was the one to say it. That innocent query had no business feeling like an accusation – and yet it did. For a long time, I couldn’t understand why.

We knew that war would bring pain and rupture. But there are ruptures no one warned us about – ruptures that are quiet and slow, and for that reason the most devastating. One of them runs not between Ukraine and the world, but through Ukrainians ourselves – between those who left and those who stayed. Almost no one talks about it. But it’s there, and with every passing month it grows wider.

Zelenskyy fires Ukraine’s army chief and offers Fedorov ‘prominent position’

Luke Harding

in Dnipro

Some much-awaited changes in the way the Ukrainian army is organised will kick in today, after president Volodymyr Zelenskyy fired Ukraine’s commander-in-chief Oleksandr Syrskyi and offered a “prominent position” to the defence minister he sacked last week in a move that sparked nationwide protests.

Maj Gen Mykhailo Drapatyi attends a meeting with Ukraine's president Volodymyr Zelenskyy, amid Russia's attack on Ukraine, in Kyiv, Ukraine. Photograph: Ukrainian Presidential Press Ser/Reuters

In a late-night TV address, Zelenskyy said he was appointing Mykhailo Drapatyi, a popular and innovative general, as the new head of Ukraine’s armed forces. Drapatyi would oversee a reorganisation of the army’s command structure, Ukraine’s president said.

Zelenskyy said he was bringing Mykhailo Fedorov back into government after his removal as defence minister prompted thousands of people to demonstrate in Kyiv and other Ukrainian cities, in one of the biggest political crises since Russia’s 2022 invasion.

Why Ukrainians are protesting against Zelenskyy's dismissal of defence minister – video

Ukraine’s president paid tribute to Syrskyi and credited him with leading the defence of Kyiv and launching a counter-invasion of Russia’s Kursk province in summer 2024, in which foreign tanks rolled on to Russian territory for the first time since the second world war.

“We have come a long way. The defence of Ukraine continues. And every soldier deserves to be treated with respect,” Zelenskyy said, adding that he was grateful to Syrskyi and to “every one of our soldiers” fighting on the frontline.

Zelenskyy will be hoping that his radical reshuffle of the army’s leadership will assuage protesters who have taken to the streets demanding Syrskyi’s removal. They had given a deadline of Friday for Fedorov to be reinstated and promised “indefinite protests” if their request was ignored.

'We are bringing the war back home, to Russia,' Zelenskyy says after overnight strikes

“We are, quite justly, bringing the war back home – to Russia. Peace is needed, and Ukraine has presented the Russian side with every proposal. They must be forced to turn to diplomacy.”

Morning summary: But can they do it on a Wednesday morning in Brussels?

Jakub Krupa

EU ambassadors are meeting in Brussels today in a bid to sign off on the 21st package of sanctions against Russia after previous attempts ended with no success.

The Ukrainian and the EU flags are seen side by side in the Berlaymont, the EU Commission headquarter in Brussels, Belgium. Photograph: Thierry Monasse/Getty Images

Lithuania’s ambassador to the EU, Nerijus Aleksiejūnas, told the Lithuanian media that he was sensing a growing fatigue with the sanctions amid concerns that some measures could actually pose difficult questions for some of the member states.

“The argument heard most often is that sanctions should not harm European countries and their economies compared with Russia,” he said, as reported by LRT.

As a result, the EU dropped proposed restrictions on Russia’s fishing sectors, a proposed ban on Russian soldiers entering Europe (as France, Italy, and Spain issued the highest number of visas to Russian citizens last year, LRT said), and on sanctions against Russian Orthodox patriarch Kirill. Greece continues to oppose proposed restrictions on Russian LNG supplies.
