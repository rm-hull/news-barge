# Banned taxi driver scraps appeal after losing licence due to driving offences

## A banned taxi driver hoping to get his licence back asked a court to give him 12 weeks to speak to a lawyer.

But Mohammed Rafiq ultimately scrapped his appeal after magistrates told him they would not be willing to allow the case to be adjourned for three months just because he couldn’t currently afford legal representation.

Rafiq, of Seldon Street, Bradford, was stripped of his taxi licence after being convicted of two driving related offences earlier this year.

In March he received a conviction for driving without due care and attention.

![Banned taxi driver scraps appeal after losing licence due to driving offences](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOmNiNDMxYjc0LWUyM2QtNGI0My05NTIxLTRmNWI0NWEzZGU5NDpiY2QzNTE4OS02ZDE1LTQzMzQtOTQxMi0yMzJmY2FkNzlmMWU=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



And last month he received a further conviction for driving while using a mobile phone.

The two offences were enough to convince Bradford Council officers that Rafiq was not a fit and proper person to operate a taxi, and he was stripped of his licence.

He has since decided to appeal this decision, and on Friday the appeal was due to be heard at Bradford Magistrates Court.

The appeal was brought by Rafiq, with Bradford Council essentially acting as the defendant.

But on the day, it emerged the former taxi driver had arrived without a lawyer or legal representative.

He requested the case be adjourned to give him time to consult with a lawyer.

When asked how much time he would need to do so, he suggested 12 weeks to a shocked courtroom.

Imran Hussain, representing Bradford Council, told magistrates that while the appeal was ongoing, Rafiq would still retain his licence. He added: “Because of this we would certainly not want to delay matters.”

Magistrates asked why Rafiq was unrepresented, and why he needed 12 weeks to speak to a lawyer.

Speaking through an Urdu interpreter, he said he didn’t have the money to get legal advice.

He was advised that if he continued with the appeal and went on to lose it, he would face a hefty bill for costs that would need to be paid to Bradford Council.

He replied that he didn’t know how he would pay such a bill.

After being told a bill for costs would be inevitable should he lose the appeal, he was asked if he wanted to proceed with the case.

He replied: “It is up to the bench (of Magistrates) to decide.”

Rafiq was reminded that it was his appeal – it was up to him, not magistrates, to decide whether he proceeds.

He then told the court he wished to withdraw the appeal.
