Andy Burnham has withdrawn suggestions he would increase the personal tax-free personal allowance but also said tax rises to pay for his measures to address the cost of living would not be likely at this year’s budget.

The prime minister had told *The Times* that “the thing I heard the most on the doorsteps” during his by-election Makerfield was the tax-free personal allowance threshold, which has been frozen at £12,570 for five years.

However, he has now said that there is “no commitment at this point” to change the allowance.

Also asked on Tuesday if tax rises were likely at the budget this year to pay for his cost of living policy blitz, the prime minister replied: “No, we’ll take decisions on tax at the budget. This is about reprioritising in this phase.”

It came after Mr Burnham announced plans to cap most bus fares in England at £2 from January.

## Burnham asks cabinet to 'prioritise cost of living measures above all else'

Chancellor John Healey is “right behind” Andy Burnham’s drive to ease the cost of living, the prime minister said.

Speaking to reporters in Bath, Mr Burnham said: “I asked the entire Cabinet at our first meeting yesterday to prioritise cost of living measures above all else.

“People are struggling, life is too hard. This Government will make life more affordable.

“Now, I know a £2 cap on bus fares doesn’t solve everything but it shows the direction in which we are going and the chancellor is right behind that with me.”

Mr Burnham added the Defence Investment Plan, unveiled before his premiership, “will have a big impact on jobs”.

He said: “The instruction I’ve given is to maximise that impact for British industry.

“In the past, as a country, we’ve been too willing, I think, to buy from anywhere.

“Well, going forward, I want to ensure that British industry is supported, prioritised.”

## 'No commitment at this point' to change the income tax personal allowance, Burnham says

There is “no commitment at this point” to change the income tax personal allowance, Andy Burnham has said.

Asked whether he was planning to change the allowance during a visit to Bath, the prime minister told broadcasters: “Well, it’s funny these days you can’t answer a question honestly without then people reading everything else into it.

He added: “I want to answer questions honestly in this job, as I said before. I think that’s all about trust in politics and doing politics differently, and I’m determined to keep keep working in that way.

“It was raised a lot, but I then went on to say, ‘But we’ll have to look at it at the budget alongside everything else’. So no commitment, no unfunded promise.

“It’s just perhaps the way politics is these days and the way people report things, but that was an honest answer. There is no commitment at this point to change, but we will look at that at the budget.”

Asked if tax rises were likely at the budget this year to pay for his cost of living policy blitz, Mr Burnham replied: “No, we’ll take decisions on tax at the budget. This is about reprioritising in this phase. As I say, going to the public’s priorities, the cost-of-living crisis, the housing crisis.

“How much longer are we going to talk about these things without actually doing something to help people? And already in the space of a few days, I’ve made a number of moves that will deal with some of these challenges, although I appreciate there’s a lot more to do.”

## Burnham: New cabinet is 'not about factionalism'

Andy Burnham rejected a suggestion he had created bad blood with his clear out of Starmer allies in his new cabinet.

Speaking during a visit to Bath, the prime minister said: “No, I spoke personally to everybody involved. I explained my decisions to them.

“Obviously, many people who were serving in Keir’s cabinet are still in their positions today.

“Heidi Alexander, who’s done a brilliant job on public transport, Pat McFadden, Shabana Mahmood, the home secretary.

“So obviously, there’s a need to bring in change to give the Government that new direction that I’ve called for.

“That’s just the reality of politics.

“It’s not about factionalism and, in fact, the way I’ve dealt with it shows that it is about building a team that reflects all sides of the Labour Party at the highest level, so everybody can see themselves represented within it, and that’s what I’ve constructed.”

## Wes Streeting unveils ‘loyal wingman’ drones in 'groundbreaking moment' for defence

Wes Streeting has hailed new British-made ‘loyal wingmen’ drones as a “groundbreaking moment for UK defence”.

The new defence secretary welcomed the Brontanax, a new unmanned fighter jet created by UK defence company BAE Systems on Wednesday.

Designed and built in Lancashire, the so-called Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) is operated by a pilot from range, allowing crewed and uncrewed aircraft to operate as a team.

Often described as "loyal wingmen," CCAs are designed to fly alongside piloted aircraft and are expected to cost roughly a third as much as a traditional fighter jet.

![UK's first uncrewed autonomous Collaborative Combat Aircraft (CCA) named Brontanax being unveiled](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/22/10/01KY4KFNP53FHTXFF4WQNANY5A.jpg)

Speaking at the Farnborough air show on Wednesday, Mr Streeting said the drone will be in the skies in 2027.

“We will adopt this as our operational concept demonstrator, and we're going to get it in the air in 2027,” he said. “This is a groundbreaking moment for UK defence, not just for air power, but for reindustrialisation.”

## Labour grandee: It’s life or death for Labour now

A Labour grandee has warned it is now “life or death” for Labour as Andy Burnham seeks to unite the parliamentary party behind him.

Lord Glasman, Labour peer and founder of the socially-conservative Blue Labour, said he thought the party was “moving quite rapidly to extinction” under Sir Keir Starmer, but now believes there has been a turn and it has “come back from he dead”.

“The thing is, what we're going to be looking at now is sort of like a proper Labour government, something from the 60s and the 70s,” he told Times Radio. There's going to be a balance of forces, but there's going to be a tendency towards these things. Cost of living, industry."

He added: “The PLP had a near-death experience ... They've seen a prime minister come and go. Now, the reality of the matter is, it is life and death for Labour now. I mean, the Labour Party can fall. And I think that that will lead to quite a significant change.”

## Sarwar appointment is ‘terrible look’ for Burnham, Swinney says

Appointing Anas Sarwar to the Lords so he can join Andy Burnham’s ministerial team is a “terrible look” for the new prime minister, John Swinney has insisted.

The Scottish first minister spoke after it emerged Mr Sarwar is to step down as Scottish Labour leader, allowing him to take up a ministerial role in the Department for Business, Innovation, Science and Trade.

But it comes after Scottish Labour suffered its worst ever Holyrood result in May – with Mr Swinney claiming the appointment was a “very very strange way” for the Makerfield MP to try to rebuild confidence in politics.

That said, the SNP leader said he wished Mr Sarwar, who has been Scottish Labour leader since 2021, well for his “exciting new job”.

But he added: “I think it is a terrible look for Andy Burnham.”

Mr Swinney, speaking on BBC Radio Scotland’s Breakfast said: “The new prime minister has made a great play of essentially improving people’s perceptions of politics and politicians, and to make one of his first decisions a decision to take the Labour leader in Scotland, who has just led the Labour Party to their worst result in the history of the Scottish Parliament elections, and give him a job for life in the House of Lords and a job in the UK Government, is in my view a very very strange way of trying to rebuild confidence of the public in politics.”

## Andy Burnham fails to commit to Healey’s 3 per cent defence spending demand

Andy Burnham has failed to commit to new chancellor John Healey’s demands for defence spending to increase to 3 per cent of GDP by 2030, raising questions about how quickly he will allocate funding to Britain’s military.

Mr Healey quit as defence secretary in June, saying the defence investment plan did not provide enough money to future-proof the armed forces and keep Britain safe and criticised then-prime minister Sir Keir Starmer for not setting out a realistic timetable for the UK’s obligations to Nato to raise defence spending.

Defence spending was due to rise to 2.7 per cent of GDP by 2030 but Mr Healey argued it should instead rise to 3 per cent by then. Asked about Mr Healey’s previous demands, the prime minister’s official spokesperson stopped short of committing to them.

## In full: Andy Burnham announces new £2 bus fare cap across England

Andy Burnham has announced that most bus fares in England will be capped at £2 from January, as part of a wider blitz of policies aimed at tackling the cost of living.

Currently, the nationwide cap on single bus fares is set at £3 until the end of March 2027. However, some areas – including London and Greater Manchester, where Mr Burnham set a £2 limit as mayor – have lower top rates for single tickets.

“Good, affordable transport links are an essential. No one should be priced out of those and left behind,” Mr Burnham said.

“But for too long people have said that cheaper transport isn’t an option. I don’t accept that. I’ve done it before and I will do it again now: a £2 cap on bus fares for millions across the country.

“Lower fares will help people get to where they need to – giving them breathing space to help with the cost of living.”
