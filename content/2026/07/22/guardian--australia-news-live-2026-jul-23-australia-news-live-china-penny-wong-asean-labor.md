China hits back at Penny Wong’s comments about South China Sea

Australia is not ruling out stepping up joint patrols of the South China Sea following “destabilising” missile testing in the region, Australian Associated Press reports.

The foreign affairs minister, Penny Wong, was asked last night about the potential of more regular regional sea patrols in tandem with the United States and Japan, following China’s launching of nuclear-capable missiles.

“The maritime zone is critical to stability in the region so, when we have a conversation about the South China Sea, that is something I’m sure we should all keep in mind,” she told reporters in Manila where she is attending the Asean foreign ministers’ meeting.

“We are concerned about some of the aggression we are seeing, we are concerned about some of the destabilising conduct we are seeing, and the countries of the region have a choice to make about how we all respond to that.”

Penny Wong speaks during the Asean conference in Manila, the Philippines on 22 July. Photograph: Ted Aljibe/EPA

Wong met her Chinese counterpart Wang Yi on the sidelines of the talks on Tuesday evening to highlight her concerns.

“You speak about the importance of mutual understanding,’’ she said.

“I agree, and I expect we will discuss our concerns ... including around peace and stability in our region, safe and professional military conduct, and the importance of transparency and reassurance of intent.”

China’s foreign ministry hit back at Wong’s comments, saying Australia was not party to the South China Sea issue and had no right to intervene.

“China urges relevant countries to stop stirring up tension and inciting confrontation,” spokesman Lin Jian told a regular briefing in Beijing.

It came as Wong prepared to warn China in a speech today that Australia would not be bullied by Beijing as it builds up its military strength.

Federal education minister says teachers should be paid as well as ‘they possibly can be’

The education minister, Jason Clare, appeared on ABC News Breakfast this morning as Victorian teachers go on strike today for the second time this year.

Asked what he had to say to parents impacted by the strike, he said “I hope this gets sorted out as quick as it possibly can”.

The teachers do the most important job in the world. I want to see them being paid as good as they possibly can be. We know that when you pay people more, more people want to do the job. In New South Wales, we’ve seen the evidence of that …

I know the Victorian government’s got a big offer on the table here of a 28% pay rise, which would be even bigger than New South Wales. So that’s a good thing and I really hope that the Victorian government and the teachers can get together and sort this out for parents and for our kids as quick as possible.

Asked if Victorian teachers should get a better offer, Clare said he wouldn’t get involved in individual negotiations but there was a teacher shortage and they should be paid “what they deserve”.

I think that the government is determined to get a good outcome here for our teachers and I’m pretty confident that this will be worked out.

Pole vault star Nina Kennedy and decorated para-swimmer Tim Hodge will lead the Australian team at the Commonwealth Games opening ceremony, Australian Associated Press reports.

Kennedy will carry the flag and Hodge will be the Games baton bearer when the team marches out early tomorrow morning AEST at Glasgow’s Hydro indoor arena.

While Kennedy will be the 24th Australian to carry the flag at a Commonwealth Games opening ceremony, the baton bearer is a new honour.

Rather than a single King’s baton, for the first time all 74 countries and territories were given their own to decorate and bring to the opening ceremony.

King Charles III started the main Scotland baton relay in March last year and it contains the full message he will read at the opening ceremony. The other batons each contain one word from his message.

Kennedy and Hodge won gold four years ago at the Birmingham Games and remain at the peak of their sports.

After winning pole vault gold at the Paris Olympics, Kennedy needed an extended break because of a hamstring injury.

But Kennedy showed only last week that she will be one of the top competitors in Glasgow, breaking the Australian record with her 4.95m vault to win the Monaco Diamond League.

Teacher strike disrupts public schools in Victoria

Students in Victoria will be kept at home as a second teacher strike disrupts public schools, leaving thousands of families to make alternative arrangements, Australian Associated Press reports.

More than 35,000 teachers, principals, and education support staff are expected to gather in Melbourne’s CBD today after rejecting a pay deal proposed by the state government.

While all public schools were set to remain open, many classes will be disrupted.

Australian Education Union Victorian members on Tuesday knocked back the government’s updated offer of a 28.3% pay rise over the next four years, calling for a better offer to combat ongoing resourcing and staffing pressures.

“The government’s done precious little to do anything meaningful to address that and our members are carrying the weight of all the excessive workload,” Victorian branch president Justin Mullaly said.

Union educators have been locked in a pay dispute with the government for nearly a year, with graduate teachers – the worst paid in the country – earning $13,000 less than those in the Northern Territory, the best paid.

Former deputy principal Melinda Davis said the teacher strike was not simply a dispute over pay, it was about addressing conditions and class sizes.

“The government has cut funding to government schools over the past 10 years and that slow decrease in funding has meant we have schools needing repairs, but also we have lost so many teachers in the profession,” the Swinburne education expert said.

More on that response from China to Penny Wong’s comments at Asean:

They came in a press conference held by foreign ministry spokesperson Lin Jian. According to the official translated transcript, he was asked about Wong’s comments that China’s missile test in the Pacific was destabilising, and her concern about “some of the aggression” it is seeing in the South China Sea, in particular a recent confrontation between China and the Philippines.

He responded:

The root cause of the tension at sea lies with the deliberate infringement activities and provocation of the Philippines. Relevant countries are not parties in the South China Sea, and are in no position to interfere in the maritime issues between countries concerned.

Apparently, whenever something happens at sea, the same few countries immediately point their fingers at China regardless of who exactly is at fault. Are they trying to keep the South China Sea peaceful and stable, or simply to destabilise? I think the answer is quite clear. We urge relevant countries to stop hyping up tensions and sowing confrontation.

He said China will “continue firmly safeguarding its sovereignty, rights and interests” in the South China Sea, and on the test launch said it was a “routine training activity” not directed at any specific country or target, and China had notified relevant countries including Australia beforehand.
