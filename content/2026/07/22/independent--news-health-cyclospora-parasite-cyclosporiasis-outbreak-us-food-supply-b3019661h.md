The *cyclospora**cayetanensis* parasite has caused a record 12,000 U.S. cases of the stomach bug cyclosporiasis this year, resulting in nasty symptoms including “explosive diarrhea,” vomiting and body aches.

People fall ill by eating produce or drinking water that is contaminated with the parasite, which lives in human waste.

That means an unsavory chain of events is likely behind the current outbreaks, although the source has not yet been identified by public health officials.

The parasite is often spread through traces of human waste that end up in water used on crops, Dr. David Relman, a professor of medicine at Stanford Medicine, explained earlier this month.

That’s the source of most major outbreaks - and potentially this one, other experts told *The New York Times* this week. 

![Water irrigating crops can be a source of cyclospora contamination that leads to big outbreaks](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/22/18/36/GettyImages-1248577681.jpeg)

“You’d have to have a lot of produce being irrigated with a lot of water that was contaminated by a lot of *cyclospora* to see this number of cases,” Keith Schneider, a professor of food safety at the University of Florida, told the paper.

But that’s not the only way *cyclospora* can spread. When an individual handles contaminated crops - whether during growing, harvesting or processing - the parasite can attach to other crops that haven’t been sprayed with contaminated water. 

And if a person infected with cyclosporiasis goes to the bathroom and doesn’t properly wash their hands, that’s another way for the parasite to travel.

“Food can become contaminated when an infected person with poor hand hygiene handles it,” UC Davis Health says.

Humans are the sole reason *cyclospora* spreads, according to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. People can only be infected if they swallow the parasite.

![This graphic image from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention shows how humans are able to spread cyclospora. The infected food enters the intestines, the parasite replicates inside the body and then it is excreted so it can become infectious again](https://static.independent.co.uk/2026/07/22/17/05/Cyclosporiasis_LifeCycle_19.jpeg)

When *cyclospora* is swallowed, the parasite infects the small intestine, multiplying in the body’s cells for weeks before it is expelled in feces. After it leaves the body, the parasite needs a couple of weeks to become infectious again in the environment, the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment says. 

“This time period outside of the body allows the parasite to become infective, which is where it contaminates food or water,” the department wrote.

That’s how the cycle continues.

Unfortunately, it doesn’t take much to become infected and everyone is at risk. Some foods have been more susceptible to *cyclospora* than others, too. Those include cilantro, raspberries, snow peas, basil and parsley.

“Because leafy greens and berries are usually consumed raw, the parasite can remain viable, even after washing,” Ynes Ortega, a food safety expert at the University of Georgia, pointed out.

Contaminated lettuce has been linked to cases in Great Lakes states and the Midwest, according to the FDA, but the investigation into cases remains ongoing.

While washing produce can help to reduce risk, it doesn’t eliminate the parasite. The only way to kill it is to cook food at 158 degrees Fahrenheit.

That’s part of the problem, too.

“Our fresh produce system is really a web, a network, going out to grocery stores, restaurants and schools. The produce industry and other stakeholders do the best they can to prevent these outbreaks,” Gerardo "Jerry" Lopez, an associate professor at the University of Arizona, said. “But once the parasite is on produce, it's hard to get off.”
