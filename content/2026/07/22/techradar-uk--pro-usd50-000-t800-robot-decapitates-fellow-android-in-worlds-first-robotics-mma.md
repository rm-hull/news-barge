- **EngineAI's humanoid robot can perform uppercuts, spinning kicks, and rapid recovery after falls**

A white T800 humanoid robot named White Eagle stunned spectators in China after landing a precision kick that dislodged its rival's head.

The bout was part of the opening contest of the Ultimate Robot Knock-out Legend (URKL) tournament, the world's first full-scale humanoid robot fighting event.

Held at the Shenzhen Nanshan Cultural and Sports Center, the event drew 32 teams from around the world.

## A robot that kept fighting

The most striking moment came when White Eagle's opponent, a black-clad robot called Matador, lost its head to a precise high kick.

Despite losing its head-mounted sensors, the machine kept throwing punches and absorbing strikes, finishing the match through its torso-based core systems alone.

Organizers said the sequence shows the robot's durability and its resistance to sustained physical impact, according to the Guangzhou Daily.

According to EngineAI, the T800 stands 1.73 meters tall and can perform uppercuts, spinning kicks and rapid recoveries after falls.

The company said each unit costs roughly $50,000 and features strong posture control, dynamic perception and shock-absorbing capabilities for combat.

All 32 competing teams used the EngineAI T800 as a standardized platform, ensuring every fighter entered the ring with identical hardware.

Selection for the inaugural tournament reportedly involved rigorous testing of each robot's stability and combat responsiveness before the event officially began.

The Shenzhen Nanshan Cultural and Sports Center provided the backdrop for a tournament organizers hope will grow into a recognized global sporting franchise.

## Tournament tests more than robot strength

The URKL format assesses robots through effective strikes, body stability, defensive and evasive abilities, and overall durability in combat.

That structure means victory depends on more than simply delivering the hardest punch or kick.

EngineAI founder and CEO Zhao Tongyang said the tournament aims to develop a globally influential commercial humanoid robotics fighting franchise.

He also said competition could support research and industrial development by exposing machines to demanding physical conditions.

"We want to use competition to drive research and industry development," Zhao said. "Let the event feed back into technology, and let technology drive the industry."

Real combat could help assess mechanical balance, millisecond-level decision-making and coordination between multiple sensor systems.

Feedback from such conditions could accelerate the movement of intelligent robots from laboratory development toward commercial use.

Actor Donnie Yen attended the event and compared the experience favourably with staged combat scenes from films.

"Honestly, I used to see robot fighting only in science fiction movies. But today, for the first time, I got to watch real robots fighting up close,” Yen said during the opening ceremony.

The event's unusual head-loss incident reveals the durability of the T800, while also showing the limits and risks of using complex humanoid machines in physical combat.

Future contests will likely determine whether such events can produce useful engineering data beyond entertainment.
