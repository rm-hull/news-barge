# Burnham’s cost of living support will barely register a ripple and is already being undermined by Starmer loyalists

## The new Prime Minister promised to make tackling the cost of living crisis his immediate priority and yesterday Andy Burnham made his first policy announcement to that effect. An £850m tax cut on electricity bills could save households around £45 per year as part of the PM’s promise to help ease cost-of-living pressures.

While any measures to cut bills are to be welcomed as a step in the right direction, this will barely make a dent when it comes to supporting struggling households. In effect, the Government will put around 86p a week into the pockets of families. Barely enough to pay for a bar of chocolate, if the majority of households haven’t already done away with such indulgences.

One of the other key pledges of Burnham’s premiership is to end factional infighting. That has got off to a rocky start. Before Sir Keir Starmer’s premiership had even gone cold, his loyalists had the knives out.

Darren Jones, who lost his Cabinet post as chief secretary to the prime minister, took aim at the policy to cut taxes on electricity bills, questioning how they would be funded.

The Government said the move will be funded in part by scrapping Starmer’s digital ID project, which had been estimated to cost around £600m a year over three years. But that falls short of the estimated £850m hit to tax revenues from the VAT cut.

Jones said “the DigitalID program was unfunded”. That reflects poorly on Labour. One unfunded policy programme that the likes of Jones were behind not long ago being scrapped to fund another policy programme that might itself end up being unfunded.

If Burnham thought Starmer loyalists would give him some respite, then he’s clearly got his answer.

As to cost of living support, people need more than a Mars bar a week in their pocket.
