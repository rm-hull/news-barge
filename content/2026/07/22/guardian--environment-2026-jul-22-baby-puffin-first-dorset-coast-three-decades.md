For years, volunteers have craned their necks and tested their patience to peer into the crevices of the cliffs hoping for a glimpse of fluffy joy.

Finally it all paid off as a puffling – baby puffin – poked its way into the sunshine, becoming the first to be spotted on the coast of Dorset in southern England for three decades.

“Seeing the puffling was a great thrill,” said the marine ornithologist Richard Caldow. “Every chick matters. One puffling alone won’t save this colony but one is a whole lot better news than none.”

This stretch of coast – the Purbeck coast near Swanage – is the last known regular nesting site for puffins on the mainland of southern England.

In the 1950s, there were about 80 birds nesting there but the number of pairs dropped to three and there had been no definitive sightings of fledglings for 30 years. The fear was that the colony was heading to extinction.

![A puffin swimming in the sea](https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/4a08d892ca89362051b6306ddcfecffbfd68b525/79_0_1681_1345/master/1681.jpg?width=445&dpr=1&s=none&crop=none)

Over the summer, 13 volunteers have been monitoring the ledges for 15 weeks, no easy task as the vantage point is hard to access and precarious.

In April, six adult puffins were recorded at the colony. Within a few weeks, volunteers were observing puffins carrying fish into at least two separate nesting locations, indicating that more than one pair may have been attempting to raise chicks.

Most of the deliveries ended after 12 June, suggesting that not all the chicks had made it but the fish drops continued to one spot and eventually the puffling was spotted and photographed.

Caldrow, who is supported by volunteers from the Purbeck Natural History Forum, said they timed the taking of the photo perfectly. “We knew its parents had been feeding it since about 29 May until 5 July so by then it was 38 days old.

“By then it was likely to come into the open occasionally, usually if a parent was there too. That was most likely in evening so we chartered a boat on 7 July to anchor in the right spot from 4 till 8pm. It took three hours but just after 7pm it popped out behind its parent. Great to see.”

![A man uses ropes to install a camera on a cliff ledge above the sea](https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/43cc83538d56f2704ef56468cf178f125d8fb481/0_0_4000_2250/master/4000.jpg?width=445&dpr=1&s=none&crop=none)

He believes the puffling may have left that night. “Where it is now we have no idea. It will be well out at sea somewhere on its own. The parents don’t look after it once it fledges. It won’t come back for at least two years and won’t breed till 2030 at the soonest. It may come back here but it could go somewhere different.”

Caldow, who has nearly 40 years’ experience researching and conserving coastal bird populations, said he was delighted at the sight of the puffling. “It tells us that Purbeck puffins can still lay fertile eggs, hatch them and, at least in some years, find enough food to rear a chick and escape the attention of predators.”

Over the last few years, the National Trust has been working to understand why the puffin colony has been failing to produce chicks. Theories have ranged from rats to peregrine falcons to the climate emergency.

![View from on top of the cliffs looking out towards the sea](https://i.guim.co.uk/img/media/4dd4b06296dede93b93a00466f1590a68f535be9/0_0_5185_3450/master/5185.jpg?width=445&dpr=1&s=none&crop=none)

Motion-sensor cameras, powered by solar panels, have been installed by high-rope experts, positioned to take images of movement within the deep fissure – the puffins nest about 2 metres back from the entrance.

The cameras will be recovered after the seabird breeding season, and volunteers will then review the thousands of images taken.

Ben Cooke, the rivers and coast project officer for the National Trust in Purbeck, said: “The images should hopefully reveal more of the nesting, feeding and fledging activity of the puffins. Understanding what is happening inside the nesting crevice is the missing piece of the puzzle.

“If we can identify the reasons why chicks and eggs are being lost, we can begin to explore what conservation measures might help. Puffins are such an iconic bird, we want to do everything in our power to help them survive in Purbeck.”

- 
  The National Trust is offering live puffin and clifftop webcams on the Farne Islands in north-east England. Streaming daily from dawn until dusk between May and the end of July, the cameras form part of the trust’s Window to the Wild project, giving viewers an up-close view of the seabirds that breed on the islands.
