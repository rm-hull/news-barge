# 'Why I'm running pilates for men in a Yorkshire brewery'

## Two months ago, Tony Leatherbarrow was so crippled with back pain that he struggled to bend down to tie his own shoelaces. Then, as serendipity would have it, a class advertised by his local brewery caught his eye.

![](https://www.yorkshirepost.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOjE4YzJmNjQ0LWEwYzYtNGRiNi1hMzZlLTk3ZmM0YWYyNDk5YjpjODEwNjM0Yi1kMWRjLTRmZTItYTJmOC1lODU1N2IwOGYyOTA=.jpg?trim=0,0,220,0&crop=&width=640&quality=70&enable=upscale)



‘Cask and Core’ was its name, a venture developed by pilates instructor Yvonne Stewart and the Harrogate-based Rooster’s Brewing Company.

The initiative was set up to encourage men to experience the benefits of pilates and challenge the idea it is a female-dominated space.

Since May, former secondary school teacher Yvonne, has been holding the male-targeted classes at the brewery, offering a workout designed to improve core strength, balance and flexibility.

Tony, 70, who had previously taken part in a mixed-sex pilates group for people over 50, says starting up again has been transformative.

"Now I can tie my own boot lace, I can do a lot more,” he says. “Pilates is great for aches and pains. I’m so much more supple. I can bend down and move around without getting pain whatsoever now.”

Yvonne, 57, was inspired to launch the classes after her husband, Jonathan, turned to pilates following a running injury, and often found himself to be the only man in the room.

His experience is far from unusual, she says, with many men reporting that they feel out of place or hesitant to attend sessions that are predominantly female and marketed as such.

“Rather than encourage men to come to my lessons, I thought I’d bring the lessons to where the men are and see what happened,” she says. “I had no idea how many men were bursting to start pilates.”

It is a man – Joseph Pilates – who is credited with inventing and promoting the pilates method of physical fitness.

“Despite being developed by a man and increasingly embraced by elite male athletes, pilates continues to be perceived by many as a female activity – and marketed as such,” says Yvonne, who turned to pilates when chronic back issues interfered with her varied exercise routine.

"This perception can discourage men from trying pilates, even when they could benefit from it.

"As awareness of pilates' performance benefits continues to grow, there is an opportunity to reposition it as an effective form of training for everyone—not just women.”

Gregory Bogle, 37, decided to attend after experiencing aches and pains whilst playing with his five-year-old son.

“I thought pilates might be a good way to manage that and strengthen it before the aches and pains increased - I’m told that happens with age,” he chuckles.

“It’s not just about lying down and breathing - through breathing is important. It’s about core strength and using different muscles that you might not use in other sports or exercises.”

- Cask and Core will run each Tuesday evening at Rooster’s Brewing Company.
