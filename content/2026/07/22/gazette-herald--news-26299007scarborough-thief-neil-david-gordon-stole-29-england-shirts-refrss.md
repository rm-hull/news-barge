A shoplifter who staged a campaign of stealing England football shirts and jackets during the World Cup has been jailed.

Neil David Gordon targeted Footlocker and JD Sports four times in four days from June 22 to June 26, taking hundreds of pounds of England sportswear on each occasion, Thomas Harris, prosecuting, told York Crown Court.

He was stopped on June 26 when he was confronted and arrested as he stole 17 England shirts worth £1,440, in a single raid.

In total, the career thief stole 29 England shirts, t-shirts and jackets during the campaign which started a day after he staged a different campaign of shoplifting in his home town of Scarborough.

Altogether, he stole goods worth £3,727.84 including walking out of Tesco’s in Scarborough on June 20 without paying for alcohol worth £770.

Judge Simon Hickey said Gordon had deliberately left Scarborough after he was arrested in the town and moved to Briggate in Leeds to carry out the “targeted” England sportswear thefts.

He called Gordon a “scourge on our society” and jailed him for two years.

“Shoplifting is a blight on our society,” he said. “A message has to go out.”

Gordon, 55, of no fixed address, has 107 previous convictions for theft and related offences out of a total of 226 previous convictions. He pleaded guilty to four thefts of England sportswear in Leeds, and in Scarborough, three thefts from Tesco, one from Sainsbury’s in Ramshill Road, one from Iceland and one from a newsagent.

In March, York Crown Court heard, he was banned under a criminal behaviour order (CBO) from several named shops in Scarborough including the Sainsbury’s store, in a bid to stop him offending. He pleaded guilty to breaching the CBO by going into the Sainsbury’s store.

Defence barrister Rachel Webster said Gordon was caught in a cycle of homelessness and being sent to jail and felt safer inside prison than outside where he felt vulnerable and targeted.

“He is released from prison without support, without financial support and no accommodation, effectively thrown on the street with nowhere to go, no money,” she said. “He needs help, he needs some support when he is released.”

He also had mental health problems.

The judge said Gordon didn’t co-operate with those who wanted to help him and was his own worst enemy.

Gordon had told a probation officer he had taken heroin for 23 years until he kicked his habit, but now, when he couldn’t cope, used spice and other illegal drugs and alcohol.
