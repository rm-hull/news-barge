Brittany Higgins says Pauline Hanson’s comments on domestic violence were ‘atrocious’

Brittany Higgins has condemned comments Pauline Hanson made on domestic violence this morning, describing them as “atrocious” and “normalising” victim-blaming rhetoric.

Appearing on radio in Brisbane this morning, the One Nation leader was asked about men who abused their partners. She said it was “wrong, but it’s also a two-way street”, adding:

Domestic violence is a scourge but look … I’ve been through it myself in my life. And I just feel if you’re not in a house where you feel safe … then you shouldn’t put up with it. If these women are getting abused by men out there, then don’t put up with it.

Posting to social media, Higgins wrote:

‘Mutual abuse’, ‘two way street’, ‘just leave’ – normalising this victim blaming rhetoric in relation to domestic violence when 38 Australian women have already been lost to femicide is atrocious.

Socceroo set for move to club which spawned Ronaldo

Nestory Irankunda is in the sights of Portuguese giants Sporting Lisbon in a move which has the potential to make the Socceroos attacker the most expensive player in the history of Australian football.

Fresh from making his World Cup debut and scoring in Australia’s only win - their 2-0 group stage victory over Turkey - former Adelaide United star Irankunda is reportedly nearing a permanent transfer to Sporting from English Championship side Watford.

Sporting have qualified for the UEFA Champions League league phase as a result of their second-placed finish, meaning that should he breakthrough into the first team, Irankunda has a chance to test himself against Europe’s best.

Reports in Portugal indicate that in a deal that includes add-ons, Watford could pocket a total of €22 million ($A35 million) for the 20-year-old’s services. That would mean Irankunda surpasses fellow Socceroos teammate Harry Souttar, who moved from Stoke City to Leicester City in 2023 for €17 million ($A27 million) as Australia’s most expensive transfer.

‘An exact flip’: JCCV chief tells royal commission security now consumes 80% of its focus

The Jewish Community Council of Victoria (JCCV) said it has had an “exact flip” of its operations since October 2023, with an increased focus on security threats and rising antisemitism.

Its chief executive, Naomi Levin, told the antisemitism royal commission that before October 2023 roughly 80% of the peak body’s time was dedicated to cultural, educational and community programs, with 20% spent on security and antisemitism:

Post-October 2023, there has been an exact flip. I would say 80% of our time is now spent discussing antisemitism or grappling with how to keep our community safe.

Levin described heavy security infrastructure now required at the JCCV’s headquarters in Melbourne. She said the building’s security, including guards and fencing, is often commented on by visitors because they are “unprecedented for a community organisation” but “entirely necessary”:

When I was growing up here in Melbourne, there was a cafe at the bottom of that building. There were no locks, guards, or fences. People just came in and out like you would expect of a community centre. [Now] they’re greeted with fences and guards … it creates literal barriers to our participation in Jewish life.

Levin added that security measures routinely discourage community members from gathering outside synagogues or events.

Legal and regulated tobacco market is ‘already lost’, National Press Club hears

Today’s National Press Club address is on illicit tobacco and its impact on public health, featuring former border force officer Rowan Pike, former deputy chief medical officer Nick Coatsworth and Liberal MP Mary Aldred.

It comes as the New South Wales premier, Chris Minns, this morning said the tobacco excise was too high and was encouraging illicit operators into Australia’s market.

Pike told the press club that illicit tobacco rates have been “hidden by the health lobby for many years”.

Only in 2023, [with] the gangs now entrenched, did health officials, belatedly and reluctantly, finally admit the problem exists … It was only the criminals themselves who made the issue too obvious to deny … Police did not create this market. Even the criminal gangs did not create the market, government policy did.

The legal and regulated tobacco market is already lost … Today, with illicit products dominating the market, enforcement alone cannot and will not reverse the damage … We need to act now and the most impactful thing we could do in the short term is to lower the excise rate.

He concluded his address by arguing that countries with the lowest smoking rates in the world have achieved it by transitioning smokers to safer nicotine products, citing the United Kingdom and Scandinavia.

Photographer denies stealing camera from Bondi victim

A photographer has denied using the chaotic aftermath of the Bondi Beach mass shooting to steal valuable camera equipment from one of the deceased victims.

Danny James Ridley formally entered pleas of not guilty to seven charges in the Waverley local court on Wednesday, including stealing $3,500 worth of photographic gear from Peter Meagher.

The 35-year-old is accused of using the ensuing chaos to steal the camera equipment of 61-year-old Meagher, a retired police officer who was one of 15 people killed in the attack at the Jewish festival.

Police allege Ridley pawned the equipment – which was later recovered – along with an SD card containing Meagher’s last photographs.

Ridley’s lawyer, Andrew Dikha,told the court some charges might be resolved through further communication with prosecutors.

Queensland police minister says he supports AusAlert technology

Queensland’s police minister say his state supports the federal government’s AusAlert technology.

At a press conference this morning, Dan Purdie reiterated previous comments made today that he wants answers to questions about the rollout of the technology, particularly how it would work in regional parts of the state where landline phones continue to be popular.

But Purdie said it was “potentially lifesaving technology and we do support that” and wouldn’t rule out rolling it out this fire season. He said:

We need to make sure Queenslanders aren’t put at risk and there are concerns around the gaps as we look to transition the emergency alert system to AusAlert.

We’re looking forward to those questions being answered today [at a meeting of state ministers with federal minister Kirsty McBain].

‘We would not have a camp without security’: youth leader says programs for Jewish schoolchildren can’t go ahead without safety protocols

A Sydney Jewish youth leader has told the antisemitism royal commission that heightened fears for safety have changed how young Jewish Australians gather, forcing programs out of the public and requiring children to disable location tracking on camps.

Giving evidence under the pseudonym ACV, the witness described the precautions now required to run events for school-age children. Activities previously hosted in public parks and pools have moved to secured venues. Campers travelling on public transport or stopping at service stations are instructed to hide Hebrew lettering and Jewish symbols.

It is a security risk to have any Hebrew or any identifying symbols while we’re in public … We try to be very transparent with [the kids]. We want them to understand the seriousness of why we tell them, ‘You have to go on to your phones and turn off your location services so that no one can come and find the campsite.’

After the Bondi terror attack, youth leaders required support from professional trauma psychologists to support children terrified of being targeted at camp, ACV said.

The volunteer-run movement, led by 18 to 22-year-olds, faced uncertainty over affording physical security for their June 2026 winter camp, receiving grant funding days before departure.

She said the movement’s programs provide a “safe, inclusive Jewish place” for participants, many of whom attend secular or non-Jewish schools where they face traumatic experiences.

She shared an incident involving a year 12 student who became school captain:

Upon receiving her school captaincy, her photograph was Photoshopped by her peers to have payot and to have ‘Jew, Jew, Jew’ written on it … She was almost desensitised to it … She couldn’t understand that what had happened was antisemitic.

“We would not have had a camp without security,” she said, noting that while physical security remains central to running programs today, it is not a solution to social division.

Physical security is not the answer to all of the issues currently being faced … In order to have true social cohesion in Australia, the education system, the curriculum being taught in schools needs to change.

Diesel over $2.20 as fuel prices rise 40 cents in three weeks

Fuel prices have raced up 40 cents per litre in three weeks, pushing diesel above 220 cents per litre and petrol above 187 cents in some cities.

Oil prices have steadily risen since the US resumed strikes on Iran. Brent crude oil rose from about US$70 on 2 July to US $91.90 today. The Australian government also reduced its fuel excise discount by 16 cents.

That has forced up wholesale fuel prices, with service stations passing the price hike on to customers.

Melbourne is facing diesel prices of 221 cents per litre today, while Canberra is at 220.2 cents, according to Motormouth. Darwin is at 217.7 cents, Hobart is at 217.5, Brisbane is at 216.7 and Sydney is at 214.9.

Adelaide diesel is at 214 cents per litre and Perth at 209.2 cents, both at their highest since late May after rising 40 cents since the start of July.

Unleaded petrol prices have risen to 187.1 cents per litre in Canberra, the highest price in two months. It’s at 184.9 cents in Perth and 181.7 cents in Melbourne, each their highest since late May. Darwin is at 183.2 cents while Sydney is at 176.2 cents per litre.

Experts warn abortion ‘fear mongering’ risks rural care

More Australian women are accessing medical terminations, but a resurgence in the anti-abortion movement threatens rural patients already forced to seek care on the quiet, experts say.

The number of women accessing abortion pills through GPs rose from one in 10,000 to more than two in 1000 between 2014 and 2021, according to research led by Flinders University. The analysis used data from more than 400 general practices across Australia, tracking more than one million women.

There was greater use of medical abortion, a non-surgical option in early pregnancy, in women aged between 25 and 29. Women in the regions or in lower socio-economic areas also accessed medical abortions at higher rates.

But only one-third of practices provided the service at any point in the study window. More than half of patients were new to the practice where they received care, suggesting they had to find a willing provider, according to the study published in an international journal.

Recent anti-abortion rhetoric, supported by One Nation MP Barnaby Joyce at a NSW rally in June and Katter’s Australia Party in Queensland, only put vulnerable women at risk, a rural gynaecologist, who spoke to AAP anonymously due to their work in the public system, said.

The data revealed continued complexities in abortion access, with higher prescribing rates likely disguising barriers.

Auditors warned to step forward as Asic investigates big four

Australia’s company auditors have been warned to report illegal behaviour as they face growing pressure from the corporate regulator after KPMG’s audit leaks scandal.

The Australian Securities and Investments Commission is already surveilling KPMG, Deloitte, EY and PwC. Now every audit professional has been told they will face Asic’s scrutiny, with the regulator today writing to all registered company auditors to outline their obligations. There are about 2,900 Australians holding that title, not all of whom work in the big four.

In the letter, seen by Guardian Australia, Kate O’Rourke, an Asic commissioner, has told auditors they must report suspected breaches of the law by audited bodies.

Asic will be reviewing auditors’ routine statements to ensure they’re being open about any findings of failures, O’Rourke wrote.

KPMG on Monday announced seven partners involved in sharing confidential Optus information had received punishments ranging from warnings to fines of up to $180,000. Two of the partners resigned, a KPMG spokesperson said, adding:

The conduct is unacceptable and inconsistent with our values, policies and obligations to protect client information. We have apologised to the clients affected.

The scandal had already seen KPMG fine three audit partners who resigned after sharing confidential information from Lendlease’s audit.
