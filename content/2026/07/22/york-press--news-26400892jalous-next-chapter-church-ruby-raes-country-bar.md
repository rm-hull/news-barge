## Nashville makeover for former York church as country bar rides into town

The Jalou Bar, Micklegate, York Picture Frank Dwyer.

  *Jalou’s next chapter? From former church to Ruby Rae's country bar - MAXINE GORDON reports on the changes at this landmark site in York*

AS York prepares for a little piece of Nashville to take over an iconic nightlife spot – we look back at the history of this popular venue.

Ruby Rae's, which already operates a popular country bar in Newcastle, has announced plans to open a second venue in the former Jalou nightclub premises on Bridge Street.

The new country and western bar will open in one of the city's busiest nightlife areas - creating a 'Nashville corner of York'.

The new bar will operate alongside other country-themed venues in the area, including Silver Spur on Bridge Street and FortyFive Vinyl Café on Micklegate, strengthening York's growing reputation as a destination for country music lovers.

But while the building prepares for its latest transformation, its history stretches back far beyond cocktails, live music and late-night dancing.

  Jalou, which is in a historic former church that dates back to the 12th Century


Many people know the site as Jalou, famed for its colourful cocktail menu and lively weekends. Others remember it as The Parish. Before that it served as the York Arts Centre and, earlier still, the Institute of Advanced Architectural Studies.

Yet the building's story begins nearly 900 years ago.

The premises were originally the Church of St John the Evangelist, one of York's historic churches. The base of its tower dates from the 12th century, although the building was altered and extended several times over the centuries.

One of its most distinctive features is the small brick and timber tower topped with an unusual pyramid-shaped roof. This dates from the mid-16th century and replaced an earlier tower which had collapsed.

  St John's Church, Micklegate, February 1955 - derelict interior of the church


The church underwent a major restoration in 1850 under architect George Fowler Jones, who rebuilt the east end and added the south porch.

However, by the early 20th century, the church's future was uncertain, and it eventually closed in 1934.

Since then the building has enjoyed a remarkable second life. It stood empty for a time before becoming home to the Institute of Advanced Architectural Studies. During conversion work in the 1950s, workers uncovered the remains of a Norman window hidden behind plaster. At the time, it was described as the only surviving Norman window in any church tower within York's city walls.

  St John's Church, Micklegate in its days as the Institute of Advanced Architectural Studies


Later incarnations included the York Arts Centre, before the building became a well-known city centre bar.

Now, as Ruby Rae's prepares to take over the historic venue, another chapter is about to be added to one of York's most fascinating buildings – a structure that has evolved from medieval church to educational institution, arts venue and cocktail bar, and is now set to become one of the city's newest homes for country music.
