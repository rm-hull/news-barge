# Review: Harrogate's Cuore Chamber Orchestra walking in the footsteps of giants

## Review: Cuore Chamber Orchestra, Royal Hall, Harrogate by Graham Chalmers

If there’s one thing we have learned about Cuore Chamber Orchestra in their first 12 months it’s this – as well as being supremely talented, this impressive collection of young musicians are somehow, despite their various backgrounds, very Yorkshire.

By that I mean there is something deeply honest about this Harrogate-based professional orchestra’s playing, an open hearted style with plenty of skill but little pretence.

Oh, and, when required, the Cuore can certainly give it some welly under the finely judged but vigorous musical direction of their youthful founder and conductor William Dutton in his modern but quietly stylish Nehru style jacket.

Twelve hours earlier, the 40 or so members of the Cuore, whose ambitions are matched only by its size, were looking relaxed inside the Royal Hall as part of an outreach open rehearsal organised in conjunction with Russell Davidson of the Royal Hall Restoration Trust.

Now, in the same space on a hot summer’s night playing the final concert of their inaugural season, there’s a tenacious drive, as well as warmth, to the Cuore’s superb string section, who bring a natural rhythmic momentum to the ebbs and flows of Mendelssohn’s A Midsummer Night’s Dream Overture and his Violin Concerto in E minor, Op. 64.

They have to be good for the latter piece, in particular, for dazzling international soloist Jack Liebeck is joining them on violin to push the bar that bit higher.

The ovation that follows for the incredible playing of Liebeck is well deserved, the satisfaction of a difficult job for the Cuore well done writ large on conductor William Dutton’s face as he puffs a little and wipes his brow while making his way off stage.

If the packed audience - which includes Melanie Eskenazi, Classical Editor of [https://www.musicomh.com/](https://www.musicomh.com/) – is clearly impressed by the first half performance, their reaction at the end of the second half threatens to take the roof off this most magnificent of auditoriums, a glimmering picture of Victorian elegance in red and gold.

Tackling Beethoven’s shape-shifting Symphony No. 7 in A major, Op. 92 is no easy feat at the best of times.

But the Cuore boasts an elegant command of any rhythmic motif thrown at it.

In the end the entire concert is a triumph, a celebration of an ending which promises a fantastic new beginning.

Outside the Royal Hall, the town is full of the hustle bustle of cars and bars, people spilling out noisely into the road on a night out.

Inside, it feels like the belle epoque of this glorious building first opened in 1900 had never ended, as if we were still in the era of Elgar who trod the same boards as William Dutton is treading now.

And that’s the feeling the Cuore Chamber Orchestra is aiming for, that’s the standard it is determined to reach.
