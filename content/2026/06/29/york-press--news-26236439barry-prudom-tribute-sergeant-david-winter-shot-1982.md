## 'Ultimate sacrifice': Tribute to North Yorkshire officer shot dead by fugitive killer

Armed police in Malton. *(Image: Newsquest)*

A North Yorkshire Police officer who was shot dead when he confronted a gunman on the run 'made the ultimate sacrifice' to protect others.

Tributes have been paid to Sergeant David Winter who was fatally shot in Old Malton on June 28, 1982, during the search for Barry Prudom who had killed PC David Ian Haigh 11 days earlier.

  Tributes to Sgt David Winter shot dead by fugitive in 1982. 

*(Image: North Yorkshire Police)*

Yesterday, Sunday, June 28, officers remembered their colleague on the anniversary of his tragic death, with The Police Memorial Trust laying a floral tribute at a memorial previously unveiled to Sgt Winter in 2023.

  The memorial to Sgt David Winter. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

The Trust said: "Today we remember Police Sergeant David Winter of the North Yorkshire Police who was murdered in 1982.

"The unarmed officer had responded to a report of a man acting suspiciously in Old Malton. As he was speaking to the suspect the man drew a gun and began shooting at him.

"P.S Winter ran for cover but was pursued by the man who caught up with him. The gunman then callously shot the defenceless policeman three times at close range.

"David was 31 years old and married with a family."

On behalf of North Yorkshire Police Federation, branch chairman Rob Bowles added: “Today we remember Sergeant Winter on this sad day.

“He was a brave and dedicated officer who represented the very best of policing. He made the ultimate sacrifice in trying to protect the public and get justice for his colleague."

The triple killer Prudom's reign of terror began on June 17, 1982, marking the start of what would become the largest armed manhunt in British police history, lasting 17 days.

That day, PC David Haigh had been sent to deliver a summons to a suspected poacher in North Yorkshire’s Washburn Valley.

  PC David Haigh who was killed by Barry Prudom. 

*(Image: North Yorkshire Police)*

When he failed to return, his colleague and friend went looking for him and found the police car, with its doors open and PC Haigh lying beside it, shot in the forehead.

A major hunt began but early leads offered little clarity. A name, date of birth and car registration found on PC Haigh’s clipboard proved false, and the vehicle was later discovered abandoned 25 miles away. With no clear motive and almost no evidence, the investigation stalled.

  Armed police in Malton. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

The gunman continued to evade capture. In Lincolnshire, he broke into the home of George and Sylvia Luckett and shot them both, killing George, before escaping in their car. Two days later, in Dalby Forest, North Yorkshire Police dog-handler Ken Oliver narrowly escaped when the suspect pulled a gun during a routine stop before fleeing and burning out his car.

  Armed police in Malton market place. 

*(Image: Newsquest)*

The breakthrough came in Leeds, where officers linked the false details found on the clipboard to Barry Prudom, known to police for his obsession with weapons and military.

But the violence continued. On the twelfth day of the hunt, Prudom walked into the centre of Old Malton where Sgt Winter and Constable Mick Wood were on patrol when Wood saw his colleague challenging a man before he was shot 200 yards from the police station.

Prudom's final hours came after he took a family hostage near Malton, before slipping away. Police tracked him to a makeshift hideout but as officers closed in, a single shot rang out. Believing they were under fire, they returned it. Prudom was found dead inside and a post-mortem confirmed he had taken his own life.
