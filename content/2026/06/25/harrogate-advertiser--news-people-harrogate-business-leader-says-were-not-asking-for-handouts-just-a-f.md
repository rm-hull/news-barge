# Harrogate business leader says ‘we’re not asking for handouts just a fair chance to thrive’

## The owner of one of Harrogate’s most popular independent hospitality venues is calling for a cut in VAT rates not as a ‘Government hand out’ but as necessity for the town’s wider community and economy.

Simon Midgley, who owns Starling Independent Bar Cafe Kitchen owner and is the Chair of Harrogate Business Improvement District (BID) and renowned for his positive attitude, said he wanted to help raise awareness locally of the pressures facing all hospitality businesses - even the most successful ones.

The leading independent businessman said he was far from alone in his thinking and issued an unprecedented warning over the potential impact on jobs, investment and community life if action was not taken.

Giving his backing to VAT’s The Problem, a national campaign to cut VAT for all hospitality businesses by 10% to “protect the future of Britain’s’ social spaces’”, Mr Midgley said: “Starling is busy, we’re doing well and we have ambitious plans for the future but the reality is that the cost pressures and tax burden facing hospitality businesses are enormous and businesses in Harrogate like our’s are suffering from that despite our success.

"Even the very best businesses are finding it increasingly difficult to make the numbers stack up.”

When he first launched Starling in 2017, Mr Midgley set out to create a relaxed venue with equal emphasis on great craft beer and specialist coffee, alongside simple but quality homemade food.

But the wider impact of this popular independent business extend far beyond food and drink and pleasing customers.

The venue hosts regular community events, four book clubs, a weekly charity quiz and fundraising activities, supports local charities and provides opportunities for placement students from organisations including Henshaws and Harrogate Skills 4 Living.

Simon Midgley also volunteers his time as Chair of Harrogate BID, helping support local businesses and promote investment in the town.

Despite the popularity of Starling, which, incredibly, has grown in the midst of a cost of living crisis since it underwent a transformative £250,000 expansion three years ago, Mr Midgley is warning that rising costs and the UK’s VAT burden were putting at risk businesses that play a vital role in communities.

And, he added, the fallout could hit more than the local economy.

![Growing pressure on Harrogate's hospitality trade  - "Even the very best businesses are finding it increasingly difficult to make the numbers stack up.”](https://www.harrogateadvertiser.co.uk/webimg/b25lY21zOjBkZmU2NTEwLTc1Y2YtNDY2Yy1hOWZkLTA3YjZiNzA3MDViMjo2M2JmODcwOS05YzBhLTQyNjAtYTc0Ny0yNTQ3OTUzYzQ5MWQ=.jpg?crop=3:2,smart&trim=&width=640&quality=65)



"These venues aren’t just places to eat and drink. They’re where people meet friends, celebrate milestones, build relationships and feel part of a community.”

“For many villages, the local pub is the last remaining social hub.

"In towns and cities, cafés, restaurants and bars create spaces where people come together.

"If it becomes increasingly cheaper to buy food and drink from supermarkets and stay at home, we risk losing something incredibly valuable.

"Supermarkets do not pay VAT on the food they sell and have the buying power to sell alcohol at close to cost, it’s simply not a level playing field.

“We want to invest, create jobs and grow but where is the incentive to take that risk when costs continue to rise and margins continue to be squeezed?”

The hospitality sector is also one of the UK’s largest employers and provides important opportunities for younger people entering the workforce.

“Hospitality gives many people their first experience of work,” said Simon Midgley.

"It develops confidence, communication skills and employability.

"If we continue to suppress the sector, there is a real danger of a knock-on effect on jobs, opportunities and local economies.”

“Once these businesses are gone, they’re incredibly difficult to replace.

"That may sound dramatic but that’s genuinely where many operators feel we are heading.”

The VAT’s The Problem campaign is calling for a reduction in VAT for hospitality, leisure and tourism businesses as part of wider efforts to support the long-term sustainability of the sector.
