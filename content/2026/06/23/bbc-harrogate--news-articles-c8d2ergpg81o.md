Record-breaking June heatwave due this week

- Published

**Temperatures are expected to soar across Yorkshire this week, as the region experiences its second heatwave in as many months.**

A Met Office Amber heat warning for parts of Yorkshire is in place, with temperatures expected to peak on Wednesday.

BBC Yorkshire Climate Correspondent Paul Hudson said "a combination of high pressure, very warm air spreading from the near continent, light winds and clear skies could see record breaking temperatures".

He said temperatures could potentially reach "as high as 35C (95F) in some places".

So what impact could the heatwave have and what do you need to know?

Here's a few answers, tips and fixes, to keep you as cool as possible as the mercury rises:

## What does an Amber warning mean?

The Met Office states: "Amber warnings suggest a higher likelihood of significant impacts.

"These could include travel delays, road and rail closures, power outages and even risks to life and property.

"When an Amber warning is issued, it's advisable to consider changing your plans and taking steps to protect yourself, your family and your community."

You can check the forecast temperatures for your area on the BBC Weather website and app.

The Met Office warning is in place until 23:59 BST on Thursday

## How long will extreme heat last?

According to the Met Office very hot conditions are expected to continue until at least Thursday across much of England and Wales.

A spokesperson said: "Temperatures above 30C could persist for several consecutive days, while overnight temperatures remain exceptionally high in some locations".

The Met Office says cooler conditions may begin to edge in from the west late next week, although confidence in the timing and extent of any breakdown remains low.

## What else does Paul Hudson say?

As well as pointing to potential record breaking daytime temperatures Hudson said:

"Night-time temperatures will also be very high, especially in towns and cities, not falling below 20C (68F) on some nights."

Nights when the temperature does not fall below 20C is classifies as a "tropical night" by the Met Office and are extremely rare.

Long-standing June records, which were set in 1976, are expected to be broken.

"Undoubtedly without climate change such temperature levels would not be possible, and scientists predict such heatwaves are likely to become increasingly common in the years to come," Hudson added.

## Tips for coping in hot weather

A spokesperson for the NHS said: "A heatwave can affect anyone, but some people are more vulnerable, including, older people, especially those aged 65 and over babies and young children aged five and under."

NHS advice is:

- Keep out of the heat if you can. 
- If you have to go outside, stay in the shade especially between 11:00 BST and 15:00 BST 
- Wear sunscreen, a hat and light clothes, and avoid exercise or activity that makes you hotter. 
- Have cold food and regular cold drinks. Drink extra fluids, but avoid alcohol, caffeine and hot drinks, and have a cool shower or put cool water on your skin or clothes. 
- Close windows, curtains and blinds during the day and open them at night when the temperature outside has gone down. 
- Electric fans can help if the temperature is below 35 degrees. 

## What are the rules around schools and temperature?

Pepe Di'Iasio, general secretary of the Association of School and College Leaders is is the former head teacher of Wales High School in Kiveton, near Rotherham.

He said schools needed to adapt their timetable to cope with the hot weather.

"Many schools are set up for children to be outside at lunch time in the hottest part of the day, come in and leave at certain times.

"What I'd be calling for is greater flexibility, the idea of agility in the school day at this time of year."

Meanwhile, the Department for Education, states:, external "During hot weather, we don't normally advise schools to close.

"This is because school attendance is the best way for pupils to learn and reach their potential, and hot weather can usually be managed safely.

A spokesperson, said: "School leaders should make sure they take any steps necessary to make sure children are safe and comfortable, and the heat-health alerts can guide this.

"Schools could consider relaxing uniform rules during hot weather to make sure pupils are comfortable."

They added: "Children should wear loose, light-coloured clothing to help keep cool and sun hats with wide brims. When outdoors, encourage children to stay in the shade as much as possible.

"Sunscreen with high sun protection factors should be used to protect skin."

## What about heat in the workplace?

In a statement the country's workplace regulator, the Health and Safety Executive, said workers should, external "talk to their employer if the temperature isn't comfortable" adding "employers need to act now to make sure their workers are protected".

The body has called on employers to plan for and support those working in extreme heat and said doing so would keep workers "healthy and safe" while also maintaining productivity.

It said: "Employers must assess risks to the health and safety of their workers by law, including risks from extreme weather such as heatwaves.

"While there is no legal maximum temperature for workplaces, heat is classed as a hazard and should be treated like other hazards."

John Rowe, deputy director for technical support and engagement for HSE, said: "The risks to workers from extreme heat must be properly assessed.

"Practical steps can include providing adequate ventilation and shade and allowing enough breaks for workers to cool down."
