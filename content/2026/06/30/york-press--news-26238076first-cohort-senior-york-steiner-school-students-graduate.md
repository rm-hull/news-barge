Celebrations have been held to mark the first cohort of senior pupils at a York school graduating.

York Steiner School has waved off its first cohort of upper school students who will now continue their studies at sixth forms.

The school, in Fulford Cross, launched its upper school in 2024 to expand its established early years (ages three to six) and main school (ages six to 14) provisions.

Rather than following a traditional GCSE route, the school follows the Steiner Waldorf curriculum, a 'holistic, age-appropriate' type of education that aims to integrates intellectual, artistic, and practical learning.

The graduation last Friday (June 26) followed the students completing the Level 2 Crossfields Institute Integrated Extended Education Diploma. This Office of Qualifications and Examinations Regulation (Ofqual) accredited qualification is equivalent to five to nine GCSEs and uses continuous, portfolio-based assessment.

Alongside the diploma, students sat formal GCSEs in English language and mathematics – marking the very first formal testing experience for those who have been at the school since early childhood.

Graduates have accepted places at sixth forms in York, Selby, Scarborough and even Germany.

  Tracey Lucas, head teacher at York Steiner School 

*(Image: York Steiner School)*

Tracey Lucas, head teacher at York Steiner School, praised the students who she said had gone into their GCSE exams “so calmly, with some of you even coming out saying that you had quite enjoyed the experience”.

The exam experience, she said, “just confirmed to me how delaying high-stakes testing works”.

In a message for the students, the head teacher said: “I know you are now totally ready to fly towards your big dreams."

"Our Upper School is founded on your belief and courage,” she added. “Anyone walking into our school today and talking to these amazing young people would, I am sure, want this extraordinary education for their children."

## 'The school’s curriculum has taught me so much more than academics,' says student

Students praised the school’s alternative curriculum.

"Moving here from mainstream school, this felt like a much safer environment,” said Laurie Hamilton.

Laurie, who performs with his band Some Lions outside of school, focused his diploma portfolio on his music talents.

He will continue his musical studies at Tang Hall Smart college.

Sebastian Haworth-White, who won a £64,000 scholarship to study at the United World College in Germany, credited York Steiner School’s unique environment.

"The school’s curriculum has taught me so much more than academics,” Sebastian said. “It has also taught me to think globally and creatively, building my levels of self awareness and personal responsibility. This is what helped me during my application to United World College, as I was able to reflect my learning both academically and my personal growth."

Iyla Cuthell, who is moving to Archbishop Holgate's CE School, to study mathematics, English, German and philosophy all at A-Levels, added: "Perhaps the thing that stands out most about this school is the sense of belonging.

“Everyone belongs, right from the three- year-old's in kindergarten to those of us who are leaving today, and that’s the reason why it hits me so hard that we’re going, because it’s been a constant in my life for 12 years now."
