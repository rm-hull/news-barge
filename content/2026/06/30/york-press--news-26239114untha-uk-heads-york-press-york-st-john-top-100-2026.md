## IN PICTURES: All the winners revealed in York Press Top 100 of successful firms

All the 2026 winners of the Top100, pictured at York St John University *(Image: David Harrison)i>*

A Boroughbridge business that that makes industrial shredding technology was the overall winner of this year’s York and North Yorkshire Top 100.

The annual event, run by the York Press and York St John University, recognises the best performing and most impactful business in the region and celebrates their contribution to the economy.

The winners were announced on Thursday afternoon at York St John’s Award-winning Creative centre in front of an audience of 150 people. It included invited business leaders, plus key economic representatives from the region including York’s Civic Party and David Skaith, Mayor of York & North Yorkshire.

First established in 2017 as the York Top 100, the awards have gone from strength to strength, with it expanding in 2024 from just York to include North Yorkshire following the creation of the York and North Yorkshire Combined Authority.

The rankings are formulated by York Business School, which analyses turnover, profit, growth, and staff numbers.

  Marcus Brew of Untha (right) talks to Steve Lowe of LOCALiQ, explaining why his company is doing so well. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

This year’s overall winner was UNTHA UK, which has operated for more than 50 years.

Managing director Marcus Brew told the gathering that almost everything you put in your recycling bin will go through one of its machines during its lifecycle.

Marcus added that last year, his machines will have recovered and recycled about 2million tonnes of waste alone.

  Steve Lowe interviews Jo Carlin, general manager of the Serious Sweet Company, which came top in the top growth category. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

UNTHA UK also sought to be environmentally-friendly by using electric motors, rather than diesel, he continued.

His company was also seeking ‘bigger and better’ premises and it was looking to expand its export markets in Australia and Asia, he added.

The Top Growth Award went to the Serious Sweets Company of Harrogate.

  Rachel Fellows, Collaborative CEO at Bettys & Taylors of Harrogate receives a trophy for winning the social impact award from Prof Karen Bryan, vice-chancellor of York St John University. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

General manager Jo Carlin said the company was primarily a fudge manufacturer, working with private labels for 14 years. Half was exported to Australia, Japan, Canada and the US.

The company also supplied retailers in the UK.

  Wayne Dawson and Michaela Lovell receive the Community Interest Award from Prof Karen Ryan, vice-chancellor of York St John University. 

*(Image: Darren Greenwood)*

Jo added: “If there’s a fudge on a shelf it’s probably ours.”

Top Manufacturer went to Power Plastics of Thirsk.

Chairman Simon Price spoke of how the company began in 1973 in Leeds making covers for swimming pools. Then, in 1991 it began supplying tent linings for the British Army, leading it to make most of the tents used by the British and Swedish armies. Half the company’s output is exported, he added, including to Poland and Spain.

The Tourism Award went to No 1 York by Guest House.

  Scott Harrison, chief operating officer of Permaquip Ltd receives the Top Small Company Award from university vice-chancellor Prof Karen Ryan. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

Front of House Suzanne Newman credited the success on the ethos of the business being its owners, who had a background in plumbing, basing the business on what they wanted to see in a hotel, giving a customer’s perspective, rather than an owner’s perspective.

The Top Creative Award went to the York Civic Trust.

CEO Andrew Morrison says the trust is innovative and keen to get people exposed to culture and history. It works with YorkBID and developers wanting to invest in the city.

  Staff from No 1 York by GuestHouse receives the tourism award from Prof Karen Ryan, vice-chancellor of York St John University. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

He said the trust was an active organisation and with businesses working with it, this showed how “fleet of foot York’s business world is.”

The Top Small Business was Ripon-based Permaquip.

Chief operating officer Scott Harrison told the gathering his company made railway trolleys and had just announced a partnership with a firm that converts rail vehicles.

The company supplies 30-40 countries.

  Simon Price (centre), chairman of Power Plastics Ltd receives the manufacturing award from Prof Karen Ryan of York St John University. 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

The Top Community Interest Company (CIC) was QueerArts UK CIC.

Chief executive Wayne Dawson says the group ‘champions queer arts and culture.’ I

Wayne said the organisation was also looking to open a venue in York, adding Pride is the best thing it has been involved in.

The Top Social Impact Award went to Bettys and Taylors of Harrogate.

Collaborative CEO Rachel Fellows spoke of the well-being initiatives at the company, and “making sure we care for the whole person at work.”

The businesses also supported apprentices ships, T-levels and had a partnership with the charity Two Ridings Community Foundation.

  The Civic Party at the Top 100 launch, which was held at York St John University 

*(Image: David Harrison)*

Finally, the Top Professional Services award went to NM Group (Network Mapping) of Knaresborough, who were unable to attend the event.

Professor Karen Bryan OBE, Vice Chancellor of York St John University, who presented the awards, highlighted the role the university plays in supporting local businesses.

Similarly, Professor Brendan Paddison, Dean of York Business School at York St John University spoke of the partnerships between the university, business and government bodies, that would drive success in the region.

He added the York Business Festival will return on Wednesday 14 and Thursday 15 October.
